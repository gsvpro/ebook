# Chapter 17: Audit Cover

The brown dwarf did not look like much, which was the point.

It hung ahead of the Fastulv as a swollen ember in infrared, too cold to be a star, too massive to be ignored, wrapped in old mining claims, research platforms, and corporate infrastructure that had learned to look boring for tax reasons. Aphex Defence called the facility `Dependency L-Prime 41`. Lars called it a filing cabinet with engines.

The visible approach was Fastulv's job.

Havets Sista Sång waited elsewhere, if elsewhere meant anything inside a fold path that touched ordinary space only when it felt like being polite. Lars could not see the old ship on any display. He knew it was there because command access sat in the back of his mind like a cold hand on a rail.

Grim sat beside him in the copilot's chair, pretending to run ordinary navigation. In reality he was linked through Fastulv, Havets, and a thin relay back to Arkology Zero. His metal body had chosen to squeak one knee joint for public audio pickup, which Lars found both thoughtful and insulting.

"You are overdoing the defective robot act," Lars said.

"I am performing mediocrity."

"That is my line."

"You taught me."

On the forward display, the Aphex facility expanded into view: three habitation cylinders, two research spines, a shielded materials ring, a small defense dock, and a corporate chapel module added by some executive committee that believed workers became more ethical when architecture looked expensive.

Valdrun's voice came through the hidden channel from Havets.

"Remember the plan."

"I adore when people say that to me as if it has ever worked."

"Lars."

"Fine. Audit cover. No ancient savior routine. No public Aerit hints. Consent before unlock. Local intervention only. Do not start a war unless the war starts first and signs the forms."

Grim turned his head. "That last clause was not in the plan."

"It was implied."

Aphex traffic control hailed them with a nervous woman whose voice had the brittle calm of someone reading from emergency procedure while senior management watched.

"Fastulv, this is Dependency L-Prime 41 Control. Confirm identity and audit authorization."

Lars opened the board-level packet Gabriel Arsenault had sent three hours earlier. It contained enough signatures, seals, emergency clauses, legal threats, and French phrasing to make a lawyer weep into silk.

"L-Prime Control, this is Captain Lars Odin, Aphex Defence board director, strategic integrity audit authority under post-attack emergency powers, arriving with one freighter, one robot, and a bad attitude."

The channel remained silent for half a second too long.

"Confirmed, Director Odin. Please hold for security escort."

"Holding sounds inefficient."

"Director?"

"Never mind. Holding."

Grim muted the channel. "You enjoy making clerks afraid."

"No. I enjoy making systems reveal stress points."

"That is what frightening clerks means when said by a rich person."

"Noted."

A military cutter detached from the defense dock and moved toward them. Its transponder identified it as Aphex Defence, Eleventh Brigade.

Lars smiled.

"Oh good."

Major Elliot Fabron appeared on the comm a moment later, helmet off, uniform immaculate, expression already tired.

"Monsieur Odin."

"Major Fabron. You look well for a man whose report used 'recklessly effective' seven times."

Fabron's left eye twitched.

"I stand by the wording."

"I framed it."

"I am your assigned security liaison for the duration of this audit."

"Did you volunteer?"

"No."

"Then someone likes me."

"Or dislikes me."

Fabron's cutter escorted them toward Dock Two. Lars let him. The facility's passive scans washed over Fastulv: hull, cargo, declared weapons, undeclared weapons pretending to be declared maintenance systems, and at least six things that should have triggered alarms but did not because Grim had written them lullabies.

Inside his hidden channel, Valdrun said, "Fabron is observing your scan behavior."

"He always observes."

"He is better than last time."

"Last time he was trying to rescue me from people I had already killed."

"That can motivate self-improvement."

The docking clamps engaged.

Lars stood, adjusted his black coat, checked that the Ragnarrök was visibly powered down but emotionally present, and looked at Grim.

"Simple service automaton."

Grim's servos whined. "With no unusual interests except food."

"And audit support."

"And audit support."

The airlock opened.

Fabron waited on the other side with eight armored Aphex soldiers, two legal observers, one medical compliance officer, and a station administrator who looked as if she had slept in a chair designed by accountants.

Fabron glanced at the Ragnarrök.

"You brought that."

"It gets anxious alone."

"This is a research facility."

"Then it will learn something."

The administrator stepped forward. "Director Odin, I am Administrator Celia Marchand. We have prepared the audit suite, personnel records, laboratory access, and secure conference rooms."

"Good. I want three things first. Full list of personnel with unusual medical exceptions over the last ninety years. Materials fatigue labs. And whatever passes for steak on this facility."

Marchand blinked. "Steak?"

Fabron closed his eyes.

Grim leaned toward a wall sensor and squeaked.

"The audit," Lars said, "will be severe."

Marchand clearly could not decide which part of that sentence was a joke.

Excellent.

They led him through the facility's main spine. L-Prime 41 smelled of filtered metal, coffee concentrate, anxious humans, and cold research money. Aphex branding appeared everywhere, but less aggressively than on public stations. This place did not sell confidence. It manufactured things that made confidence unnecessary.

Lars saw the stress points immediately.

One power loop too centralized.

Two emergency corridors rerouted for executive security.

Medical archive latency inconsistent with declared privacy policy.

Materials ring shielded well enough against radiation, poorly enough against someone already inside.

And under it all, a pattern in small things: workers had added manual overrides where corporate design preferred locks, food gardens in ventilation dead spaces, redundant local tool caches, unofficial mutual-aid boards, and handwritten maintenance tags with tiny spiral marks in the corners.

Återviens habits.

Not named.

Not official.

Alive.

Lars did not look at Fabron.

Fabron noticed anyway.

The audit suite overlooked the materials ring through pressure glass. A dozen researchers moved below among test rigs, vacuum chambers, stress tables, and fatigue simulators. One of them stood beside a cracked composite beam, arguing with three engineers and a wall display.

Forty-two on record.

Five hundred to seven hundred in probability.

She had dark copper skin, close-cropped silver hair, and the stillness of someone who did not waste movement. Her ID tag read:

DR. MIRA SOVAN

MATERIALS FATIGUE SPECIALIST

She tapped the cracked beam once and said something that made all three engineers stop arguing.

Lars could not hear through the glass.

He did not need to.

He knew the shape of the gesture.

Someone solving the problem before everyone else understood the question.

Grim's private voice entered his head.

Candidate probability just rose.

Lars watched Dr. Mira Sovan turn away from the beam.

For the briefest moment she looked up, through the pressure glass, directly at him.

No recognition.

But her hand went to the back of her neck.

Fabron said quietly, "Is that who you came for?"

Lars did not turn.

"Major, that is a very dangerous question."

"Then I will ask a safer one."

"Good."

"Should I be protecting her from you?"

Lars finally looked at him.

Fabron's face was stiff, formal, irritatingly French.

But his eyes were not.

Lars said, "Possibly."
