# Chapter 20: The First Ask

The secure conference room had been built to survive lawsuits, hostage events, vacuum breach, and senior executives explaining budget decisions.

It was not where Valdrun would have put a retrieval operation.

That was why they did not use it as one.

Dr. Mira Sovan occupied the far end of the table with one shoulder strapped, one hand wrapped, and the expression of a woman deciding which weapon in the room would be least annoying to use. Major Fabron stood near the door with two soldiers outside, one inside, and enough suspicion to power emergency lighting. Grim stood beside the wall display like a polished metal crime. Lars leaned against the table because sitting felt too much like a board meeting, and he had survived enough horrors without adding proper posture.

That was the visible scene.

The real one had already divided itself into lanes.

Havets Sista Sång had folded its attention around the med bay where the captured Black Hole infiltrator lay in a containment coffin, alive enough to be a problem. Mira was in the conference room, injured, angry, and very much not a prisoner. Ödesvaka watched Arkology-side consent gates through a link so narrow it could barely be called a link. Grim ran the boundary monitor and sanitized every data layer before it touched the room. Fabron held Aphex local command, med security, legal cover, and the right to say no if Lars became an ancient disaster in a black coat.

Valdrun was only a voice in Lars's hidden channel.

That was deliberate.

An ancient half-machine guardian appearing in person would turn a careful knock into a hammer.

"Quiet Knock," Valdrun said.

Lars heard more in the old man's two words than anyone else in the room could. Not a title. A protocol. A memory of Alpha Fourteen engineers who had known their own mercy might become a prison.

Finding locked Aerits had never been the impossible part.

They still carried the nanobot line. Even buried under cover lives and hypnosis, those machines remained faintly coupled to the larger Aerit network and, beyond it, the older mindfield Lyra had only partly understood. Locked minds did not broadcast names, memories, or addresses. But the nanobots left pressure: background coherence, repair signatures, route drift, impossible survivals, small alignments that only made sense across decades.

The hard part was not finding the sleepers.

The hard part was knocking without making the lock defend itself.

This was not what had happened to Lars.

Lars had reached Station Zero half-broken by emergency repair, old command access, Valdrun's presence, Ödesvaka's authority, and medical hardware built for the lock itself. His dehypnotization had been surgery inside the original hospital, performed because the lock and his body were already fighting hard enough to kill him.

Mira had none of that here.

No Station Zero chamber. No guardian in the room. No helmet. No safe surgical map of her lock. No right to open anything just because danger had arrived.

Quiet Knock was not dehypnotization.

It was how not to do dehypnotization by accident.

"Layer one," Grim said in the public channel.

The wall display lit, but it did not show Mira's old face, sealed files, impossible ages, or any word that could hit the lock like a blade. It showed a narrow operational panel.

NETWORK SIGNAL: CANDIDATE LOCATED

QUIET KNOCK: CURRENT LIFE ONLY

CONSENT GATE: PERSON CONTROLS NEXT LAYER

Mira read it once.

"Person," she said, as if testing whether they meant it.

"Person," Lars said. "Grim was raised in tanks and became rude through paperwork."

"Correction accepted under protest," Grim said.

Mira did not smile. Her attention stayed on the display the way a good engineer watched a load test: not trusting the frame, not trusting the numbers, not trusting the men standing too close to it.

"Start," she said.

Lars wanted to answer.

Valdrun spoke first, privately. "No history. No identity. Ask for present work."

That hurt more than it should have. Lars wanted, stupidly, to tell her enough to make the shape of danger fair. He wanted to say Aerit, old war, locks, Lyra, blood harvest, all the words that had nearly killed him before they saved him.

He did not.

Competence, he was learning, sometimes meant leaving the dramatic sentence in its cage.

"You were attacked because someone else noticed enough of your signal to come hunting," Lars said. "We are not asking your blood to explain itself. We are asking whether you will help solve a current engineering problem under boundaries you set."

Mira's eyes moved from him to Grim.

"Clarify the signal without naming it."

Grim highlighted a redacted signal map. "Station Zero and the quiet-node mesh can hear active nanobot carriers as weak coherence signatures. Hidden arkology fragments, guardian stations, low-noise observatories, dormant relay caches, and old ships all listen from different angles. Your signal is one of seventeen strong candidates across ninety years. It identifies a probable carrier. It does not reveal identity, memory, consent, or ownership."

"What does it hear?"

"Not words," Grim said. "Not thoughts. Nanobot timing drift. Repair-cycle harmonics. Biological error correction too clean for ordinary medicine. Route choices bending toward low-noise systems. Professional problem-solving with the old shape. Enough to locate probability. Not enough to open a person."

Mira's fingers flattened on the table.

"That is surveillance."

"Yes," Lars said.

The room tightened around the honesty.

He let it.

"It is also the reason we found you before the people who tried to harvest you got a second attempt. Both things are true. One does not clean the other."

Fabron's face changed by one millimeter: approval filed where no one could use it against him.

Mira looked at the next column.

"Quiet Knock."

"The network can find you," Lars said. "That does not give us the right to touch what it found. The knock has to fit the life you know, or it is just another violation wearing clever shoes."

Grim added three current items:

MATERIALS RING SENSOR ACCESS

HOSTILE HULL FATIGUE CLASSIFICATION

NO BIOLOGICAL INFERENCE / NO IDENTITY LANGUAGE

"No old names," Grim said. "No direct contradiction of cover memory. No identity prompts. No memory work. If distress crosses your boundary, the system stops."

"Whose system?"

"Mine," Grim said. "Fabron's witness record. A sealed offsite veto you do not have to name. Your local vault if you approve."

"And him?" Mira nodded toward Lars.

"He gets overruled," Fabron said.

Lars pointed at him. "He has been waiting all day to say that."

"Longer."

Mira's mouth almost moved.

Not a smile.

A structural test passing under load.

Then the lights flickered.

This time the emergency system did not recover cleanly. A low tone pulsed through the wall. Fabron's comm lit with fresh alerts, three at once.

"External approach," he said. "Three signatures. No transponders."

Grim's head tilted. "Fast. Badly concealed. Aggressively biological."

"Black Hole?" Lars asked.

"Or someone imitating them with poor taste."

The operation shifted without anyone needing to declare it dramatic.

"Lane two active," Valdrun said in Lars's ear. "Do not pull her. Let the work invite her."

Lars pushed off the table.

"Doctor Sovan. Current problem. Three ships are approaching through the brown dwarf's particle wake. We need hull behavior classification from local fatigue sensors. You may refuse. You may limit scope. We will not ask what you are. We will not ask what you remember."

Mira's gaze went to the wall, then to the pressure glass, then to the vibration monitor mounted above the materials ring. Her face changed, not into revelation, not into destiny, but into work.

Work was safer than wonder.

"Scope," she said.

Grim answered before Lars could. "Current hull stress, drive compensation, sampling geometry, station structural risk. Excluded: blood inference, personal identity inference, memory correlation, old-language triggers."

"Stop condition?"

"You say stop," Fabron said. "Or the monitor flags distress. Or I judge the room unsafe."

"You?"

"Yes."

"That is irritatingly useful."

"I cultivate it."

Mira looked at Lars. "If I say no, do those ships still arrive?"

"Yes."

"If I say yes, do I become your tool?"

There it was.

Not fear alone. Not accusation alone. A person feeling the edges of a machine built around her without permission and demanding to know whether the next machine would be different.

Lars made himself answer without charm.

"No. You become a specialist choosing a bounded task under hostile conditions. Afterward you can throw us out, ask one question, or never speak to me again."

"That last option has appeal."

"People keep saying that. Very rude pattern."

Mira breathed once through her nose. Her shoulder hurt; Lars could see it in the controlled angle of her posture. Her hand was wrapped from the fight. Her life had been invaded by false soldiers, impossible metadata, polite military protection, and a rude man refusing to say the dangerous word she could feel waiting behind the room.

She still reached for the console.

"Current evidence only," she said. "Hull behavior. Station risk. No blood. No identity. No old language. No clever nudges."

Grim's lenses brightened. "Consent boundary recorded."

"Do not sound pleased."

"Pleased state suppressed."

"Badly."

Fabron opened a local command bridge to the materials ring and cut Aphex central out of the loop with the elegance of a man committing treason through proper procedure.

"Doctor Sovan has provisional technical authority over structural interpretation," he said. "Adjacent to my command."

"Adjacent," Mira said.

"Under would be inaccurate."

"Better."

Lars moved toward the door.

"Grim, clean local data only. Fabron, med bay hard seal and no remote orders. Havets, keep being creepy and useful. Valdrun, tell me if I start acting like a throne-shaped idiot."

"I have been doing so for centuries," Valdrun said.

"I meant more urgently."

"No."

Mira's fingers paused over the controls.

"Director Odin."

He stopped.

"Earlier. The word you almost used."

He knew which one.

Coherence.

The old greeting sat behind his teeth like a lit match.

"I won't say it unless you ask."

She nodded once.

Not trust.

A temporary load rating.

"Good."

Outside the conference room, the station shifted into emergency posture. Doors sealed. Soldiers moved. Remote command began issuing instructions that local hands were already learning to distrust.

Somewhere beyond the hull, three silent ships entered the brown dwarf's dim and dirty light.

Inside the room, Mira Sovan began reading their bones through stress.

It was not an awakening.

Not yet.

It was a bounded task.

It was the first ask.
