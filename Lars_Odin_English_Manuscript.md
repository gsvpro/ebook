# Lars Odin

## Chapter 1: The Rim

Lars "Plates" Odin ordered a large bottle of Xaltor X whisky, four small glasses, and one Galactic IPA, then pretended to pay with the comm unit on his wrist.

In reality he paid with his hand.

It was a stupid habit. A dangerous habit. The payment lattice was supposed to authenticate through the visible consumer layer. Thumbprint. Retina flicker. Receipt to his comm. Then forget about him. Lars did not like polite systems. His fingers brushed the bar's worn composite counter. The payment lattice opened in his head like a cheap tent in bad weather.

Every weakness was there.

Bad session rotation. A lazy checksum. Three old privilege escalations hidden under two decades of emergency patches. A maintenance backdoor left behind by someone who had been drunk, underpaid, or both.

He could have emptied the bar's entire account in less than a second.

That would have been low, even for a man wearing yellow sunglasses with pink frames.

Lars made a small grimace, authorized the transfer, and tried not to flinch as the information streamed through him. The bartender stared at the whisky bottle with the same expression ordinary humans used for unexploded ordnance.

Lars pointed over his shoulder with his thumb.

Four bald, broad-shouldered Abantu were sitting in the back of the pub, half hidden in the greasy fog from the failing air scrubbers. Their table leaned slightly to one side. So did they. The pub itself occupied a forgotten wedge of The Rim's lower commercial ring, where old neon advertisements flickered in Galactic Swedish, Abantu trade pictograms, and one unknown script that had been blinking the same insurance warning for at least ninety years.

Ungrounded cables hissed above the bar. A bass-heavy techno track made the recycled air move in visible pulses. Somewhere behind the wall, a coolant pump coughed with the wet determination of an animal that should have been put out of its misery.

The bartender looked from Lars to the Abantu, then back to the bottle.

"For them," Lars said.

The bartender nodded, relieved. That made sense. Abantu could drink Xaltor X. Humans could not. One sip would put a normal, unmodified human into a coma, assuming the liver did not resign first.

Lars had never had that problem.

He did not know why.

He only knew he had to hide it.

The Abantu, bless their heavy skulls and generous hearts, made excellent cover.

His boots clicked against the sticky floor as he crossed the room. They were military grade, smart-balanced, adaptive-grip, and disgracefully expensive. He had bought the most costly equipment available in three systems so he could look like a spoiled idiot who had no idea what he was doing.

The black captain's coat helped. It was dusty, oil-stained, and cut from a graphite-kevlar composite reinforced with synthetic spider silk he had manufactured himself in a private lab near LP 890-9c. Under it he wore a battered work overall that looked like it had survived three engine rooms, two fistfights, and a regrettable romance with a plasma welder.

On his shoulder rested an Aphex Defence Ragnarrök 5000.

That part was harder to explain away.

The weapon threw coherent bursts of gamma radiation and proton plasma wrapped in a containment pulse. In ordinary language: bright, angry balls of physics that turned most problems into smoke and regret. Aphex Defence had given it to him six months earlier, along with a board seat he had not asked for. Lars had helped Professor H. Fayolle solve what the professor called an impossible weapons optimization problem.

Lars had called it "a Tuesday with decent coffee."

He set the whisky bottle down on the Abantu table with a heavy thud.

"Gentlemen," he said, because he was in a good mood and because Abantu enjoyed ceremony when poison was involved. "I come bearing medicine."

The Abantu nearest the wall made a rumbling sound of approval. His name involved two throat clicks, a nasal whistle, and a jaw motion Lars refused to attempt in public. Lars had privately named him Copper, because his skin had a faint metallic sheen and because Lars was lazy.

He uncorked the bottle, poured four proper measures, and slid the glasses across the table. Then he took a long pull of IPA and, while the Abantu lifted their glasses, tipped a generous splash of Xaltor X into his beer.

No one noticed.

For a few seconds the whisky bit through the beer, through his tongue, through the constant electrical weather behind his eyes.

Then the world became quieter.

Not quiet. Never quiet. But quieter.

His mind usually ran ahead of him. Questions unfolded into answers before he had finished asking them. Machines exposed themselves by rhythm, heat, and tolerance. People betrayed intent through pulse, scent, eye movement, micropressure in the skin—a hundred tiny signals he wished he could stop seeing.

The Xaltor X dulled the edge.

It was like wrapping blankets around several illegal quantum computers someone had hidden in his skull.

Copper raised his glass.

"To surviving another shift."

"That bad?" Lars asked.

Copper drank, exhaled through all four nostril slits, and leaned back. "Quota day."

The others groaned.

"Ah," Lars said. "The sacred ritual of management pretending rock has ambition."

That got a laugh. Abantu laughter sounded like gravel being poured into a metal drum, but Lars liked it.

"We missed it," Copper said. "Plasma shovel failed. Threw the mining robot off course. By the time we corrected the drift, the whole seam had thermal stress fractures. Useless."

"What model?"

"Tarkon-8 industrial scoop. Modified for nickel-iron vacuum work."

"Oh, that idiot thing."

Copper blinked. "You know it?"

Lars took another drink. The shape of the machine assembled itself in his head—not a picture, exactly, but a pressure diagram with moods. Tarkon had built the scoop around a cheap field stabilizer and compensated with software. That was the sort of decision that made Lars want to hit executives with laminated safety manuals.

He pulled a paper napkin from the dispenser. Actual paper. The Rim had many wonders, most of them old and poorly maintained.

"Your shovel isn't failing," Lars said. "It's obeying the wrong part of its own control stack."

He sketched quickly. Three loops. One heat vector. A compensation curve. A little ugly, but readable. His hand moved before he had settled the whole explanation, which annoyed him. It always annoyed him. He liked to pretend he was thinking. Most people found it comforting.

"There," he said, sliding the napkin over. "Disable the second smoothing routine. Not the first. The second. Then cap the plasma pulse at ninety-two percent and let the scoop stutter every seventh cycle. It will sound broken, but the seam won't crack."

Copper stared at the napkin.

The Abantu beside him leaned over.

The third Abantu whistled.

"Have you worked mining?" Copper asked.

Lars opened his mouth.

The pub dimmed.

Not physically. The neon still flickered, the cables still spat, the bass still thudded in the ribs. But something in him slid backward.

For a moment he smelled hot metal and asteroid dust.

He was not in the pub anymore. He was in a rented manufacturing bay on the SPECULOOS consortium station orbiting the super-Earth LP 890-9c, four months into building a freight ship no sane insurance cooperative would certify. Robots climbed the ribs of the hull. Printers sang in layered harmonics. Beyond the pressure glass, the planet's terminator line glowed blue-white, a knife edge between weather and night.

He had been tired then too.

Not normal tired. Driven tired. The kind that makes you forget food and sleep and social obligations with equal contempt.

He had already made more money than any reasonable person could spend by shouting nonsense into the Galactic Net as a fashionable idiot with good hair. He had invested some of it in quantum crypto, left most of it earning interest in boring accounts, and then, for reasons he could not explain, started building a ship.

It had felt urgent.

That was the word. Urgent.

Not exciting. Not profitable. Not even sensible.

Necessary.

Something had been gnawing at him. There were the voices that were not voices, more like thoughts arriving in the wrong accent. There were objects that moved when he was sure he had left them elsewhere. There were people following him badly, then professionally, then not at all, which was worse. Over the last few years, several high-ranking corporate directors had suffered accidents so strange that the official explanations sounded like parody.

There was a pattern.

Lars hated patterns he could not finish.

So he built the ship.

He worked fourteen hours a day in the lab, then let the robots and printers spit out parts through the night. When rare metals ran short, he went into the asteroid belt himself. He developed survey methods, plasma drills, and handheld plasma shovels that one person or one robot could operate without turning the whole rock into glittering useless slag.

Professor Fayolle had occupied the neighboring lab, which was much cleaner and much more French.

Fayolle worked for Aphex Defence. He spoke lyrically about coffee breaks and called fika "a grand Swedish civilizational contribution" with an expression so solemn that Lars had not dared laugh in his face.

One afternoon the professor showed him a weapons module and a set of equations that had nearly broken an entire Aphex research division.

Too many unknowns, Fayolle said.

Lars saw the solution before the professor finished complaining.

He tried to hide his grin by sounding casual.

"It's pretty simple if you think about it," Lars had said, which was never the right way to begin with a French scientist. "You have fundamentals. Thermodynamics, entropy, volume energy. Volume is the bastard, obviously, but only because you're treating it in ordinary three-space."

Fayolle had gone very still.

Lars grabbed a marker and drew three axes on the lab wall because the board was full and because walls were just boards with worse manners.

"Plot the tolerance cloud here. Then transpose it through the time axis. No, not like that. You need the collapse boundary as a spacetime volume, not a point. Then use the golden ratio as a search constraint."

"The golden ratio?" Fayolle had whispered, offended and fascinated.

"Nature likes practical jokes."

Twenty minutes later, Fayolle sat down, pale as printer resin.

"Merde."

"You solved the collapse problem," he said.

"Probably."

"And the optimization problem."

"Possibly."

"And three thermal bleed issues we had not disclosed."

"I read a lot."

Fayolle had stared at him as if Lars were either a genius, a criminal, or both.

Lars had finished his coffee.

"Don't forget to put me on the patent."

He had meant it as a joke.

Aphex Defence did not understand jokes when profit was involved.

Now, sitting in the pub on The Rim, Lars realized Copper was still staring at him.

"Asteroids," Lars said, touching the Ragnarrök's damaged-looking grip with one finger. "I did some asteroid mining in a previous life."

The phrase made the back of his neck hurt.

Only slightly.

Enough.

He took another drink.

"Anyway," he said, too loudly. "Try the napkin before your manager steals your pay."

Copper folded the napkin with great care, as if it were a treaty.

Lars stood, adjusted his black coat, and gave the Abantu a lazy salute.

"See you a little later."

He turned toward the exit.

On the way out, a drunk at the nearest table knocked over his beer.

For everyone else, it happened quickly.

For Lars, the glass began to fall through a thick, merciful syrup of time. Foam climbed the rim. Amber liquid rolled outward. The drunk's fingers twitched too late. A woman across the table started to frown. A neon sign buzzed twice.

Lars reached out, caught the glass, and set it upright without spilling a drop.

The drunk stared at him.

"Careful," Lars said. "Belgian beer. Strong stuff."

The man looked angry, which was unreasonable but common.

Lars left before the universe offered him another opportunity to be helpful.

The corridor outside was long, half empty, and built for crowds The Rim no longer had. Once, centuries ago, this station on the edge of the KBC Void had been a frontier jewel. Now it showed its age: patched hull plates, mismatched gravity tiles, old alien ornament under cheap human signage. The air smelled of metal, ozone, frying oil, and too many species breathing the same recycled air.

The Rim sat where the Milky Way began to thin toward the impossible emptiness beyond.

People called it the edge of the known.

Lars disliked that phrase. It sounded dramatic and imprecise. The KBC Void was not an edge. It was an absence so large that even modern cosmology spoke of it with professional embarrassment. Billions of light-years of almost nothing around the galaxy.

Nobody knew why it was there.

That bothered him.

Everything bothered him lately.

His comm unit buzzed.

Incoming quantum-encrypted bitstream.

Source: Gabriel Arsenault, chairman of Aphex Defence.

Lars sighed. "Of course."

He accepted the connection.

Arsenault appeared above the comm in a sharply dressed projection with silver hair, a dramatic jaw, and the facial expression of a man who had never entered a room without believing history had improved.

"Oui, oui, Lars Odin. Excellent. I finally have you."

"That sounds ominous, Gabriel."

"Not ominous. Administrative."

"Worse."

Arsenault smiled. "At the last quarterly meeting, the board confirmed your position. You are now formally seated as a director of Aphex Defence."

"Do I have to attend meetings?"

"Remote attendance is acceptable when necessary."

"That is not a no."

"There are very few meetings."

Arsenault ignored him. "We have transferred the first retroactive compensation to your account. You will also find the revised patent allocations attached. Professor Fayolle continues to speak of your fika sessions with extraordinary passion. A grand Swedish tradition. We have begun introducing it across our major offices."

"Weapon engineers with cinnamon buns. Terrifying."

"Productive," Arsenault said. "We are now the leading weapons firm in the galaxy. Partly, I must admit, because of your unconventional contributions. We have also adopted your recommendation that our highest-performance systems be sold only to allied governments and verified defensive coalitions."

"That wasn't a recommendation. That was basic self-preservation."

"Often the same thing."

Lars felt the contract arrive, then the account notice behind it. He did not open the visible display. The figures resolved somewhere behind his eyes.

Seven point three billion Galactic kronor had landed in his account.

He laughed despite himself.

"Is there a problem?" Arsenault asked.

"No. I just have even less reason to work now."

"You have responsibilities, monsieur Odin."

"I have beef to buy. Different category."

Arsenault leaned closer to the projection field. "One more thing. We take care of our own."

"That sounded rehearsed."

"It was sincere."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

Arsenault's smile widened. "On récolte ce qu'on sème."

The connection ended.

The corridor became too quiet.

Lars stopped walking.

Something shifted.

Not enough for a normal person to notice. A tiny compression front moving through recycled air. Heat rising behind it. Electromagnetic noise blooming in an ugly, disciplined pattern.

His neck pain vanished.

Everything else became sharp.

A wall of hot plasma and gamma radiation came around the bend at supersonic speed.

Lars had already opened the locked maintenance door to his right.

He took two fast steps, pivoted, pulled his shoulder through the gap, and slammed the door as the corridor became white.

Heat punched through the seam.

For a fraction of a second his left shoulder felt as if someone had filled it with needles and sunlight. The maintenance alcove lights flickered alive. Smoke curled under the door. His yellow sunglasses had slipped from his face and lay on the floor as a melted pink-and-gold puddle.

"Brand new," Lars said.

His hair smoked.

The grip of the Ragnarrök had softened under his hand.

He looked at his shoulder. The graphite-kevlar coat had five pinprick holes in it, black-edged and glowing faintly.

His skin underneath felt fine.

That was wrong.

He did not have time to think about it.

Old habit surfaced, cold and absolute: if it is not immediately fatal, continue.

Old habit from where?

The thought scraped the inside of his skull.

He pushed it away, raised the Ragnarrök, and opened the door.

Five figures in black combat armor advanced through the smoke thirty meters down the corridor. Heavy suits. Military posture. Wrong station markings. Their weapons were up, but their formation was sloppy in the way people became sloppy when they believed the target had already died.

"Boring," Lars said.

He crouched in the middle of the corridor and fired five times.

The Ragnarrök kicked against his shoulder with a deep, satisfied thump. Five contained plasma bursts crossed the corridor in staggered lines, each one wrapped in a brief halo of impossible violet. The attackers had less than half a second to understand the situation.

They used it poorly.

Five black-armored bodies became five smoking heaps of fused ceramic and metal.

Lars stood and walked toward them.

His internal link to the ship opened before he consciously called it.

Grim?

The answer arrived as a voice in his head, dry and metallic and far too pleased with itself.

You should probably deal with the microdrone cloud first.

I saw it.

Then why are you walking dramatically?

Because walking dramatically is free.

A swarm of microdrones spilled out of a side conduit ahead, each no larger than a fingernail, each carrying enough charge to blind sensors or poison lungs. Lars sent an electromagnetic pulse through the corridor lights.

The drones died in a sparkling rain.

Grim continued, Do not shoot the people coming behind them. That is the cavalry. They appear to be rescuing you.

How charming.

Twenty armored soldiers in polished Aphex Defence suits emerged from the far intersection at a run. Their lead officer reached Lars first, breathing hard inside his helmet.

"Major Elliot Fabron, Eleventh Aphex Defence Brigade. We are here to extract you. Are you injured?"

Lars pointed at his smoking hair.

"Emotionally."

Fabron lifted his visor. He had the stiff, handsome, irritated face of a French officer who had expected a corpse and found an inconvenience.

"Since 17:20 station time we have engaged and neutralized one hundred and fifteen hostile combatants across this sector."

Lars frowned. "That was when I went to the pub."

"Yes."

"Who attacks a man during pub time?"

"We believe the force belongs to Black Hole B-7."

Lars went still.

Even through the whisky and adrenaline, the name landed heavily.

Black Hole B-7 was not a gang. It was a fascist paramilitary consortium with corporate organs, military doctrine, and penal brutality baked into its command culture. They traded in weapons, slaves, stolen artifacts, and political collapse.

They believed knowledge was something you tortured out of people and stored in a bunker until it became useful.

"Why would they want to bother me?" Lars asked.

Fabron glanced at the Ragnarrök, then at the five smoking heaps behind Lars.

"We do not yet know."

Lars looked into the station systems.

He did not mean to.

The local camera grid unfolded. Security telemetry. Door states. Heat signatures. Drone traffic. Emergency dispatch. The Rim's internal networks were old, fragmented, and badly patched, but that almost made them easier to read. Like listening to an old drunk tell the truth by accident.

There.

Level 197. Maintenance spine, moving toward the freight terminal. Seventy-five microdrones. Ten armed signatures. Black armor.

Grim's voice arrived first.

I have just cleaned vermin from our docking bay. Ten Black Hole mercenaries. Also drones. Disappointing manners.

Good work.

You are welcome. I used restraint.

Define restraint.

The docking bay remains attached to the station.

Lars cleared his throat and looked at Fabron.

"You missed seventy-five drones and ten mercenaries heading for my ship. Grim removed them. I can confirm Black Hole B-7."

Fabron stared.

"Your ship AI did what?"

"Housekeeping."

Lars lifted the Ragnarrök and held it out, grip first. The handle had cooled into warped strands with the impressions of his fingers pressed deep into the material.

"Also, this is flimsy. Tell Aphex you have improvement potential."

Fabron's eyes dropped to Lars's bare hand.

"Let me see your palm."

"Later."

Lars was already moving.

Grim, status outside the station?

Two Black Hole frigates are currently pounding the excrement out of an Aphex destroyer.

Can we help?

We have more power than all of them combined, but we came here for beef and wine. Not a small war.

We'll be quick.

That is what you said before the asteroid refinery incident.

Lars found a small maintenance hatch, opened it, and jumped.

He fell thirty-five levels through a vertical service shaft.

The station blurred around him in bands of light, pipework, maintenance graffiti, and startled sensor motes. Near the bottom he spread his hands and boots. The smart grip surfaces bit the shaft walls, shedding speed in a shriek of friction and heat.

His palms burned.

Then it stopped.

He landed in a crouch outside the docking concourse and kept running.

Behind him, Fabron's voice echoed faintly through the comm system.

"Odin! Stop! That is not a transit route!"

"Works fine!" Lars shouted back.

His ship waited in Bay K-44 under low amber lights.

The Fastulv looked like an old freight hauler that had seen too many border inspections and not enough paint. Its hull was patched in four colors. One cargo clamp was deliberately misaligned. The name was stenciled slightly crooked in Galactic Swedish and old runic lettering because Lars had been tired and Grim had found it funny.

Under the ugly skin lay a machine that could embarrass most cruisers.

The gangway lowered before he reached it.

He sprinted up, through the pressure seal, along the narrow spine corridor, and onto the bridge.

Grim sat in the copilot's chair.

Two months ago Grim had been a ship computer with a biological quantum core cultured from human neural tissue and fed through a nutrient lattice. Then the computer had announced it wanted to eat.

Lars had made the mistake of saying, "Fine, draw up a plan."

Grim had returned the next day with blueprints for an android body, digestive analysis modules, taste receptors, etiquette subroutines he did not use, and a procurement list that included beef, saffron, polymer muscle, and three illegal servo assemblies.

Now Grim had a tall, angular metal body, expressive optical lenses, and the irritating habit of copying everything Lars did. In public he walked stiffly, let his servos whine, and pretended to be far less intelligent than he was.

At the moment he had forgotten to do any of that.

His metal fingers danced across the controls with inhuman precision.

"You are late," Grim said.

"I was attacked."

"That is your usual excuse."

"It keeps being true."

The docking clamps released. The Fastulv dropped out of the bay, rolled under The Rim's lower habitat arc, and accelerated.

The tactical display lit.

Two Black Hole B-7 frigates held a brutal crossfire pattern around a wounded Aphex destroyer. The destroyer's aft section vented atmosphere and frozen debris in a glittering plume. Its port engine flickered. Its shields were down to ragged scraps.

The frigates were larger than Lars liked.

Then again, most things were larger than he liked until they exploded.

"Plotting attack vectors," he said. "Give me a half-elliptic intercept, delta point zero two, then bring our nose through the radiation shadow of the destroyer."

"Already doing it."

"Then do it with more admiration."

"I am overwhelmed."

Lars armed four tactical nuclear missiles, two kilotons each, and a hundred microdrone penetrators. Too much for station-adjacent combat. About right for a message.

He opened a channel to the wounded Aphex ship.

"Aphex destroyer, this is Captain Lars Odin of the freight vessel Fastulv. It is about to become warm. Reverse thrust if you enjoy remaining in one piece."

The destroyer captain appeared in a shaky projection, blood running from one eyebrow.

"We have hull breaches and half propulsion."

"Then limp with enthusiasm."

Grim brought the Fastulv between the destroyer and the frigates. The freight ship shuddered as incoming fire splashed against its layered field geometry.

One of the frigates shifted aim.

Too late.

Lars fired the plasma cannons first, not to kill but to herd. The frigates rolled away from the wounded destroyer, exactly as their doctrine required. Sensible. Predictable. Fatal.

The drones arrived from below the elliptic plane, a direction no lazy tactical officer watched during a close assault near a station. They struck sensor clusters, point-defense nodes, and maneuvering vanes.

The missiles came from the opposite vector.

Both frigates bloomed white.

In vacuum, nuclear weapons were not the great atmospheric fists old movies loved. No fireball rolling through air. No thunder. Just radiation, particle spray, hard light, and every unshielded system suddenly learning humility.

The Fastulv groaned.

A burnt smell spread through the bridge.

"Damage?" Lars asked.

"Mostly cosmetic," Grim said. "One thermal manifold is sulking. Also, the wine locker held."

"Good."

To starboard, the Aphex destroyer drifted, alive. To port, the frigates tore themselves apart from the inside as secondary explosions walked through their hulls.

Lars leaned back.

"Maybe the nukes were excessive."

"No," Grim said. "You were irritated."

"They interrupted pub time."

"And beef procurement."

"Exactly."

For a few seconds, neither of them spoke.

Beyond the tactical display, The Rim curved against the blackness: a battered ring of light and habitation at the threshold of the KBC Void. Past it there was almost nothing. A darkness so large it made distances inside the galaxy feel domestic.

Lars looked at that darkness and felt, absurdly, that something inside it was looking back.

Not a ghost.

Not a god.

Not nonsense.

A signal, maybe. A statistical pull. A pattern too faint for instruments and too persistent for imagination.

The back of his neck began to ache.

He looked away.

"Can we buy beef now?" Grim asked.

Lars rubbed his face with both hands.

"Yes. Beef, wine, new sunglasses, and then we leave before another fascist circus decides my blood is collectible."

Grim turned his metal head.

"Your blood?"

Lars frowned.

He had no idea why he had said that.

For one sharp instant he saw a room he had never seen: space-gray walls, black glass, white light shaped into lines so elegant they seemed grown rather than built. A pale blue interface moved in wavelengths no human eye should see. A woman's voice, not heard but understood, said one word in a language he did not know.

Then pain closed around the memory.

Lars sucked air through his teeth.

"Migraine," he muttered.

Grim watched him too carefully.

"You do not get migraines."

"I do now."

The Fastulv turned back toward The Rim, engines burning clean and silent.

Behind them, the wreckage of Black Hole B-7 cooled in the dark.

Ahead, somewhere inside the old station's artificial hemisphere, a farmer named Vigor Hallman was preparing dinner, wearing a false beard, guarding a self-sufficient oasis on the edge of impossible emptiness, and waiting for Lars Odin as if he had known he would come.

Lars did not know that yet.

He only knew the headache was getting worse.

## Chapter 2: The Farmer

The Rim's inner market hemisphere had been designed by someone who believed commercial zoning should include weather.

Lars stepped through the pressure arch and into artificial afternoon. The ceiling was a sky. Not a screen, not exactly. A layered atmospheric projection, humidity-managed and optically deep enough that clouds moved with convincing laziness across a blue vault three hundred meters overhead. Birds crossed between them. Real birds, according to the station registry, though Lars suspected half were sensor drones with better manners.

The ground curved upward in the distance until farms, stalls, workshops, and little houses clung to the far side of the sphere like a child's model of a world turned inside out. At the opposite horizon, a herd of cattle grazed on green pasture under a sun that had never been born.

Grim stopped beside him.

"That," the robot said, "is where the beef lives."

"Try not to sound predatory."

"I am expressing logistical interest."

"You are drooling through a metal face."

"That is condensation."

Lars glanced down. Two pack robots followed them, hovering half a meter above the path with obedient blankness. Grim had insisted on bringing them. Lars had argued they could carry their own food. Grim had replied that sixty kilos of beef, wine, root vegetables, emergency saffron, and possible dessert required dignity.

After the last hour, Lars did not have the energy to dispute dessert.

They left the main walkway and followed a gravel path toward the pasture. Actual gravel. It crunched under Lars's boots with unnecessary honesty. The air smelled of grass, wet wood, soil, warm animal hide, and somewhere in the distance, fresh bread.

It was absurd.

It was a self-contained farm inside an ancient space station balanced on the edge of the greatest void known to civilization.

It should have felt fake.

It felt more real than the rest of The Rim.

Lars did not like that.

He also did not like the way his headache eased when he looked at the farm's layout. The fields were not arranged for maximum output. Any corporate agricultural engineer would have called the design inefficient. Too many small plots. Too many crop types. Redundant irrigation lines. Storage sheds placed in odd triangular patterns. Livestock distributed across three separated loops instead of one optimized feed chain.

Inefficient, unless the goal was survival after partial system failure.

The thought arrived fully formed.

Lars frowned.

"What?" Grim asked.

"Nothing."

"Your nothing face is different from your normal face."

"That's because my normal face has charm."

They passed a low stone wall. It was not stone. The texture was convincing, but its thermal profile was wrong, and the seams breathed microscopically with station ventilation. Smart material. Very good smart material. Better than anything the public Rim maintenance economy should have been able to afford.

Lars looked away before the observation became a question.

Questions were dangerous lately.

At the top of the path stood a timber building with a red-painted facade, white trim, and solar tiles disguised as old roof shingles. Behind it, greenhouses stepped down the slope in terraces. Small robots moved among the plants with the calm precision of experienced gardeners. None of them bumped into anything. None of them hesitated. Their pathing was too clean.

A man came around the corner of the house carrying a crate of something leafy and green.

He wore a battered hat, a red-checked shirt, denim overalls, and green rubber boots. A thick beard covered half his face. His movements were broad and rural and just a little too slow.

That was what caught Lars's attention.

Not too fast.

Too slow.

Someone pretending not to be fast.

"Welcome!" the man called. "Vigor Hallman. What can I do for you?"

His Galactic Swedish had the rounded vowels of old Earth countryside filtered through centuries of station dialect. Lars had heard stranger accents from aristocrats who had never visited a planet.

"Lars Odin," Lars said. "This is Grim."

Grim lifted one hand. His servos whined theatrically. "I am a simple service automaton with no unusual interests except food."

Lars closed his eyes for half a second.

Vigor looked at Grim, then at Lars, then back at Grim.

"Of course you are."

"We're looking for farm-direct beef," Lars said. "Preferably the sort that makes a man reconsider civilization."

Vigor's eyes narrowed with amusement beneath the hat brim. "Then you have come to the right place."

"We need a large load. About sixty kilos. Maybe more if the robot starts making emotional arguments."

"He will," Grim said.

"And wine," Lars added. "Good wine. Not station solvent with a vineyard label."

"Naturally."

"And elderflower cordial," Grim said.

Lars turned to him. "You have never had elderflower cordial."

"Then I am correcting a deficiency."

Vigor clapped his hands together. "Excellent. Follow me."

He led them around the main house to a shop built like an old Swedish farm store. The floor was covered with clean straw. Dried herbs hung from dark beams. Glass-fronted coolers displayed vacuum-packed cuts of beef in perfect rows, marbling like pale lightning through red muscle. Root vegetables filled wooden bins. Cheeses rested under controlled humidity bells. Wine bottles lined the walls with labels from Earth, Mars, Xaltor, Heliostat imports, and two regions Lars had never heard of.

That last part bothered him.

He had heard of most regions that produced expensive wine.

"We are self-sufficient," Vigor said, spreading his arms as if introducing a kingdom. "Meat, vegetables, fruit, seed stock, water recycling, soil cultures, independent energy loops. We sell the surplus."

"On a station where half the lower ring smells like overheated socks and industrial despair."

"That is why people pay."

"Fair."

Lars walked to the beef display and forgot, briefly, about terrorists, headaches, Aphex Defence, and the KBC Void.

"Look at that marbling."

Grim pressed his metal face almost against the glass. "I am looking."

"You don't have salivary glands."

"I can install some."

"No."

"A small reservoir?"

"Absolutely not."

Vigor laughed. "You two are welcome to eat here while my robots pack your order. Long-distance guests should not be sent away hungry."

Lars meant to decline.

He really did.

They needed to leave. Black Hole B-7 had just thrown a small army at him. Aphex would flood the sector with security within hours. Galactic police would arrive within days and begin asking questions with forms. The sensible move was to buy supplies, board the Fastulv, and disappear into an ugly vector no one expected.

Instead he heard himself say, "That would be appreciated."

Grim's lenses brightened.

"Pepper steak and potato gratin," Grim said. "If possible."

Vigor looked delighted. "Very possible."

The robots began packing their order with efficient care. Vigor guided Lars and Grim back outside to a table under a white parasol on the lawn. The artificial sun warmed Lars's face. A waterfall spilled down a rock wall in the distance, feeding a narrow stream that cut through the pasture before vanishing under a wooden bridge.

No single water line.

Three backup channels hidden under decorative stone.

No monocrop.

No central control tower.

Defense positions concealed as birdhouses, fence posts, irrigation housings, and one extremely innocent-looking compost shed.

Lars sat slowly.

Vigor sat opposite him.

Grim sat beside Lars and, to his credit, remembered to make one knee joint squeal.

Two waiter robots in white jackets emerged from the house. One carried three glasses. Two whisky tumblers and a smaller glass filled with pale yellow cordial. Another set down a pitcher, then an unmarked bottle of whisky.

Lars knew the bottle shape before he read anything.

There was no label.

There did not need to be.

Xaltor X.

Real Xaltor X.

Not the pub's expensive bottle with distributor stamps and customs codes. This one was old, handmade, probably illegal in twelve jurisdictions and sacred in three.

Lars's right hand moved toward the Ragnarrök.

Vigor did not move.

That made it worse.

"Is this a test?" Lars asked quietly.

Grim's head turned a fraction. The robot's posture changed so subtly that only someone watching for violence would notice.

Vigor leaned back and lifted both hands, palms open.

"No test."

"Because I have had enough charming surprises today."

"I believe that." Vigor's eyes stayed kind. "You are among friends."

"That sentence gets people killed."

"Sometimes. Here it gets them dinner."

Lars scanned the farm.

Not with any equipment he admitted to owning.

The local wireless spectrum was clean. Too clean. Signal dampening embedded under the grass. Acoustic baffles in the fence line. Thermal masking in the roof. The robots had patrol subroutines behind their hospitality layers. Vigor's farmhouse was not a farmhouse. It was a fortified, self-sufficient node disguised as a farm by someone with excellent taste and a deeply suspicious mind.

Lars's breathing slowed.

Vigor watched him notice.

"The entire property is shielded from casual surveillance," Vigor said. "And most professional surveillance. We can defend ourselves."

"Against Black Hole B-7?"

"For a while."

"That's not much."

"It is more than most farms."

Lars almost smiled.

Vigor reached up, took hold of his beard, and lifted it away from his face.

Without it he looked younger. Not young, exactly. Time sat strangely on him. His skin had the mild weathering of a station farmer, but his eyes were sharp in a way farmers, soldiers, surgeons, and predators sometimes shared. There was something familiar in the shape of his cheekbones.

Lars had seen him before.

Impossible.

A memory rose: the same face without the hat, lit by pale blue lines; a corridor of space-gray material; laughter; someone saying a word that meant home, or danger, or both.

Pain tightened at the base of Lars's skull.

He shut the memory down.

The pain remained as a warning.

"You okay?" Grim asked, not aloud.

The question arrived inside Lars's head.

That was another thing they did not discuss in front of people.

"Fine," Lars answered silently.

Vigor poured whisky into one tumbler, then the other. He poured cordial into Grim's glass with equal solemnity.

"You recognize a like-minded person when you see one," Vigor said.

"Do you?"

"Sometimes."

"And what am I?"

Vigor pushed one whisky glass toward him.

"Tired."

Lars stared at him.

It was not the answer he expected.

"That is either very perceptive or very lazy."

"Both can be true."

Lars picked up the glass. The whisky smelled of smoke, minerals, and cold fruit. In him it landed like a sedative.

"To surviving the day," Vigor said.

"The day isn't over."

"Then to surviving the next hour."

They drank.

For a moment, the noise inside Lars fell back.

His shoulders loosened.

Grim sipped the cordial.

The robot froze.

Lars looked at him. "What?"

Grim's voice came out very carefully. "What is this called?"

"Elderflower cordial," Vigor said.

Grim turned to Lars.

"We need one hundred liters."

"No."

"Fifty."

"No."

"Forty."

Vigor laughed so hard he had to put down his glass.

The waiter robots brought dinner soon after: pepper-seared beef, potato gratin, salad bright with herbs, warm bread, butter, and a red wine that made Lars reconsider several prejudices about station agriculture.

For several minutes no one spoke.

This pleased Grim enormously.

The robot ate with focused reverence, cutting each bite to exactly the same size. Lars had once told him etiquette involved moderation. Grim had interpreted this as geometric consistency.

Halfway through the meal, Lars leaned closer.

"Your left wrist servo is too smooth," he murmured.

Grim paused.

"What?"

"When you reached for the glass. No theatrical lag. No cheap-robot nonsense."

"I was distracted by flavor."

"That is exactly how people get caught."

"People?"

"Robots. People. Whatever category prevents you from saying something philosophical during dinner."

Grim looked at his fork.

"I will squeak next time."

"Good."

Vigor's eyes crinkled.

"It is hard," he said, "pretending to be less than you are."

Lars did not answer.

The statement sat on the table between the wine and the butter.

Too accurate. Too sharp. Too close.

The headache nudged him again.

He took another bite of beef instead of thinking.

After dinner, Vigor poured coffee. Real coffee, dark and bitter. Not synthesized station concentrate. Lars held the cup in both hands and watched steam curl into the artificial sunlight.

"So," Vigor said, "you are provisioning for a trip."

"Maybe."

"The kind where you buy the best beef on the station and enough wine to survive your own company?"

"Grim's company. Mine is delightful."

"Debatable," Grim said.

Lars looked across the pasture.

The cattle moved in calm loops. The robots tended the fields without hurry. Nothing here was maximized. Everything had a backup. The farm could lose half its systems and still feed people. It was not efficient in the way corporations used the word.

It was alive.

He felt unexpectedly sad.

"We are being hunted," he said.

Vigor did not pretend surprise.

"I heard there was trouble."

"That was before the two frigates."

"Ah."

"Black Hole B-7. They hit the pub sector, my docking bay, and an Aphex destroyer. Maybe more. They wanted something."

"You think they wanted you."

"People usually do. I am charming, wealthy, and heavily armed."

"And modest."

"That too."

Vigor drank coffee and waited.

Lars hated good listeners. They left too much room for truth.

"I said something on the ship," Lars admitted. "About my blood being collectible."

Grim's head turned.

Vigor did not move.

"I don't know why I said it."

The artificial sun passed behind a programmed cloud. The table dimmed.

For a moment Vigor's eyes caught a faint blue reflection from nowhere Lars could identify.

"There are old stories," Vigor said carefully. "About people whose blood carried machines too small to see. Machines that could heal, remember, build, communicate. Most stories are wrong."

"Most?"

"Enough wrong to be dangerous. Enough right to be worse."

Lars's fingers tightened around the coffee cup.

"You trade in old stories?"

"I farm."

"With surveillance shielding, layered defense, illegal whisky, and robots that move like special forces."

"Difficult century for farmers."

Lars snorted.

The laugh helped.

The headache did not.

Vigor set down his cup. "In a few weeks this sector will be thick with Galactic police, Aphex Defence, private investigators, bounty systems, insurance auditors, and opportunists pretending to be journalists."

"Then we'll be gone."

"Good."

"You say that like you know where."

"No," Vigor said. "I say it like staying would be unwise."

A little wind moved across the grass. Somewhere, a cow lowed with deep philosophical indifference.

One of Vigor's robots approached with the pack order: sealed crates of beef, vegetables, wine, coffee, and, despite Lars's direct refusal, four containers of elderflower cordial.

Grim looked innocent.

Lars did not believe in robot innocence.

They stood.

At the gate, Vigor shook Lars's hand.

His grip was ordinary.

Deliberately ordinary.

Lars felt the same recognition flicker through him and avoided it with professional cowardice.

"If you need anything," Vigor said, "you can count on me. And on the station, more than you might think."

"That is a suspicious amount of generosity."

"Take it anyway."

Lars nodded.

He surprised himself by pulling Vigor into a brief hug.

Grim immediately did the same.

Vigor endured both with good humor.

They had gone twenty meters down the gravel path when a small column of armored figures came over the hill, walking with the exhausted stiffness of soldiers who had won the wrong kind of day. Major Elliot Fabron led them. His polished armor was scorched, his expression sour.

Lars pointed back at the farmhouse.

"See the gate? Follow the gravel path. You will be well looked after."

Fabron stopped in front of him. "Odin. You destroyed two enemy frigates with tactical nuclear weapons within station traffic control limits."

"You're welcome."

"That is not the phrase I would choose."

"Try it. Feels good."

Fabron looked past him at the pack robots. "Are those groceries?"

"Strategic groceries."

"We are conducting a live security operation."

"And you look hungry."

Fabron's jaw worked.

Lars clapped him on the shoulder.

"Take care of yourself. See you a little later."

He kept walking before the major could decide whether regulations permitted strangling a board member.

The path sloped down toward the market arch. Gravel crunched underfoot. Birds circled in the false sky. The farm receded behind them, peaceful and impossible.

Grim matched Lars's stride.

"Vigor is not a farmer," Grim said silently.

"He is definitely a farmer."

"You know what I mean."

"Unfortunately."

"His movements are masked. His property is a hardened node. His systems follow a resilience pattern I have seen in old fragments about Aerit arcologies."

The word struck.

Aerit.

Pain flashed white behind Lars's eyes.

He stumbled.

Grim caught his arm with too much speed.

Several market visitors looked over.

Grim's servos began whining loudly, belatedly theatrical.

"Apologies," Grim said aloud. "Simple service automaton clumsiness."

"Ow," Lars muttered.

Grim lowered his voice. "Do not think about the word."

"Great advice. Very actionable."

"I can say 'beef' repeatedly."

"That may help."

"Beef. Beef. Beef. Elderflower cordial. Beef."

The pain retreated from blinding to merely unpleasant.

Lars straightened.

For one second, he had seen something.

Not Vigor. Not the farm.

A station in darkness.

A hull that bent light around itself.

An airlock outlined in pale blue, visible in wavelengths no human eye should see.

Home.

The word came with such force that Lars nearly stopped again.

He swallowed it.

"We leave now," he said.

"Vector?"

Lars looked toward the pressure arch leading back to the docking ring. Past it waited the Fastulv, ugly and overpowered, while the expensive beef followed behind them on two levitating pack carts.

Past The Rim waited the KBC Void.

The emptiness no one understood.

"Outward," Lars said.

Grim was quiet for half a step.

"Into the Void?"

Lars did not answer at once.

The back of his neck pulsed, not exactly pain now. More like a hand pressing gently against a locked door from the other side.

"Just a short look," he said.

Grim made a sound that might have been a laugh or a servo fault.

"With you, that has never once been true."

## Chapter 3: The Dark Object

The Fastulv left The Rim under a filed civilian departure vector, then abandoned that vector eight minutes later.

Lars did not call it abandoning.

He called it "correcting for emerging navigational intuition."

Grim called it "lying to traffic control."

They were both right.

The Rim shrank behind them, its old habitat arcs and docking spines losing definition against the star-thick wash of the Milky Way. Ahead, the KBC Void opened like an error in the universe.

No destination marker.

No beacon.

No trade lane.

No sane reason to go that way.

On the way back through The Rim's market, Grim had bought replacement sunglasses from a kiosk between a knife vendor and a noodle stall.

Lars sat in the captain's chair with one boot hooked under the console, a cup of coffee clipped to the armrest, and the replacement pair on his face. The new ones had black lenses and blue frames. Grim had chosen them while claiming to understand human aesthetics.

"They make me look like a corrupt ski instructor," Lars said.

"You said you needed sunglasses."

"I meant good sunglasses."

"They were discounted."

"I'm worth forty-seven billion Galactic kronor."

"Fifty-four point three, after Aphex compensation."

"Then why are you buying discounted sunglasses?"

"We spent heavily on beef."

The ship hummed around them. Not loudly. Lars had tuned the engines until the vibration pattern sat just below irritation. A good ship had a voice, but it should not nag. The Fastulv's antimatter assembly, however, had developed a coolant condensation issue in the aft thermal manifold, which meant it had chosen nagging as an art form.

From somewhere below the deck came a metallic clang.

Grim tilted his head.

"The reactor is still possessed."

"Reactors are not possessed."

"Then explain the dripping."

"Thermal cycling through a badly seated condensate return valve."

"That is exactly what a possessed reactor wants you to think."

Lars sighed and pulled up the diagnostic model. The solution appeared before the schematic finished rendering. A minor tolerance mismatch caused by a print head that had lied about calibration by three microns. He made the correction, routed a maintenance drone to fabricate a new collar, and told the aft manifold to stop embarrassing him.

"Fixed," he said.

A pause.

Then the deck clang came again.

Lars closed his eyes.

The warning alarm chirped once.

Not an attack alarm. Not collision. Not radiation.

A sensor ambiguity.

Lars opened his eyes.

The forward display filled with blackness.

That should not have meant anything. Space was black. The Void was blacker. But this was not ordinary absence. The ship's passive optical layer had found an area ahead where background starlight behaved incorrectly. Not blocked. Not absorbed. Bent. A patch of darkness moved against the sky with the subtle wrongness of heat shimmer, except there was no heat and no medium to shimmer through.

Grim leaned forward.

"That is a strange signature."

"Define strange."

"No."

"No?"

"If I define it, you will pretend you already knew."

Lars enlarged the sensor map. Gravitational lensing without corresponding mass. Weak vacuum polarization. Intermittent femtosecond-scale timing offsets in the local clock grid. Thermal profile indistinguishable from background. Radar returned nothing. Lidar returned nonsense. Neutrino scatter showed a volume where a volume should not be.

"It's a station," Lars said.

The words left him before he had decided them.

Grim turned his metal head slowly.

"A station."

"Yes."

"In the KBC Void."

"Near it."

"Invisible."

"Mostly."

"With no normal mass signature."

"Apparently."

"And you know this because?"

Lars stared at the display.

The headache was back, but smaller now. Focused. A nail pressed at the base of his skull.

"Because the light is bending around structure," he said. "Not a sphere. Too many flat transitions. It is refracting through layered spacetime curvature. The mass isn't where the geometry says it should be because the geometry is being cheated."

"That was almost an explanation."

"Thank you."

"It also sounded like something from the six-hundred-year-old Aerit fragments I read during my leisure time."

The nail became a spike.

Lars gripped the armrest.

"Do not use that word."

Grim watched him. His optical lenses narrowed, an affectation he had copied from humans and then refined until it was more annoying than useful.

"Noted."

"And you do not have leisure time."

"I do when you sleep."

"I don't sleep."

"Exactly."

The dark object grew on the display as Grim brought the ship in along a half-parabolic approach. Lars felt the trajectory before he saw Grim enter it. It was correct. Not safe. Correct. Those were different categories.

"Decelerate on my mark," Lars said.

"I am already calculating the burn."

"Five."

"I know."

"Four."

"Lars."

"Three."

Grim was not strapped in.

"Two."

"You are enjoying this."

"One."

The Fastulv decelerated hard.

Grim hit the forward console with a resonant metallic clang.

The ship stopped short of the invisible surface by less than a hundred meters.

Grim lifted his head from the console.

"Was that necessary?"

"Do you want to collide with the invisible station?"

"I want workplace safety."

"You're a robot."

"With a brain."

"Wear a seatbelt."

Lars unbuckled and stood. The object ahead occupied the entire forward view now, though his eyes could barely make sense of it. The hull, if it was a hull, bent the background around itself. Stars smeared at the edges. Here and there, along angles too subtle for ordinary sight, something pulsed in pale blue.

Not visible blue, exactly.

His eyes registered it as blue because his brain needed a color and had given up.

Grim enlarged one of the pulses.

"I need to recalibrate."

"For what?"

"Your visual range."

Lars looked at him.

Grim's fingers moved over the console. "Do not start. I have known for weeks your retinal response includes wavelengths outside baseline human norms. You see things our public sensor suite does not. I have been politely ignoring it."

"That is not polite."

"It is extremely polite. I have a folder called Things Lars Pretends Are Normal."

"Delete it."

"It has backups."

The pale blue structures sharpened as Grim adjusted his optical systems. Lines appeared along the dark hull: not seams, but potential seams. Places where material could decide to become an opening. The design was almost offensively elegant. Space-gray, black, white where light caught it, and that strange blue beyond human range. No exposed bolts. No decorative aggression. Form and function fused so thoroughly that the object looked less manufactured than reasoned into existence.

Lars swallowed.

He had seen this aesthetic before.

No.

He had not.

"Suit up," he said.

"That is your response to an impossible ancient station hidden near the Void?"

"Would you prefer a speech?"

"I would prefer a risk assessment."

"Risk: high. Assessment: interesting."

"That is not an assessment."

"It is concise."

They left the bridge and moved down to the cargo bay. Lars pulled on a vacuum suit with reinforced joint seals and an EVA ion pack. The suit's outer layer was scuffed, patched, and much more advanced than it looked. Grim needed only pressure protection for his biological core and a small emergency oxygen loop mounted between his shoulders. He attached his own EVA pack, then adjusted the right arm he had dented earlier against the console.

Lars checked the Ragnarrök.

Grim watched.

"You expect to shoot the invisible station?"

"No."

"Then why bring the weapon?"

"Comfort."

"For you or the station?"

"Depends how rude it is."

The outer bay opened.

They launched into vacuum.

The Fastulv hung behind them, ugly and loyal, station lights reflected faintly along its patched hull. Ahead, the dark object distorted the universe.

Small ion pulses carried Lars and Grim toward it.

Distance became hard to judge. The surface seemed near, then far, then beside them. Lars's suit lidar refused to agree with itself. His eyes did better. He followed the pale blue pulses, not because they marked a path, but because something in the rhythm suggested where not to go.

"I dislike this," Grim said over the suit channel.

"You dislike many things."

"This is a mathematically supported dislike."

"Better than your emotional one?"

"More expensive."

They reached the hull.

Lars extended one boot. The magnetic sole clicked and failed to grip. He bounced gently backward, corrected with a wrist pulse, and touched the surface with one gloved hand.

The suit reported no contact.

His hand felt texture anyway.

Not rough. Not smooth. Responsive.

Like touching a material that was waiting to decide what kind of material it should be.

"That is rude," Grim said.

"The station?"

"Physics."

They moved along the hull, using tiny protrusions Lars could feel more than see. Fifteen meters to port, a faint rectangular pattern brightened in the strange blue.

"There," Grim said. "Possible interface."

"Possible trap."

"You said that with enthusiasm."

"I contain multitudes."

The rectangle sat within a wider halo of pale blue emission. Lars's suit sensors showed almost nothing. His skin prickled under the gloves.

"What can go wrong?" he said.

"I have prepared a list."

Lars pushed himself into the halo.

The scan hit him in less than a microsecond.

His suit did not detect it. His body did. A cold, precise force passed through his bones, blood, nerves, and skull. Not destructive. Evaluative. It ran through him with the intimacy of a medical procedure and the speed of a weapons lock.

His hands tingled.

The back of his neck burned.

A square lit at his right side, bright enough in the impossible wavelength that even Grim's recalibrated optics flared.

"Lars," Grim said. "Do not put your hand there."

Lars put his hand there.

"You put your hand there."

"I noticed."

The pull returned, this time inside his mind.

Not thought reading. That was too crude a phrase. It was pattern comparison. Neural architecture. Blood chemistry. Unknown machines in his bloodstream responding before he knew they were there.

Lars tried to pull away.

He could not.

The scan deepened.

Images sparked behind his eyes: a child running along a white corridor; a blue-white man laughing; a woman's hand resting on a control surface; seven points of light arranged around a dark center; a word he could not hear without pain.

Then it vanished.

Lars gasped.

The rectangle dissolved.

The hull opened.

No seam. No hinges. The material simply chose to be a doorway.

A voice spoke through the suit channel, through the hull, through the bones of his inner ear, and through some private channel he did not want to acknowledge.

The language was unknown.

He understood it anyway.

Welcome home.

Lars froze.

Grim's voice came tight over the comm. "Translation?"

"It said..." Lars swallowed. "It said welcome."

"Only welcome?"

"Close enough."

A field caught them both and drew them inside.

The doorway sealed behind them with silent speed. They entered a vast airlock lined in space-gray material. Warm light appeared in long bands along the walls and ceiling. Artificial gravity rotated orientation so smoothly that one wall became the floor before Lars's inner ear could complain.

They landed with soft thuds.

Air hissed in.

The suit confirmed pressure, temperature, oxygen, trace noble gases, and no identifiable pathogens. The air composition was almost Earth-normal, but cleaner than any station air Lars had ever breathed. Not sterile. Balanced.

The outer door vanished behind them.

Not closed.

Vanished.

"Nice door," Grim said.

His voice was quiet.

That worried Lars more than jokes would have.

Lars removed his helmet.

The air smelled faintly of cold stone, metal after rain, and something green.

A memory stirred.

He killed it before it could stand up.

"Keep recording," he said.

"I began recording before you touched the ancient brain-scanning murder square."

"Good."

"I also began drafting a will."

"You don't own anything."

"I own opinions."

"No one wants those."

They walked thirty meters to a wall console. It had no buttons. No screen in the ordinary sense. When Lars approached, symbols surfaced in pale green and blue layers, some visible, some sitting above visibility like weight behind the eyes.

Grim adjusted his optics again.

"This interface is encoded partly outside standard human visual range."

"I can see it."

"Yes," Grim said. "That is increasingly difficult to ignore."

Lars reached toward the symbols, then stopped.

He had no idea what they meant.

His hand moved anyway.

One sequence. Then another. The console warmed under his fingers. A headache bloomed, but with it came certainty. Not memory exactly. Procedure without story. Skill without explanation.

A rectangular opening formed in the wall to the right.

Grim stared.

"Have you studied ancient alien access systems?"

"I read a lot."

"That answer is deteriorating."

"Still useful."

They stepped through.

The next chamber was enormous.

The light bands along the walls barely reached the ceiling. Their footsteps echoed into distance. The floor was seamless black, polished without reflection. The air held no dust. No decay. No sense of abandonment, despite the impossible age implied by the technology.

Lars walked toward the center.

Two hundred meters in, he hit something.

His forehead made contact with an invisible surface.

"Ow."

Grim stopped just in time.

Lars lifted both hands. There was a ship in front of them.

He could feel the hull, but barely see it. The craft bent light around itself so perfectly that the far wall appeared through it with only slight distortion. Its surface was cool, continuous, and immense. Not a fighter. Not a shuttle. Something larger. A vessel built for distances that made normal navigation look parochial.

"It's a ship," Lars said.

"You are doing that thing again."

"What thing?"

"Knowing."

Lars ignored him and felt along the invisible hull.

"If there is a personnel lock, it will be forward of the midline, offset along the port curvature. Cargo access below, maybe aft, but this shape suggests internal modular bays. The crew lock should be..." He moved three steps. "Here."

A pale blue rectangle appeared under his palm.

Grim applauded once, the sound ringing through the vast hangar.

"Wonderful. Either you are the luckiest idiot in the galaxy or we are in serious trouble."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

Lars placed his palm in the rectangle.

The scan returned.

This time he was ready.

It did not help.

Images came harder: a boy standing beside a tall blue-white man; a woman with eyes too bright to be merely human; a fleet burning in silence; a station hidden inside folded spacetime; a name spoken over and over until it became impossible to bear.

Laurdinn.

Not Lars.

Laurdinn.

The headache became a blade.

The ship opened.

Lars staggered.

Grim caught him.

"We are leaving," Grim said.

"No."

"Your neurological readings just performed a small opera."

"No."

"Lars."

Lars looked through the open lock into the invisible ship.

Light came on inside, one band at a time, waiting for him.

He did not know this place.

He knew exactly where to go.

"We keep moving," he said.

Grim's grip tightened on his arm.

"Toward what?"

Lars stepped through the lock.

The answer came from somewhere deeper in the station, carried by no sound at all.

Not a ghost.

Not a vision.

A system recognizing a missing component.

"Home," Lars said, and hated the word as soon as it left him.

## Chapter 4: The Sea's Last Song

The invisible ship accepted him like a machine with excellent memory and no concern for his comfort.

The inner lock opened into a corridor wide enough for cargo, troops, or someone with a dramatic sense of personal importance. Light woke ahead in clean bands, pale blue at the edges, warm white through the center, never too bright, never too dim. The walls were space-gray with black inset lines that were not decorative. Lars could feel them as data channels, structural stress paths, access seams, or all three.

Grim stepped in behind him, one hand hovering near Lars's shoulder as if deciding whether to drag him back out.

"I am registering active systems," Grim said. "Power grid stable. Atmosphere stable. Gravity stable. No dust. No corrosion. No microbial bloom. Either this ship was sealed yesterday or someone here has humiliated entropy."

"Entropy needed humbling."

"That sounds like something you would put on a mug."

The corridor widened into a bridge.

Not a cockpit. Not even a command deck in the Aphex sense, with heroic chairs and too many screens. This was a quiet oval chamber, built around a central standing console and a shallow ring of workstations grown from the floor. The forward wall was black, glossy, and blank until Lars crossed the threshold.

Then the galaxy appeared.

Not a camera feed. Not a projection. A spatial reconstruction unfolded across the wall and into the room, layered with mass shadows, radiation flows, local curvature, and possible vectors. The Rim hung behind them. The Fastulv showed as a small tagged outline clamped outside the hidden structure. Beyond, the KBC Void expanded as a measured absence.

Grim made a small sound.

"I dislike admitting this," he said, "but that is better than our tactical display."

"Our tactical display has personality."

"It has latency."

A voice spoke from everywhere at once, in flawless Galactic Swedish.

"Welcome back, Captain Laurðinn. System diagnostics confirm all primary and secondary functions operating within nominal parameters. No critical deviations registered during command absence. Storage integrity remains intact. Redundant systems are in standby. Continuity simulation confirms three hundred and fifteen years, seven months, twelve days of uninterrupted watch-state operation. Command access reactivated. It is an honor to serve again."

Lars stared at the empty bridge.

Grim slowly turned his head toward him.

"Captain Laurðinn."

"Shut up."

"I have not yet begun."

"Begin later."

Lars touched the central console. It shaped itself under his palm, not soft, not liquid, but obedient in a way metal had no right to be. A set of symbols rose beneath his hand. He understood none of them. His fingers knew several.

The ship responded with a soft tone.

The headache remained, but it did not spike. That was worse. It meant he could keep going.

"Ship," Lars said, then stopped because speaking to an unknown ancient warship while pretending not to panic was harder than it looked. "Identify yourself."

A pause, almost human in timing.

"I am Havets Sista Sång."

Grim's optical lenses brightened. "The Sea's Last Song. That is unnecessarily beautiful."

"Aphex names guns Ragnarrök," Lars said. "Everyone has hobbies."

Lars removed his hand from the console.

"Absolutely not."

"Recommendation logged."

"Do not log my medical cowardice."

"Logged under behavioral continuity."

Grim made a delighted metallic noise.

Lars pointed at him. "No."

"You thought loudly."

"You heard?"

Lars opened his mouth and closed it again.

The bridge became very quiet.

Grim's amusement disappeared.

"Lars."

"I don't want to discuss it on the haunted nostalgia ship."

"This ship is not haunted."

"Good, then it won't mind."

He turned away from the console. The walls had begun to display status layers in the strange blue-white emission. Grim adjusted his optics again and muttered about spectrum compensation. Lars could see the data without effort. That, too, was a problem for later.

"Havets Sista Sång," Grim said, with the careful politeness he used for powerful systems, expensive kitchens, and armed strangers. "Can you provide station map access?"

"Guest entity," Grim repeated.

"Don't get sentimental," Lars said.

"I am deciding whether to be offended."

"Pick quickly."

The map unfolded. It was a folded arcology: a vast complex suspended inside a spacetime bubble, parts of it geometrically adjacent despite being separated by impossible external distances. Hangars, habitat rings, manufacturing cores, data vaults, energy loops, hydroponic cylinders, medical bays, memory archives, and regions the map refused to name.

Several sectors glowed green.

Many glowed muted amber.

One deep internal sector pulsed red.

Lars felt that red pulse in his teeth.

"What is that?" he asked.

"Central power distribution auxiliary chamber," the ship replied. "Containment anomaly. Maintenance delegation incomplete."

"How incomplete?"

"Forty-nine years, two months, nine days."

Grim's head snapped toward the map. "There has been an unresolved containment anomaly for forty-nine years?"

"Correct."

"Robotic maintenance units failed within affected chamber. Drone attempts failed. Biological access restricted. Guardian Valdrun immobilized within chamber. External signalling suppressed under Alpha Fourteen watch-state protocol."

The words struck Lars unevenly.

Guardian.

Valdrun.

Alpha Fourteen.

Each one found a locked door inside him and hit it with a hammer.

Pain rose up his neck.

He grabbed the console edge.

Grim stepped in close. "Stop listening."

"That's hard when the big ship keeps talking."

"Ship, reduce sensitive terminology."

"Acknowledged."

"Don't order my weird ship around."

"Someone has to."

The map shifted, replacing labels with neutral icons. The pain lessened by one degree.

Lars breathed through his nose. "This Valdrun is alive?"

"Yes," said Havets Sista Sång.

"Human?"

"No."

"That's a useful answer."

"Entity classification restricted by captain medical lock."

Grim looked at Lars. "Your own brain is apparently censoring the ship."

"My brain is overachieving today."

"Can we reach the chamber without dying?"

"Route available," the ship said. "Recommend respiratory protection, radiation shielding, and no robotic entry beyond safety threshold."

Grim went still.

"No robotic entry."

"Correct. Prior machine failures indicate localized field effects damaging synthetic cognition, servo control, and non-biological quantum substrates."

Lars looked at Grim's chest, where the biological quantum core sat behind layered armor.

"You stay at the threshold."

"No."

"That wasn't a vote."

"You are neurologically unstable, recently attacked, and following instructions from a ship that thinks your name is Laurdinn."

"Laurðinn," Lars corrected automatically.

They stared at each other.

"Interesting," Grim said.

"I hate this place."

"You just corrected the pronunciation."

"I hate this place accurately."

They left the bridge.

Havets Sista Sång opened a side passage without a seam. They passed through the hangar again, the invisible ship fading behind them until only its distortion remained. Lars forced himself not to look back. The station beyond the hangar unfolded in long corridors of gray-white material, rounded corners, hidden lighting, and interfaces that surfaced only as they approached.

Every design choice had restraint.

No banners. No gold. No monumental statues. No childish empire language carved into bulkheads.

It was all function, but function with taste so precise it became almost arrogant.

Grim noticed too.

"If Apple had a secret civilization and a thousand years of better engineers," he said, "it might produce a bad imitation of this corridor."

"That's a sentence with legal consequences."

"Only if Apple survives in this jurisdiction."

They reached a long equatorial passage. A narrow window ran along the right wall, rectangular with rounded corners. Outside, the Milky Way glittered with uncomfortable beauty. Because of the spacetime bubble, the view was wrong. Stars shifted as Lars walked, not with parallax but with folded perspective, as if the station were looking at the galaxy from several nearby positions at once.

On the left, cross-corridors and wall consoles passed in measured intervals.

Lars pointed. "We turn here."

"Map says straight for another seventy meters."

"Then the map is being polite."

He turned left.

Grim checked the projection. The route recalculated and turned left.

"I am adding that to the folder," Grim said.

"There is no folder."

"There are several folders."

They had gone perhaps a hundred meters inward when a sphere floated toward them from the distance.

It was small, maybe half a meter across, space-gray and black with pale blue symbols moving across its surface. No visible thrusters. No sound. It slowed as it approached.

Lars pulled the Ragnarrök from his shoulder and unlocked the safety.

Grim shifted behind him. "Probably a maintenance drone."

"Probably is a word that gets people shot."

"Usually by you."

The sphere stopped five meters away.

Symbols flickered across its face, then reorganized into Galactic Swedish.

"Captain Laurðinn," it said. "You have returned. Assistance required. Time margin unacceptable. Follow."

Then it turned and accelerated back the way it had come.

Lars and Grim looked at each other.

"Friendly maintenance drone," Grim said.

"Bossy maintenance drone."

Lars secured the Ragnarrök and ran.

The sphere led them through two corridors, a descending lift with no visible motion, and a lock chamber whose doors formed and unformed around them. Lars tried to keep track of direction, then gave up. The station's interior geometry cheated. The only honest things were his headache and Grim's increasingly concerned silence.

"Name and function," Lars called after the sphere.

"Designation: Ödesvaka," it replied without slowing. "Function: watch compliance and continuity under Alpha Fourteen watch-state. Current priority: prevent guardian termination."

The pain jabbed again at the protocol name, but the drone was already veering through a wide portal to the right.

They entered a large chamber.

The first thing Lars noticed was the smell.

Burned insulation. Ozone. Hot metal. Something chemical and bitter that his suit filters should have removed but did not. The lighting flickered in weak bands along the walls. Enormous machines stood in two rows, their surfaces mostly intact, but several panels had blown open like peeled fruit.

The second thing he noticed was the bodies.

Not biological bodies. Robots. Drones. Maintenance units. At least ten lay twisted in a line from the entrance toward the left machine bank. Five spherical drones were crushed or burned mid-path, as if they had simply fallen from the air and died.

Grim stopped at the threshold.

His left hand twitched.

Then twitched again.

"I am receiving control noise," he said.

"Back up."

"I can compensate."

"Back up."

This time Lars did not make it a joke.

Grim stepped back into the corridor.

Ödesvaka hovered beside him. "Guest entity should remain outside."

"Guest entity agrees under protest," Grim said.

Lars moved deeper into the chamber.

His boots struck something with a metallic clang. He looked down. A robot hand, severed cleanly at the wrist, fingers still curled around a tool. Beyond it, more wreckage led toward the right-side machine bank.

There, under a collapsed power distributor the size of a small ground vehicle, lay a humanoid figure.

At first Lars thought it was an android.

Then the figure turned its head.

It had a human face, mostly. Tall-boned, blue-pale, with skin that looked too cool and too strong. One side of the skull and neck showed integrated material that was not prosthetic in any ordinary sense. Muscle, synthetic structure, and something like living metal disappeared under torn clothing and a half-crushed torso harness. Long arms. Broad shoulders. A face like a Viking statue designed by a medical engineer with unresolved issues.

The figure saw Lars and smiled.

"Took you long enough," he said.

His voice was rough, exhausted, and amused.

Lars stopped.

Something in him recognized the tone before the face.

Pain struck, then retreated as if the locked doors inside him had decided this one could stay partly open.

"Do I know you?" Lars asked.

"Yes."

"Do I want the full answer?"

"Not yet."

"Good. I am Lars."

"I know."

"Everyone keeps saying that today."

"Valdrun," the trapped man said.

From the corridor Grim said, "I am choosing not to be insulted by the word eats."

"Wise robot," Valdrun said.

Grim paused. "I may choose to be slightly flattered."

Valdrun closed his eyes for a second, gathering strength. "The distributor weighs about two metric tons. The chamber can vent enough contamination for you to work, but not enough for me to lift it. My left-side actuators are pinned. Biological repair systems are keeping me alive. They have been doing so for forty-nine years."

Lars looked at the collapsed structure.

Two tons. Bad angle. Damaged floor anchor. Heat distortion in the surrounding frame. No heavy robot could enter. Drones had failed. Valdrun could not free himself without shifting the distributor through his own torso.

The solution began forming.

Not enough tools here. Need a pulley, cable, overhead beam, remote counterforce from outside the field. Service void above the ceiling. Work robot outside the threshold. Redirect load through a mechanical advantage system. Use station material if it behaved.

Lars hated how quickly it arrived.

"We need cable, pulleys, a structural anchor above you, and a big stupid machine that can pull from outside the chamber."

Valdrun's smile widened. "Still you."

"Stop recognizing me. It is giving me a headache."

"Workshop outside," Valdrun said. "Right of the entrance. Hidden service access in the rear wall. Ödesvaka can open it."

Lars turned toward the drone. "Can you?"

"Yes."

"Why didn't anyone do this earlier?"

"Prior attempts used autonomous maintenance units. Units failed. Guardian unable to authorize biological-risk override without captain command confirmation."

"That is a terrible protocol."

Ödesvaka's surface symbols flickered. "Logged."

"Don't log sarcasm."

"Logged."

Grim made a soft sound from the corridor.

"I like this drone."

Lars ran to the workshop.

The door formed when he reached for it. Inside, tools waited in recessed walls, arranged with insulting calm. Cable spools. Composite struts. Pulley blocks. Field clamps. Manual tools that looked simple until Lars picked one up and felt it adapt to his grip.

He grabbed what he needed and what his hands insisted he needed. Grim stayed just outside the threshold, taking parts as Lars shoved them toward him.

"Find us a work robot that can pull three tons from outside the danger zone," Lars said.

"Three?"

"Two-ton load, bad angle, unknown friction, and I don't trust ancient ceiling beams."

"Sensible."

"Don't sound surprised."

Ödesvaka projected a small map. "Heavy manipulator unit available in adjacent logistics bay. Remote control possible from corridor."

"Good. Wake it."

"It has been awake."

"Then tell it to stop being lazy."

"Instruction translated."

Lars found the hidden service access in the workshop wall because his left hand pressed a blank surface before his conscious mind caught up. A panel vanished. Behind it, a narrow maintenance shaft climbed into darkness.

"Of course," he muttered.

He hauled the pulley assembly up into the shaft.

Heat increased as he crawled above the chamber. The air tasted metallic. His suit filters complained. He reached the approximate position over Valdrun by counting steps, structure intervals, and the vibration pattern from the damaged distributor below.

"Ödesvaka," he called over the comm. "Open a ceiling panel one meter square above my position."

"Place hand on surface."

"Everything here is hands with you people."

He pressed his palm down.

A square of ceiling vanished.

Below, the chamber opened beneath him: machines, wreckage, Grim at the safe threshold, Valdrun pinned under the distributor and looking up with the maddening calm of someone who had waited half a century and could wait another five minutes just to be annoying.

"Nice of you to drop by," Valdrun called.

"I'm charging hourly."

"You always did."

The headache flared.

"No past-tense jokes."

"Understood."

Lars looped the cable around the hidden beam, locked the first pulley, tested the anchor, hated the angle, added a second pulley, then a third because ancient arrogance did not repeal load physics. Sweat gathered under his suit collar. His hands felt weak. That was new.

The chemical field from below climbed into him despite the filters.

His muscles trembled.

He lowered the cable through the ceiling gap.

Grim took it from below with a manipulator pole, staying outside the chamber. The heavy work robot arrived in the corridor like a walking loading crane, squat and ugly, with grippers built for moving station machinery. It clamped the cable.

Lars crawled back through the service shaft, too fast and too clumsy. Twice his shoulder hit the wall. Once his vision narrowed to a gray tunnel.

By the time he reached the chamber floor again, his legs felt wrong.

"You look terrible," Grim said.

"Not a joke moment."

"Then why are you here?"

Lars staggered to the distributor and threaded the lower cable around its frame. The surface was hot through his gloves. Not burning. Not quite. The chamber smell thickened. His suit alarm flashed yellow, then orange.

Valdrun watched him closely.

"It affects you too."

"Everything affects me. I'm sensitive."

"Lars."

The name landed differently from him.

Gentler.

Older.

Lars tightened the final lock and stepped back.

"Pull slowly," he said.

Grim relayed the command. The work robot outside the field engaged.

The cable tightened.

The overhead beam groaned.

"Stop," Lars said.

The pull stopped.

He recalculated. The first angle would drag the distributor sideways across Valdrun's pinned actuator column and probably tear something important. He needed lift first, then translation.

He moved to the left, attached a secondary guide line, and nearly fell.

Grim took one step into the chamber.

"Stay back," Lars snapped.

Grim froze.

"I'm fine."

"You are swaying."

"That's tactical."

"It is not."

Lars wrapped the guide line around a floor anchor that surfaced when he kicked the right panel. Again, the station obeyed him before he understood why.

"Now," he said. "Ten centimeters lift. No more."

The robot pulled.

The distributor rose with a sound like a mountain changing its mind.

Valdrun inhaled sharply.

"Hold," Lars said.

The cable held.

"Guide line left. Slow."

Grim controlled the secondary winch. The distributor shifted away from Valdrun's torso by one centimeter, then three, then six.

The overhead beam screamed.

"Stop."

Lars stumbled forward, grabbed Valdrun under one arm, and pulled.

Valdrun did not move.

"You weigh a lot."

"Rude."

"Accurate."

Valdrun's free hand dug into the floor. His damaged side sparked once, then locked. Lars pulled again. Something in his own shoulders burned. Not muscle. Deeper. As if the body had reserves he had never been shown and was angry about being asked.

Valdrun slid half a meter.

"Again," Lars gasped.

The distributor shifted.

Valdrun pushed.

Lars pulled.

They came free together.

"Release load," Lars said.

The work robot eased the distributor down. It struck the floor with a deep metallic boom.

For one second, everything held.

Then Lars's vision folded inward.

He heard Grim shout his name.

He heard Valdrun say something in a language that made the locked places in him blaze.

He heard Ödesvaka report medical emergency with obscene calm.

Then the chamber, the station, and the impossible blue light went away.

## Chapter 5: The Helmet

Something beeped.

Lars disliked it immediately.

Beep.

Beep.

Beep.

The sound had the personality of hospital equipment: smug, repetitive, convinced of its own importance. He tried to open his eyes and discovered someone had replaced reality with a blurred white rectangle.

Voices moved around him.

One was Grim.

One was deeper, rougher, unfamiliar and familiar at the same time.

One was a machine that sounded like it would report a fire in the same tone it used to announce soup.

Lars closed his eyes again.

This proved to be a mistake because the beeping remained.

The second time he woke, the world had improved enough to include edges.

He lay in a medical bed in a white room with black equipment columns and pale blue diagnostic layers moving silently above him. The walls were smooth, rounded, and much too clean. No stains. No cracked paneling. No old Rim smell. The air was perfect. Lars distrusted perfect air.

Grim sat beside the bed holding a cup of coffee.

Not drinking it.

Holding it like a threat.

"If that is for me," Lars said, "you are forgiven for whatever expression you are making."

Grim's head tilted. "You cannot see my expression."

"I can hear it."

"You collapsed."

"People do that."

"For forty-eight hours?"

Lars blinked.

"That seems excessive."

"We had to keep you sedated," said the rough voice.

Valdrun stood near the foot of the bed.

Free, now. Tall. Very tall. Blue-pale skin, long limbs, broad shoulders, and visible integrated structure along his neck and left side where biological tissue met engineered material without apology. Not a robot. Not a man in the ordinary sense. Something half human, half machine, and entirely comfortable being terrifying in a quiet room.

He looked like a Viking who had survived a starship factory accident.

"You," Lars said.

"Me."

"You were under a power thing."

"Power distributor."

"I rescued you."

"You did."

"Good."

Valdrun smiled.

Grim handed Lars the coffee.

Lars took it with both hands. His fingers trembled slightly. He pretended they did not.

"What hit me?" he asked.

"Radiation, chemical contamination, local field interference, and your own nanomedical systems attempting to reconcile a neurological lock with emergency repair demands," Valdrun said.

Lars lowered the cup.

"My what?"

Pain began at the base of his neck.

Not a warning this time.

A guillotine being raised.

Grim leaned forward. "Careful."

Valdrun nodded once. "Right. We do not push through the lock verbally."

"I would appreciate everyone not discussing my body like it has unauthorized aftermarket parts."

Grim and Valdrun exchanged a look.

Lars pointed at them.

"No meaningful looks."

"We are in a room full of meaning."

"Then look at the wall."

Ödesvaka floated near the ceiling, half in shadow, symbols moving across its spherical body.

"Captain medical stability at seventy-two percent," the drone announced.

"Do not call me captain."

"Command title suppressed."

"Thank you."

"Suppression logged."

"Everything is logged with you."

"Correct."

Lars drank coffee and tried to assemble the last clear memory.

Then nothing.

"Why did the chamber affect Grim faster than me?" Lars asked.

Grim answered before Valdrun could. "The field damaged synthetic control loops and non-biological quantum substrates. My biological core tolerated the edge, but my motor coordination started desynchronizing. Deeper in, I would likely have become modern sculpture."

"Bad sculpture."

"Probably."

Valdrun sat in the chair opposite Grim. The chair shaped itself to him. Lars noticed that and tried not to think about how natural it looked.

"The accident began forty-nine years ago," Valdrun said. "A distributor failure in the auxiliary chamber. It should have been routine. A local mechanical collapse, toxic outgassing from damaged insulation layers, and a field harmonic from the power grid that disabled maintenance robotics. I was able to prevent cascade failure, but the distributor pinned me before I could clear the chamber."

"Forty-nine years under a machine."

"Yes."

"That sounds boring."

"Deeply."

"Why didn't you signal someone?"

"I did. Locally. The station could not risk external transmission under watch-state. Too many hostile receivers near the galactic rim. Radio would spread and die after a few light-years. Tightbeam paths were blocked or unsafe. The only vessel in this facility able to cross the required distance quietly was locked under your command envelope."

"My command envelope."

"Yes."

Pain pressed.

Lars took more coffee.

"Convenient."

"Inconvenient for me."

"Fair."

Valdrun leaned forward. "Your arrival was not random."

Lars raised a hand. "If you are about to say destiny, I will throw this very expensive coffee at you."

"Good. Destiny is a lazy word."

Lars lowered his hand slightly.

"Continue."

"There are systems that can bias probability without controlling choice. Signals too weak to be orders. Pattern nudges. Cognitive resonance through nanoscale communication, memory locks, old navigation habits, stress responses. You felt urgency because parts of you had enough information to know where to go, but not enough permission to explain why."

That was better.

Still horrible.

But better.

"So not fate," Lars said.

"No. Engineering."

"I hate when engineering does psychology."

"It hates you back," Grim said.

Lars looked between them. "And the headache?"

Valdrun's expression softened.

"A safeguard. You were not meant to think directly about certain memories until you returned to a proper node."

"Hypnosis."

The word came from Lars's own mouth.

The room froze.

The pain rose, then stopped.

Not gone.

Paused.

Valdrun watched him carefully.

"Yes."

Lars expected the migraine to crush him.

It did not.

Instead a thin line of memory opened: a room like this one, but larger; seven adults standing in a circle; a woman with dark hair and star-bright eyes; someone laughing because the plan was both brilliant and appalling.

Then the line closed.

Lars exhaled slowly.

"I don't want the whole explanation yet."

"Wise," Valdrun said.

"I want the practical version."

Valdrun stood and crossed to a device in the center of the medical room. It had been folded into the floor when Lars first woke. Now it rose in three clean arcs: a chair, a halo-like frame, and a set of optical emitters that glowed in wavelengths that made Grim adjust his eyes again.

"This can remove the lock in stages," Valdrun said. "Not all at once. Your body just survived a bad interaction between the lock and emergency repair systems. If we leave it as is, the next shock could kill you or scramble memory access worse than death."

"Worse than death is a strong sales pitch."

"You have died before."

Silence.

Grim stopped moving.

Lars looked at Valdrun.

"That was not the practical version."

"No," Valdrun said. "But it is the part that explains why I am not asking."

The room seemed too bright.

Lars's hands tightened around the coffee cup until the material warned him with a soft tone.

"How many times?"

"Not now."

"You just dropped dead-before on me and now you're rationing?"

"Yes."

Lars almost laughed. It came out wrong.

Grim spoke quietly. "Lars."

"I know."

He swung his legs over the side of the bed. The room tilted. He waited until it stopped.

"If this chair turns me into a religious person, I am blaming both of you."

"It will not," Valdrun said.

"If I start speaking in riddles?"

"We sedate you."

"Good protocol."

He stood, took one step, and nearly sat down by accident. Grim moved to help, then stopped when Lars glared.

"I am fine."

"You are seventy-two percent stable," Ödesvaka said.

"No one asked the ball."

"Logged."

Valdrun helped him into the chair without making it look like help, which Lars appreciated and resented. The material adjusted around his back. The halo lowered over his head but did not touch him. Pale blue patterns formed in the air, layered with ultraviolet structures Grim could barely track and Lars could see too well.

"Look at the center," Valdrun said. "Do not chase memories. Let the system map the lock first."

"How does it work?"

"Neural phase alignment, memory index verification, controlled suggestion reversal, nanomedical synchronization."

"That sounded almost respectable."

"It is respectable."

"Then why does it look like a dentist chair designed by a cult?"

Grim said, "Because you are frightened."

Lars looked at him.

Grim did not soften it.

For once, Lars did not answer.

The pattern began to move.

At first it was only light. Then timing. Then compression. The emitters pulsed in ratios that did not repeat but never became random. His breathing tried to follow them. His pulse corrected. Somewhere in his blood, systems he did not remember owning answered.

The headache changed shape.

It had been a wall.

Now it became a door.

The first thing through was not grand.

Not Lyra.

Not war.

Not ancient secrets.

It was a smell.

Fresh bread in a station corridor.

Someone's hand ruffling his hair.

A voice: "Laurðinn, if you rewire the nursery lights again, at least make them stop blinking before Valdrun sees."

Then another memory slammed into place.

He was small, running along a white corridor on Station Zero.

He was not from Stockholm.

He had never been from Stockholm.

His eyes opened.

The medical room returned.

Valdrun stood beside the chair, face grave.

Grim's metal hands were clenched.

Ödesvaka hovered motionless.

Lars tried to speak.

For once, no joke arrived first.

"Station Zero," he said.

Valdrun nodded.

"Your birthplace."

Lars looked at his hands.

They were still his hands.

That seemed unlikely.

"How old am I?"

Valdrun did not look away.

"Six hundred and forty-one."

The answer passed through Lars like cold water.

Somewhere far behind him, a fashionable idiot with yellow sunglasses and too much money fell silent.

The man in the chair remained.

He took one breath.

Then another.

"I need," Lars said slowly, "a much stronger coffee."

Grim made a sound that was almost relief.

Valdrun smiled.

"That, at least, I remember."

## Chapter 6: Stronger Coffee

The stronger coffee arrived in a cup that looked too elegant for what it contained.

Lars took one careful sip and immediately forgave the cup.

"That," he said, "could restart a dead moon."

"It has restarted you before," Valdrun said.

Lars held up one finger. "We are limiting the amount of before in this conversation."

"Reasonable."

"Also the amount of dead."

"Less reasonable, but noted."

Grim stood near the medical room's wall display with four diagnostic layers open in front of him. Station Zero had given him access to Lars's live medical telemetry after Valdrun authorized it and Lars complained for six straight minutes. Grim had then spent three more minutes complaining about the interface being too good.

The display showed Lars as a translucent figure surrounded by cellular activity, neural synchronization bands, blood chemistry maps, and several layers Grim had labeled unacceptable weirdness.

"I can see that label," Lars said.

"It is accurate."

"Medical terminology has collapsed since I was thirty."

Valdrun folded his arms. "You were never thirty."

Lars looked at him.

Valdrun sighed. "You believed you were around thirty."

"Your current cover identity was designed to pass casual scrutiny. Birth records, education fragments, social media history, financial trails, medical records. Stockholm was useful because your surface accent and habits already leaned Swedish."

"Surface accent?"

"You were raised with several languages here. Galactic Swedish became dominant later, for obvious historical reasons."

"I remember Stockholm."

"You remember inserted connective tissue. Not all of it is false. Some is drawn from actual stays on Earth and in Swedish habitats. The lock did not simply erase and replace. It compressed, braided, and redirected."

Lars drank coffee.

That was better than answering.

The medical chair had given him one clean fact and a thousand dirty implications. Six hundred and forty-one. Born on Station Zero. Not from Stockholm. His name had been Laurðinn before it became Lars Odin, which sounded like someone had taken an old thing and filed the dangerous edges off.

He did not feel six hundred and forty-one.

He felt tired, hungry, irritated, and possibly under-caffeinated.

Apparently some truths lacked theatrical ambition.

"How much is still locked?" Grim asked.

Valdrun looked to the diagnostic display. "Most of the autobiographical chain. The first stage restored identity anchors: birthplace, age, primary name recognition, station familiarity, and enough medical access to stop his own systems fighting the lock."

"So I know who I am," Lars said, "but not what the idiot did."

"A fair summary."

Valdrun crossed to the wall and opened a new display. It showed Station Zero in layered geometry: hangars, medical rings, habitat districts, manufacturing zones, archives, power loops, hidden docks, and the damaged auxiliary chamber now marked amber instead of red.

"This place," Valdrun said, "is Arkology Zero. Station Zero is the old public translation. It sits a few light-years into the KBC Void, but not in ordinary space the way The Rim does. The outer approach you saw was only the coupling surface. The real structure is folded inside a spacetime bubble."

"That explains the rude windows."

"It explains why external observers have never mapped us, why conventional signals fail, and why only certain ships can enter without tearing themselves into interesting vapor."

Lars looked at the map.

It was too large. Not city-large. Civilization-large. Not in population now, maybe, but in intent. A place built by people who expected time to be an engineering parameter.

"How many are here?"

Valdrun's face changed.

Not much.

Enough.

"Awake? Now? Me, Ödesvaka, the station intelligences, and some limited biological cultures in preservation. You and Grim arrived from outside. Most residential sectors are empty or in conservation."

"Empty because everyone left?"

"Because everyone hid."

The word hid did not trigger the old migraine.

That almost made it worse. The lock had stopped punishing him, which meant the words could get all the way in.

Lars set down the coffee.

"Practical version."

Not painfully.

It rang instead.

Far away.

"Alive?"

"Me."

That was one of the hardest parts.

Being known by a stranger.

Återviens.

## Chapter 7: Pluto Protocol 9

The archive lay below the medical ring and above the memory vault, which was the kind of sentence Lars preferred not to examine.

They reached it through a lift that did not move so much as renegotiate location. One moment the door sealed in the medical corridor. The next it opened into a chamber shaped like a shallow amphitheater, all black glass, white stone, space-gray ribs, and pale blue filaments suspended in the air like frozen rain.

There were no shelves.

No drives.

No dramatic crystal library.

Just the chamber, the filaments, and a central table grown from the floor.

"Where is the archive?" Grim asked.

Valdrun pointed around them. "Here."

"I was afraid of that."

The filaments brightened as Lars stepped forward. Each one resolved into dense encoded structure: metadata, checksum trees, quantum error correction patterns, spectrum keys, access histories, emotional hazard ratings, medical warnings, and content maps. Most of it appeared in light no baseline human could see. The visible blue glow was only leakage.

Grim recalibrated his optics for the fifth time that day.

"I am beginning to resent eyes as a concept," he said.

"Install better ones," Lars said.

"I did."

"Install even better ones."

"That is always your answer."

"Because it is usually correct."

Lars stopped.

Pluto.

The word did not hurt. It pulled.

A cold world at the edge of the old Solar System. A buried object. A woman touching something she did not yet understand. The beginning, or one of the beginnings.

Valdrun brought up the metadata.

"Original capture: 2032, Pluto artifact zone, ISEA isolated module four. Transcoded and annotated several times. This copy was sealed into Station Zero's staged reactivation archive after Alpha Fourteen began."

"Approximately, depending on which clock standard you use."

"Let's not use the depressing one."

The chamber dimmed.

A holojournal formed above the table.

At first it looked like a weak blue ghost, exactly the sort of thing Lars did not want it to look like. Then his eyes adjusted and the sensor layers resolved: temperature noise, radiation scatter, camera correction, nanobot emission overlays, microtremor analysis, neural stress markers inferred later from posture and voice. Not a ghost. A recording, rebuilt with obsessive fidelity from multiple instruments and later annotated by people who distrusted single-source evidence.

Good.

He could work with that.

The image showed a small room under harsh light. Ice dust clung to the seams of equipment cases. A pressure door sat behind the subject, rimed white at the edges. Beyond a narrow observation slit lay a darkness that felt cold even in reconstruction.

Lyra Vargstjärna stood beside a workbench.

Lars had expected myth.

He got a tired scientist in a thermal suit with cropped dark hair, sharp cheekbones, and eyes that moved too quickly.

That was worse.

More real.

She looked into the recorder.

For a moment, nobody in the archive spoke.

Then the old recording began.

"Pluto artifact zone. Isolation module four. ISEA internal log. Lyra Vargstjärna recording. Timestamp: mission day... irrelevant. Local clocks have drifted twice since the last incident. Use the corrected packet header."

Grim leaned slightly toward Lars. "I like her."

"Shh."

The holojournal displayed a second layer beside her: microscopic footage, later reconstruction, particles moving through blood plasma.

"We found the first machines in my bloodstream thirty-seven hours later. I am using the word machines because nanobots is both imprecise and emotionally unhelpful. They did not behave like infection. They did not replicate blindly. They mapped. Waited. Adjusted."

She paused.

Her eyes flicked to something off-screen.

"I am stronger. Reflex tests are outside human range. Visual response has expanded into ultraviolet and adjacent bands. Radiation damage from yesterday's suit breach has repaired faster than current medicine can explain. Lars felt his own skin prickle.

Grim looked at him, but said nothing.

Lars stared at the recording.

His mouth had gone dry.

"Oh," Grim said softly.

"Shut up," Lars said.

Grim did.

The recording shifted to a data overlay: superconducting loops, quantum noise irregularities, correlations beyond known communication limits.

"The artifact is not just an object. It is a node. We thought the unknown civilization had left a machine on Pluto as a test or beacon. That may still be partly true. But the machines inside me are not merely transferring stored knowledge. They are attempting handshake with something larger."

Lyra smiled then, without humor.

"I hate that sentence. It sounds like religion. It is not. We have signal correlations, nonlocal timing anomalies, and enough reproducible data to make three physicists angry."

Lars exhaled.

"Thank you," he murmured.

Valdrun glanced at him.

Lyra looked down at her hands in the recording.

"The immediate problem is practical. Earth-side actors already know we found something. Corporate probes, state military assets, religious lunatics pretending to be environmentalists, and at least one private fleet are maneuvering toward Pluto. They do not understand what this is. Grim whispered, "Portal."

Valdrun did not correct him.

Lyra leaned closer to the recorder.

"If anyone is watching this centuries from now, do not assume we understood it. We did not. We were frightened, exhausted, and moving too fast because the alternative was letting idiots with flags and quarterly targets touch a million-year-old system with dirty hands."

Lars smiled despite himself.

Grim made a delighted sound.

The base cannot depend on one reactor, one food loop, one air plant, one authority chain, or one person who happens to be infected with alien machines and too tired to sleep."

Lyra's tired face hardened.

"For now this is not philosophy. It is plumbing. If one valve fails, we breathe. If one greenhouse dies, we eat. If I die, the others continue. If Earth cuts supply, Pluto does not. We can argue about grand principles when nothing is actively trying to kill us."

The recording ended.

The holojournal collapsed back into pale blue filaments.

The archive chamber returned.

For a while, nobody spoke.

Lars stared at the empty space above the table where Lyra had been.

Not a goddess.

Not a prophet.

A scientist on Pluto, tired and afraid and angry at idiots.

That, he trusted.

"She reminds me of you."

"That is deeply unfair to her."

"She also complained about French people under strain."

Valdrun rested both hands on the table. "That log is before Heliostat. Before the Kalari. Before the Rakh'Na attack in 2083. Before the word Återviens. It is not the doctrine. It is the strain that made the doctrine possible."

Lars looked at the filaments.

"Show me the later branch."

Valdrun studied him. "You just learned your age."

"Yes."

"You just watched the beginning of everything that made you possible."

"Also yes."

"You need rest."

"I need a plan."

Grim turned from the archive display. "For once, I agree with both of you, which is unpleasant."

Lars looked at him.

Grim lifted a metal finger. "You need rest. We need a plan. These are not mutually exclusive. I can run archive indexing while you perform the disgusting biological ritual called sleep."

"I dislike sleep."

"You dislike being unconscious. You just did forty-eight hours of that and still found time to complain."

"Valid."

Valdrun opened another layer of the archive. "The later branch contains Lyra's reactivation criteria. Threshold models. Guardian contact protocols. Known hidden nodes. Risk estimates for hostile acquisition of nanobot carriers."

"Black Hole B-7."

"Among others."

"Earth Reclaimers?"

"Syren Collective?"

Valdrun's face became grim.

"Worse than present."

The lock was no longer a wall or a door.

It was a room full of boxes, each labeled in a language he used to know.

That was enough for one day.

## Chapter 8: The Barge

Lars woke to wind.

This was unacceptable.

He opened one eye and found himself under a hard yellow sun, lying on a narrow bunk inside a wooden cabin that smelled of tar, damp rope, old coffee, and engine oil. Something rattled above him. Water slapped against a hull. A gull screamed with the confidence of an animal that had never paid rent.

For three seconds he was sure Station Zero had drowned.

Then the ceiling dissolved into a polite line of pale blue text.

SIMULATION CHAMBER 3. REST INTERVAL COMPLETE. NON-BEEPING WAKE PROTOCOL SUCCESSFUL.

Lars stared at it.

"Ödesvaka."

A small spherical drone hovered outside the open cabin door, backlit by sunlight.

"Yes."

"Why am I on a boat?"

"Rest environment selected from recovered comfort index."

"Comfort index."

"Correct."

"This boat is shaking."

"Historical watercraft did that."

"The sun is stabbing me."

"Historical suns did that."

"I asked for coffee staged at the bed."

A small hatch opened beside the bunk. A metal arm extended with a steaming cup.

Lars took it.

"You live," he said.

He sat up slowly. His body felt better. Not good. Better. The chemical weakness from the chamber had retreated into the background, and the strange internal hum of his repaired systems had steadied. His mind still felt overcrowded, but the old lock-trigger pain was gone. The forbidden words no longer struck him. They waited.

That was almost more frightening.

He stepped out of the cabin onto a barge in the middle of Mälaren.

Or a reconstruction of Mälaren. A very good one. The water glittered under the sun. Wind dragged small waves across the surface. The shoreline sat low and green in the distance. Rigging knocked against metal. The barge's engine vibrated through the deck with old industrial stubbornness.

Not a naval officer's hat.

A cheap white tourist captain's hat with gold plastic braid.

Lars stopped.

"No."

Grim turned. "Good morning."

"You look like you sell shrimp sandwiches to retirees."

"Is that prestigious?"

"No."

"Then it is disguise."

Lars looked at it.

"If that is medicine, I choose death."

"Breakfast," Valdrun said.

"Then I choose war."

Valdrun glanced around the barge. Wind tore at his dark coat. In the sunlight his blue-pale skin looked even less human, though the ridiculous setting somehow made him more believable. "Is this a memory?"

"Apparently."

"A good one?"

Lars watched the water move.

He remembered nothing specific. No childhood on Earth, no real barge trip, no single clean event. But the scene carried a composite comfort: yellow sunlight, engine vibration, cold water, coffee strong enough to make moral arguments. Maybe his cover life had borrowed from actual visits to Earth. Maybe his mind had chosen old Sweden as a quiet place to store things too large for sterile rooms.

"Good enough," he said.

They sat at a bolted metal table on deck. A planning model appeared above it, projected by the simulation rather than placed there. It showed the Milky Way as a slow-turning cloud of lights, colored not by politics but by risk: red for active predation, amber for systemic fragility, blue for hidden nodes, green for self-sufficient clusters.

There was too little green.

"I completed the initial archive index."

"While I slept?"

"While you performed tactical unconsciousness."

"Findings?"

Grim lifted one finger. "One. Lyra's reactivation criteria have crossed the first threshold. Possibly the second, depending on how pessimistic we are about Syren Collective fragmentation."

"Pessimism is just optimism with maintenance records," Lars said.

"Then threshold two."

Valdrun drank the gray liquid. Lars tried not to watch the lumps.

"Suspected carriers?"

"Likely hypnotized Aerits."

The word sat in the simulated sunlight.

No pain.

Only weight.

Lars nodded. "Three?"

"Three. Vigor sent a delayed packet after we left The Rim. He knows you entered the Void. He does not know whether you reached Station Zero. The packet contains sixteen jokes, two warnings, one invoice for elderflower cordial Grim added without authorization, and a guardian-authenticated offer of assistance."

"I authorized the cordial," Grim said.

"You are not fiscally sovereign."

"I have needs."

"You have taste receptors and poor impulse control."

Valdrun smiled into his horrible drink. "Vigor always did prefer cover identities with kitchens."

"That farm," Lars said. "It was not just a farm."

"No. It was a working example."

"Of Återviens."

He said the word for the first time.

It felt old in his mouth.

Not mystical. Not sacred. Dense. A technical term that had survived long enough to acquire gravity.

The planning model changed. Green points brightened where decentralized systems still functioned: independent arkologies, hidden farms, mobile habitats, autonomous AI clusters, small colonies with closed-loop energy and food systems, cultures that had remembered restraint without remembering who taught it.

"Lyra's term," Valdrun said. "Or one of them. It began as a resilience index. Then a design framework. Then a cultural infection, deliberately released in stories, engineering standards, rituals, jokes, old songs, school lessons, food systems, architecture, and boring maintenance manuals."

"The boring manuals survived best," Grim said.

"As they should," Lars said. "Civilization is mostly maintenance."

"Jevons," Lars said.

The model shifted to a historical overlay: coal, oil, fusion, antimatter, computation, simulation economies, habitat megastructures, energy markets. Each efficiency gain lowered immediate cost and increased total dependency. The curve did not look like progress. It looked like a feeding pattern.

Gas giant cities requiring constant thermal management. Simulated luxury worlds using star-scale energy budgets to keep bored aristocrats entertained. "Of course it is."

Lars watched the red zones pulse.

"Exactly."

"It is now," Grim said.

Valdrun answered. "There are seven guardians. Lyra's channel is not a normal channel anymore. Your parents' channels are dormant but not confirmed dead. Vigor is reachable. I am here. The remaining two are buried behind conditions I cannot safely open until your next stage."

"That is irritating."

"Yes."

"Usually."

It was less a battle plan than an immune response: wake hidden nodes, reinforce vulnerable carriers, reduce dependency in selected colonies, seed better cultural patterns, expose hostile acquisition networks, avoid dramatic centralization, and absolutely do not announce the return of a half-mythical enhanced human lineage with ancient nanotechnology.

"No."

"We are rebuilding a nervous system."

Valdrun's eyes sharpened.

"Remembering sideways."

He could live with that.

"After food," Grim said.

"I had coffee."

"Coffee is not food."

"That is a controversial position."

## Chapter 9: Threshold Models

Lunch did not float.

It was herring, potatoes, crispbread, cheese, butter, and something Grim called historically suspicious but ate anyway after installing a temporary brine-tolerance patch.

Afterward, the barge simulation faded.

Not dramatically. The water lost depth. The sun softened. The deck became a grid, then white floor, then the clean oval of Simulation Chamber 3. Lars preferred the barge, which annoyed him because it meant the machine had been right.

The planning model remained.

Grim had turned Lyra's threshold archives into a layered working board. Each branch showed confidence intervals, source quality, how old the data was, and recommended actions. No glowing prophecy. No chosen-one nonsense. Just models, data, and enough uncertainty to keep honest people awake.

Lars respected that.

He distrusted certainty. Certainty was what idiots used when they ran out of instruments.

"Start with the first threshold," he said.

Grim enlarged a band of amber indicators. "Threshold One: hostile interest in carrier biology. Triggered by artifact black markets, unexplained disappearances of long-lived individuals, abnormal blood-analysis procurement, and pattern-matched attacks like the one on The Rim."

"How many suspected carrier incidents?"

"Seventeen strong matches over the last ninety years. Forty-two weak. Three confirmed by guardian-side metadata. The acceleration is recent, but the pattern is not."

"Confirmed deaths?"

Valdrun looked at Grim.

Grim's voice flattened. "Two."

Lars nodded once.

He did not know them.

That did not help.

"Threshold Two," he said.

The model shifted. Red filaments appeared between corporate sovereignties, paramilitary zones, collapsed colonies, and religious war networks.

"Civilizational overcomplexity," Grim said. "Lyra's later models call it systemic overmaturity. The polite term. The less polite annotations use tumor metaphors."

"Civilizational cancer," Lars said.

Again, the phrase arrived sideways.

Valdrun did not smile this time.

"Systems that cannot simplify keep adding control layers," Valdrun said. "Each layer fixes failures the last one created. Eventually the management structure eats more resilience than it gives back."

Grim highlighted a gas giant megacity in a French-Chinese corporate protectorate. "This habitat ring requires thirty-two synchronized energy imports, fourteen external food streams, two proprietary atmosphere engines, and a legal code so large its judicial AI uses a dedicated moon."

"That sounds expensive."

"It is considered prosperous."

"Naturally."

The model showed one broken energy import. Then a delayed food stream. Then a maintenance strike suppressed by private security. Then riots. Then emergency control layers. Then more dependency.

The habitat did not collapse in the simulation.

It survived by becoming worse.

Lars disliked that more.

"And Threshold Three?"

Valdrun took over. "Guardian reactivation. Not waking all Aerits at once. That would be stupid, dangerous, and exactly the kind of single dramatic gesture Lyra designed against. "How many?"

Grim opened a list and immediately blurred most of it. "Medical lock still protects parts of your memory. But from archive indexing and current threat data: initial priority group is nine."

"Nine Aerits."

"Nine likely Aerits," Valdrun said. "Some may not survive full unlock. Some may refuse. Some may have built lives that cannot be broken open casually."

That landed harder than Lars expected.

Six hundred years old or not, hidden superhuman lineage or not, people had lives. Jobs. Friends. Children maybe. Bad habits. Favorite pubs. Debts. Dogs. Cover identities that had become real identities, because time did not care what the paperwork said.

"We ask," Lars said.

Valdrun studied him.

"We do not drag anyone into this because an archive says threshold," Lars continued. "If they are in immediate danger, we protect first. But the unlock is consent-based unless they are dying and unable to choose."

Grim tilted his head. "That was in Lyra's later ethical constraints."

"Good."

"Your phrasing was less formal."

"Also good."

Ödesvaka hovered in from the chamber wall, where it had been pretending not to listen.

"Consent constraint marked as captain directive."

"Do not make it sound grand."

"Directive downgraded to practical rule."

"Better."

Valdrun folded his hands behind his back. "There is one more immediate issue."

"There always is."

Grim expanded The Rim's traffic logs. "Aphex has secured the station sector. Galactic police have opened a formal terrorism inquiry. Black Hole B-7 survivors are absent, which means either none survived, or someone extracted them quickly. The Fastulv's departure vector is filed, but our correction into the Void is suspicious."

"To an idiot, very. To a competent investigator, extremely."

Grim looked offended.

"Can we alter them elegantly?"

"Yes."

"Gabriel Arsenault owes me several billion favors."

"He paid you several billion kronor," Grim said.

## Chapter 11: The Useless Junk Bazaar

The first thing Lars noticed was the gravity.

Too clean.

SPECULOOS station.

LP 890-9c.

Grim's voice, but younger.

In the planning chamber, the real model floated behind the false projection: Aphex nodes, audit pathways, legal triggers, internal security chains, Major Fabron's brigade, Professor Fayolle's lab network, and three possible locations where the priority Aerit might be buried under ordinary employment records.

Name hidden.

Age on record: forty-two.

Forty-two.

Five hundred to seven hundred.

Somewhere out there, another person was walking around with a life, a job, habits, perhaps friends, perhaps a favorite bad pub. They thought they were mortal in the ordinary way. Not fully.

They were going to find someone asleep inside their own life. They would walk near Black Hole B-7 nets, Aphex secrets, and whatever else had been hunting carriers for ninety years. On The Rim, Vigor Hallman served dinner to Aphex soldiers and pretended to be only a farmer.

Somewhere in Aphex records, a forty-two-year-old materials specialist continued a normal day under a false sky, unaware that Black Hole B-7 had found a pattern in their bloodwork.

And far away, or perhaps not far at all, a temporary body stood in a locked room for less than two seconds and left behind particles that folded into nothing.

This time, when the residue vanished, someone was watching the watchers.
