# Chapter 43: No Gods, Just Old Engineers

The first person to kneel to Lars did it in a docking corridor beside a broken vending machine.

He was a mechanic from a pilgrim transport diverted away from First Door by Vigor's food-and-safety blockade. Young, exhausted, wearing a Syren thread around one wrist and grease under his nails. When he recognized Lars, his face changed with unbearable relief.

Then he dropped to one knee.

Lars reacted badly.

"Get up."

The mechanic flinched.

"I said get up. I am not your altar, your door, your proof, or your excuse to outsource thinking. Stand up and check that vending machine. It is making a noise like it wants to kill children."

The mechanic stared.

The vending machine clicked ominously.

Grim tilted his head. "It is indeed unsafe. Probable snack delivery, possible arson."

That saved the moment. The mechanic stood, confused and crying, then opened the service panel. Inside, a power regulator had scorched halfway through its casing. The smell of hot plastic cut through incense residue from the pilgrim transport. Five minutes later he was repairing it while Lars held a flashlight and felt like the galaxy's least qualified counselor.

"I only wanted to thank you," the mechanic said.

"Then thank me by not kneeling."

"What should I do instead?"

Lars looked at the exposed regulator. "Learn why it burned. Teach someone else. Refuse anyone who says the Void wants your blood. Eat before making cosmic decisions. Hungry people join stupid movements."

The mechanic laughed through tears.

Fabron watched afterward with a strange expression.

"What?"

"Pastoral care by assault," Fabron said.

"Worked, didn't it?"

"Theologically alarming. Operationally sound. I will deny approving it."

The incident spread because everything spread. Someone had recorded the first thirty seconds, missing the regulator and catching only the command to stand. The Refuser nickname grew teeth. Syren splintered over it. Some called Lars proof that true messengers denied themselves. Others called him enemy of transcendence. A smaller, more useful group began calling for repair vigils: public maintenance work instead of pilgrimage.

Vigor amplified that mercilessly.

Bring tools, not candles. Soup if the job runs long.

Madame Fabron added: Candles acceptable if power fails.

Mira, unwilling patron saint of people refusing sainthood, sent Lars a message.

Your anti-cult has terrible branding and unstable load distribution.

Good, he replied.

The public noise was almost easier because it could be insulted.

The private conversations were harder.

Valdrun admitted he had wanted Lars central because he was tired. Six centuries of watchfulness made command look merciful. Ödesvaka logged the admission without mockery. Grim admitted he preferred central models when distributed emotion became difficult to parse. Vigor admitted usefulness sometimes tempted him toward permission. Fabron admitted his order could become exactly what it opposed if fear made ritual rigid.

Mira listened, then said, "I am afraid that if I ask for more memory, I will lose the life I built without it."

No one answered quickly.

Finally Lars said, "You might lose parts. You might gain parts. You might decide not to ask. All allowed. Even if we need you, your head is not public equipment."

"Even if you need me?"

"Especially then."

The words cost him. Not because he doubted them. Because need was standing in the room with a knife and excellent arguments.

Mira saw that. "That answer passed stress tolerance."

"Put it on my grave."

"Premature."

"Optimistic."

Black Hole responded by accelerating toward First Door with a biological processor fleet. Syren's largest faction announced synchronized silence. Aphex central prepared an emergency fleet despite Arsenault's stalling. NUKEA moved power ships "for safety."

Every pressure line converged.

Lars stood aboard Havets and looked at the KBC basin map. Civilizations hiding by dimming themselves. Older engineers spread across field states. Creator ruins like abandoned transit mistakes. Humanity and its cousins shouting in the dark with weapons and sermons.

The raw data did not care what anyone called it. Not god. Not ghost. Not destiny. It was residue, thermal debt, mass accounting, failed bodies, field models, bad maps, better warnings, and living people trying not to repeat old engineering sins with newer flags.

He accepted the scale.

He refused the interpretation.

"No gods," he said.

Grim waited.

"Just old engineers. And living people who still have to fix vending machines."

Grim considered this.

"The vending machine remains statistically dangerous."

"Then we start there."
