# Chapter 28: Fabron Sees Too Much

Fabron began keeping two notebooks.

The first was official, encrypted, formatted, and suitable for Aphex command review. It contained hostile movements, audit findings, tactical risks, and recommendations phrased so cowards could approve them without noticing they were brave. It used words like anomalous, contested, salvage, liability, and local authority. It did not use the word Aerit. It did not mention old greetings, stolen voices, or the fact that an antique ship no one could see had changed the behavior of three modern battle problems.

The second was paper.

He bought it from a Rim station shop that also sold prayer candles, valve grease, and lottery cards. The cover was plain brown. The pages had a faint tooth under the pen. Paper could burn. Paper could be hidden in a boot sole. Paper could not be remotely amended by a superior with a clean office and a frightened imagination.

In it he wrote the impossible things.

Lars Odin recognized Creator prompts without training.

Grim negotiated with ancient systems faster than any legal cognition and then complained about pantry taxonomy.

Havets Sista Sång appeared in no sensor record yet altered outcomes.

Dr. Sovan refused memory restoration but solved old interfaces by treating them like tired machinery.

Vigor routed secure packets through recipes.

Fabron's childhood prayers matched operational doctrine older than France.

Repair before worship.

He wrote that line three times and hated the tremor in his hand.

The confrontation happened aboard Fastulv because Lars believed difficult conversations improved when the furniture was ugly. The cargo bay smelled of preserved lemons, ozone, old insulation, and whatever Grim had spilled while testing whether noodles behaved differently in low spin. A patched crate served as table. A dent in the wall had been labeled NOT MY FAULT in Lars's handwriting and GRAVITATIONAL DISAGREEMENT in Grim's.

Fabron placed both notebooks on the crate.

"You are withholding a civilization from declared authority," he said.

Lars leaned against a cargo crate. "Large accusation."

"You are not denying it."

"I deny your phrasing."

"Fine. You are withholding a people, ships, technologies, an old war, and a doctrine my family has apparently recited at dinner for centuries without proper footnotes."

Grim raised one metal finger. "Footnotes would have been tactically risky."

"I was not asking you."

"Your tone implied an audience."

Fabron turned back to Lars. He had rehearsed six versions of the question. None survived the room.

"What are you?"

Lars hated the question less than expected. Fabron was not asking as zealot or collector. He was asking as an officer who had realized the battlefield extended under his childhood and had the decency to be angry about it.

"Aerit," Lars said. "Human-adjacent, not exactly human anymore. Pluto artifact contact, survival, GRIN, Kalari pressure, Heliostat mistakes, and fifty years of very expensive stupidity wearing lab coats. Not gods. Old idiots who learned maintenance before dying got boring."

Fabron absorbed this without screaming.

He sat because his knees had made a private recommendation.

"And Sovan?"

"Probably. By consent, she decides how much that matters."

"You are protecting her by withholding a truth about her own body."

"No. I am protecting her right not to have truth used as a crowbar."

"That distinction is morally convenient."

"It is morally inconvenient as hell. That's how I know it may be real."

Fabron looked toward the inner hatch, beyond which Fastulv's systems hummed with old personality and cheap repairs. "How many?"

"Locked? Unknown. Enough to be hunted. Not enough to save anyone by central command."

"You expect me to accept unknown as operational planning?"

"No. I expect you to hate it and still not build a prison with better lighting."

Fabron opened the paper notebook. Between notes on the survey platform and Mira's waveform theft, he had drawn the Clear Hand emblem from memory: three circles, deliberate crack.

"Clear Hand. Do you know it?"

Lars saw the emblem and memory stirred.

Not a migraine. Not the old lock. A pressure of recovered things behind the ribs: Heliostat corridors full of tired people making culture out of emergency procedure. Lyra arguing that encryption died when keys died, but a habit could survive being misunderstood. Someone painting three circles on a pump casing because the pump had saved fifty children and deserved a mark that said do not centralize this.

"Not your branch," Lars said. "But I know the ancestor pattern."

Fabron shut his eyes.

He had expected denial, perhaps manipulation, perhaps one more clever answer from a man allergic to plain reverence. Recognition was worse. Recognition made his grandmother's table, his father's polished tools, the order's midnight repair vigils, and old prayers over valves part of a larger machine no one had told them they were carrying.

"My loyalty is now divided."

"It was always divided," Lars said. "Aphex pays you. France trained you. Your order shaped you. If Återviens requires you to obey me, it has failed so hard we should shoot the paperwork. If it requires you to protect civilians, stop Aphex from becoming a cancer, and tell me when I start acting like command owns the room, it may be useful."

Grim nodded. "He is well qualified for the final item."

Fabron gave Grim a look. "I will include you in my prayers."

"Threat detected."

The humor did not remove the weight. It only kept the room breathable.

"Fabron," Lars said, quieter. "You are human."

The major's jaw tightened. "I know that."

"Good. Stay human. Don't let anyone make your family into proof that I own something."

"My family would be relieved to remain irritating without cosmic promotion."

"Excellent tradition."

Fabron looked down at the notebooks. The official one could destroy careers if written well. The paper one could destroy him if found. Neither answered what duty meant when declared authority was one of the risks.

"What do I tell Arsenault?"

"Enough to keep him useful. Not enough to make him overconfident."

"That is not a report. That is animal husbandry."

"You're learning Vigor's language."

The Syren ship complicated everything ten minutes later by broadcasting a public claim.

The message hit every local relay with soft music under it and a visual header of Door Nine rendered as a halo. They had recovered a Creator fragment. They invited seekers of the Void to witness the first holy silence at a neutral parley. They promised evidence, sanctuary, and surrender without fear. They called the KBC Void an open mercy.

Mira called immediately.

Her face appeared on Fastulv's cargo screen, pale with contained anger.

"They are using my stolen waveform in the header. Not enough for identity load. Enough for scent."

Grim isolated the sound. The dead platform's trick, made theatrical. Not enough to unlock her. Enough to scent her. Enough for Black Hole pattern hunters, Aphex factions, and hungry believers to attach her to the myth without her consent.

"Can it trigger your lock?" Lars asked.

"Not directly. Too thin. But it scrapes the boundary. It wants associations to propagate."

"Dirty correlation window," Grim said.

"Dirty ethics," Mira replied.

Fabron closed the official notebook first. Then the paper one.

"Orders?" Lars asked.

Fabron stood. He looked angry in three jurisdictions.

"Aphex order: attend and prevent hostile monopoly. Clear Hand order: keep the authority narrow. Personal order: make sure nobody uses Dr. Sovan's voice again without consequence. All three are actionable."

"Coherence?"

Fabron did not know the old answer by memory. He answered anyway.

"Coherence pending."
