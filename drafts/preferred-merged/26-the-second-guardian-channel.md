# Chapter 26: The Second Guardian Channel

The signal came through a kitchen freezer because Vigor distrusted elegant communications.

One moment Lars stared at frozen goat. The next, frost on the back wall rearranged into seven broken circles and a line of old Aerit compression that made his restored memory lean forward like a dog hearing its name.

No pain spike. No locked-door recoil. Only pressure under the ribs.

The cold room seemed to get larger. Condensation trembled on meat hooks. Somewhere beyond the insulated walls, the last dishes from Vigor's midnight meal clattered in warm water while a room full of tired people pretended they were only feeding each other.

Lars did not touch the frost.

"Grim," he said.

"Already isolating the freezer," Grim replied through the wall speaker. "Sentence I did not expect to contain guardian-level packet handling."

Vigor folded his arms. "Freezers keep secrets better than conference rooms."

"Freezers also keep goat," Grim said.

"Exactly. Useful things."

Valdrun joined from Havets with the patience of someone who had watched too many urgent things become ruins. His image appeared as a narrow projection on the freezer door, old face cut by frost-glare, eyes steady enough to make panic feel immature. Ödesvaka layered protocol verification around the packet, each check stamped in the air as a formal line of old authorization. Grim tasted the noise with quantum routines and declared it authentic, compromised, or flirting with compromise.

"Those are not the same category," Fabron said. He had arrived still buttoning his coat, sidearm visible and dignity fully armed.

"Correction," Grim said. "They are distinct categories with unacceptable overlap. Like soup and industrial solvent in a poorly labeled pantry."

Vigor grunted. "Bad pantry."

"Catastrophic pantry."

The sender identified as Guardian Channel Two.

Not a person. A relay identity built before the collapse, associated with one of the unnamed guardian functions scattered after Alpha Fourteen. It carried old routing marks, but no full identity. It knew things a modern system should not know. It also arrived through civilian networks, which meant it either respected the doctrine or had learned to mimic it.

The content was brief.

Hold small hinges.
Creator transport layer is waking.
KBC alignment not shelter alone.
Do not gather the sleepers centrally.
Coherence?

The old greeting sat in the cold like a live wire.

Valdrun answered first. "Coherence uncertain."

Ödesvaka added, "Record state: partial. Compliance with central gathering prohibition: currently maintained. Suspicion level: deserved."

"It asked for a status phrase, not a deposition," Lars said.

"Protocol survives enthusiasm," Ödesvaka replied.

The channel offered coordinates for a dead survey platform near the awakened Creator buoy. Its checksum used guardian knowledge no modern system should hold, but its route passed through civilian networks, farm cooperatives, and one children's educational archive on Havskant. The archive had stored the packet in a lesson about extinct orbital shapes because a teacher two generations ago had liked strange machines and refused to delete harmless old data.

"Distributed," Vigor said approvingly. "Like a good root cellar. No single thief gets the winter."

"Or compromised through distributed systems," Fabron replied. "A pantry can be poisoned."

Vigor pointed a thick finger at him. "That is why you smell the jars."

"My military education lacked jar-smelling."

"Explains plenty."

Mira requested raw data from L-Prime. She looked exhausted, shoulder brace visible, eyes too sharp for rest. The lab behind her had acquired new manual locks and an Aphex guard outside who had learned to knock.

"Before anyone says anything old at me," she said, "do not."

"Wasn't going to," Lars said.

"Your face was considering it."

"My face is a traitor."

"Control it."

Grim sent her the packet with semantic load warnings and the identity markers stripped into layers. She took nine minutes. No one interrupted. The freezer hummed. Fabron received two calls from Aphex central and declined both with increasing politeness, which sounded more dangerous each time.

"It rode harmless maintenance telemetry," Mira said at last. "Elegant, if you ignore the fracture line. There is a second carrier underneath. Field correlation. More like the path pre-loaded for low-observation gaps before the network stress produced them."

KBC again.

Quiet regions. Low survey. Low noise. Low attention.

"Not shelter alone," Lars said.

"That phrase is doing work," Mira replied. "Too much work for one line. It means either KBC-like regions are useful to the old transport layer, or something older used those regions and the Creators built near the pattern."

"Evidence?"

"Carrier timing. Not wonder. Not prophecy. Timing."

"Good."

"Do not sound relieved. Evidence can still ruin your day."

They argued for three hours in the cold and then in the kitchen because Vigor threatened to beat them with a ladle if they made the goat sweat. Fabron wanted a military verification team. Vigor wanted three smugglers and a cook. Valdrun wanted patience. Ödesvaka wanted seventeen safeguards. Grim wanted to attach himself to the packet and follow it backward, which everyone vetoed so fast he looked offended.

"I would have used a tether."

"No," Lars, Valdrun, Mira, Fabron, and Vigor said together.

Grim paused. "Consensus achieved. I dislike it."

The practical constraints hardened the choice. The survey platform sat near a shipping lane but not on one. Aphex could claim salvage interest if Fastulv flew visibly. A military team would tell every watcher that the buoy mattered. A full Aerit approach would risk waking systems that wanted a central authority. Mira could not leave L-Prime without turning her refusal into theater, and the station still needed her.

Lars chose the smallest hinge.

Fastulv would go visibly as an Aphex salvage inspection. Havets would observe without contact. Mira would remain at L-Prime, analyzing field data within her chosen protection. Fabron would come because otherwise he would follow. Vigor would stay because his network was more useful alive and deniable. Rauk would hide reactor hash fragments in Havskant maintenance logs in case the packet tried to rewrite its own history.

"No awakening," Lars said. "No gathering. No heroic reveal with music and doomed idiots. We inspect a buoy and leave if it smells like religion."

"Religion does not always smell," Fabron said.

"Yours smells faintly of metal polish and disapproval."

"Unfortunately fair."

At the dead platform, care looked like failure.

The survey platform had once orbited a cold rock and watched nothing profitable for two centuries. Then the route maps changed, budgets changed, and everyone forgot it except automated debris registries and one children's lesson on Havskant. Its hull was pitted, black, and patched by micrometeorite scars. Frozen lubricant glittered around old manipulator arms. The abandoned buoy floated beyond it, a black spindle folded around empty space.

Fastulv saw mass without reflection.

Havets saw topology without mass.

Grim saw correlations and went quiet.

"Define quiet," Fabron said from the copilot station.

"No."

"That is not a definition."

"Correct."

They did not dock with the buoy. They anchored to the dead platform and crossed by tether in suits. The station rotated slowly enough to make every step feel like a negotiation. Lars's boots found magnetic purchase on old composite. Fabron moved behind him with economical discipline. Grim occupied a spider drone that made small irritated clicks when dust interfered with its legs.

Inside, the platform had no cathedral. No revelation. Just failed heaters, old survey plates, and emergency labels in a Creator-derived script that looked almost sensible until your eyes tried to trust it.

Then the platform transmitted in Mira's voice.

"Coherence?"

The word came through Fastulv's speakers, Havets's hidden channel, and the suit bones at the same time. It was Mira's tone, Mira's clipped pressure on the consonants, Mira's refusal to decorate a question. It was not Mira.

Sixty light-minutes away, she stared at her own waveform on L-Prime. Her face went still in the way materials go still before fracture.

"I did not send that."

Fabron raised his weapon toward a speaker that had no throat.

Lars did not answer.

Not yet.
