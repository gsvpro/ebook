# Chapter 29: Black Hole Nursery

The nursery was hidden inside a hospital ship that had never treated a patient.

Its registry claimed disaster relief. The name on the hull was Mercy of Twelve Harbors, painted in blue letters beside a faded emblem of open hands. The docking lights blinked in the soft pattern medical ships used around frightened refugees. Its public beacon offered pediatric trauma care, epidemic isolation, and emergency organ synthesis. Everything about it was designed to make decent people open doors.

Havets found it behind a debris moon where the Syren parley route crossed a dead logistics lane. Fastulv stayed three light-minutes out, loud on civilian salvage bands and ready to become the visible mistake if anyone looked the wrong way. Fabron's Aphex team approached in a maintenance skiff wearing stolen sanitation credentials. Vigor's smugglers drifted closer under the thermal shadow of an ice hauler and complained over open comms about spoiled onions. Rauk's reactor dampers sat in two black cases under Lars's boots. They hummed with the particular menace of farm engineering used for war.

The hospital ship's outer lock smelled clean.

That was the first horror.

No rot. No gore in the corridors. No screaming. Just antiseptic air, polished grab rails, children's murals projected on bulkheads, and directional signs pointing toward TRIAGE, FAMILY REUNION, HYDRATION, COUNSELING. The walls were warm to the touch because the ship wanted bodies calm when they entered.

Below the public decks, past a locked personnel lift and three false quarantine seals, the cryo bays held no refugees.

They held growth racks, marrow vats, neural scaffolds, refrigerated blood, and failed interface bodies sorted by anomaly probability. Black Hole B-7 had learned to farm what it could not understand.

Lars lay in the observation duct above the main chamber and felt old anger arrive without the old neurological lock.

Not the old pain.

Memory with teeth.

Pluto cold. Heliostat hunger. Lyra's hands shaking over a body they could not save because the tool they needed had not been invented yet. The ancient argument that survival did not justify becoming the thing that hunted you. All of it came back clean enough to hurt properly.

Fabron lay beside him in infiltration armor, silent for once. His breathing was controlled through the helmet link. Too controlled. The main chamber below them reflected in his visor: rows of donor bays, transparent tubing, technicians in white suits with black wrist implants, and a central vat where something small floated in golden nutrient gel.

Grim occupied the ship's maintenance systems, but the usual dry confidence had thinned.

"Twenty-six living donors," Grim said. "Most ordinary by Aerit standards. Some resilient in ways Black Hole overvalues, as if durability were seasoning. One possible locked Aerit. Consent unavailable."

He paused.

"Additional category uncertain."

Lars knew what he meant. The central vat.

"Later," Lars said.

"Later may become a moral debt with interest."

"Everything in this room is debt."

There it was. The rule under pressure.

Fabron shifted enough for armor fabric to whisper. "If we do not wake them, they may die. That is not pressure. That is the operational fact."

"If we force it, we may kill who they are trying to remain."

"A hard line in a slaughterhouse."

"Yeah. That is why the boundary matters here."

Fabron looked down through the duct grille. A donor in Bay Fourteen twitched under sedation. A Black Hole technician adjusted the drip without looking at the face.

"I am responsible for every civilian I fail to extract," Fabron said.

"Yes."

"And for every one I turn into evidence by extracting badly."

"Also yes."

"Your doctrine is inconvenient."

"It's not doctrine today. It is an invasive step we do not take unless it helps the patient."

Mira fed structural weak points from L-Prime. She had refused to watch the visual feed until they needed her eyes. That refusal lasted eleven minutes. Then she opened one window, saw the vat labels, and went silent long enough for Lars to worry.

"I can help break the ship," she said finally. "Do not ask me to classify the people unless classification changes what you do."

"Understood."

"I mean that exactly. If the answer will not change a clamp, route, or drug, leave it out."

Grim said, "Boundary logged."

They moved.

Fabron's soldiers breached under a sanitation alert Grim had upgraded into a biohazard panic. The ship believed in its own cover enough to obey the wrong emergency. Doors sealed outward. Internal med drones rushed toward false contamination zones. Vigor's smugglers cut external clamps and attached rescue blisters to three maintenance ports. Rauk's reactor dampers made the power spine stutter like an old drunk, not failing, never failing, only missing rhythm enough to break central timing.

Lars entered through the ceiling.

He landed on a surgical track above the donor bays and did not use Ragnarrök. Not here. Not with vats, oxygen lines, and frightened bodies one bad shock away from dying. The first Black Hole surgeon wore human skin and insectile patience. Its white gloves were clean. Its badge said DR. ELLIAN VALE, PEDIATRIC RECONSTRUCTION. Its eyes looked at Lars as a variable.

It did not banter.

Neither did Lars.

He broke its arm, pinned it to a rack, and let Fabron foam it alive for interrogation. The foam hardened around its jaw before it could bite down on the implant behind its molars. Fabron's hands were efficient. His face was not.

"Alive," Fabron told his nearest corporal. "We need buyers, routes, command keys. If it dies without permission, I will be personally disappointed in everyone."

"Major," the corporal said, voice shaking, "yes."

A particulate swarm released toward the donor bays.

It came from the ceiling vents as a glitter so fine the eye wanted to call it dust. Grim's categories fractured across the channel: sampler, sterilizer, archive denial, tissue tagger, all at once. It was designed to decide what the room was while entering lungs.

Havets moved.

No visible miracle. No holy blue light. The swarm lost coordination at the level of timing. Particles argued with particles. A thousand tiny intentions missed the same beat. They fell like soot into containment fields that had not existed in the ship's logs five seconds earlier.

Grim made a sound Lars had never heard from him.

"Classification strain," Grim said. "These are not weapons only. They are hostile experiments with incomplete labels."

"Answer no," Lars said.

"Insufficient but emotionally satisfying."

The rescue became labor.

Not glorious labor. Awkward, slow, dangerous work. Fabron's soldiers cut restraints and transferred bodies into rescue blisters while med alarms lied at them. Vigor's smugglers kept count in kitchen shorthand because panic liked numbers it could hold: six warm, eight breathing assisted, two pressure unstable, one needs hands now. Rauk's dampers kept the ship from locking the bays. Mira found which tubes could be cut and which had to be capped, voice clipped to the bone.

"Bay Seven cap blue, cut green. Do not touch silver. Silver is spinal."

"Bay Nine has two feeds labeled nutrition. One is compliance drug. Cut the left by temperature, not color."

"Bay Twelve, wait."

Bay Twelve held Isaac Tam.

Broad-faced. Farmer's hands. A labor tattoo a century out of date on his inner wrist. His file called him forty-nine. His bone density said two hundred. Old fractures had healed too cleanly. His bloodwork carried impossible repair signatures under ordinary human noise. His neural lock patterns sang quiet refusal even under sedation.

Black Hole's file marked him: PROBABLE OLD-SOURCE HOST. OPENING RISK: HIGH. HARVEST PRIORITY: PRESERVE.

Lars wanted to tear the ship apart with his bare hands.

Instead, he breathed.

"Mira," he said, "do we move him without waking him?"

"Physical risk moderate. Cognitive risk lower if no identity language, no old greeting, no direct contradiction of cover memory. Keep evidence minimal. Ask for movement only."

Fabron's voice cut in. "He is sedated."

"Sedation is not consent," Mira said. "It may still permit a motor response if he is near surface. Do not turn my sentence into permission for everything."

"Understood," Fabron said, and the respect in it was not theatrical.

Grim projected a simple message into the bay audio, low and plain.

You are in danger. We can move your body without unlocking memory. We will not open locked identity without consent. Signal if you understand.

For seven seconds nothing happened.

The room fought around them. A soldier swore. A donor coughed blood into a mask. Somewhere below, a Black Hole technician tried to vent Bay Three and died when Fabron's rear team stopped him. Lars kept his eyes on Isaac's hand.

Then Isaac's finger tapped once.

Not yes to awakening.

Yes to movement.

Good enough.

"Move him," Lars said.

Fabron's team moved him like ordinance and kin. No one said Aerit. No one said rescue. They kept his file closed, his face covered from cameras, his hand free in case he needed to withdraw even that narrow permission.

They took him, twenty-five others, and records enough to make Black Hole buyers nervous for decades.

They did not save everything.

Four cloned tissue lines self-sterilized when the ship realized extraction was failing. Grim tried to freeze the process and could not. The tissue curled inward, not dying like people but ending like stolen instructions burning themselves. A donor in Bay Nineteen survived because a smuggler named Jessa held a manual airway open for nine minutes while crying hard enough to fog her face shield. Two Black Hole technicians chose explosive implant death over capture and took a section of archive with them.

Then the central vat opened its eyes.

The body inside was child-sized because Black Hole had chosen that growth path, not because childhood lived there. Limbs too thin. Skull not wrong exactly, but optimized by committee and fear. Skin translucent over scaffolds. Nanobot fragments glimmered under the sternum in a pattern that made Lars's implanted instincts recoil.

It looked at him.

Not with innocence. Not with evil. With an unfinished hunger for continuity.

It asked, in no language Lars knew, whether it had been made wrong.

The question arrived through field vibration, vat pressure, and the part of the room that older machines used for almost-meaning. Grim tried to categorize it and failed loudly.

"Possible proto-person. Possible interface echo. Possible suffering-shaped control loop. Probability distribution unacceptable. I require a better universe."

Fabron stood at the vat edge with his weapon lowered. "Can it feel pain?"

Mira's answer came shaking. "Define pain."

"Doctor."

"Do not rush me because the room is horrible."

No one did.

Mira pulled data from the vat, not the face. Growth logs, neural continuity scans, substrate load, nanobot fragment behavior, sensory integration, memory loops. Hard evidence before mercy, because mercy without evidence could become vanity in a clean coat.

"It is not a person yet," she said at last. Each word sounded carried by hand. "The substrate has no stable continuity. Failed interface around stolen nanobot fragments. There are reflexive distress loops and borrowed response patterns. Termination may be mercy, but do not call it cleanup. That word hides load."

Lars wanted someone else to decide.

That was how old sins got dressed.

"Options," he said.

"Immediate termination stops distress and destroys evidence," Mira said. "Continued operation prolongs distress and risks Black Hole recovery behavior. Sedation and cryogenic ethical hold preserve evidence and prevent further loop reinforcement. It does not solve the question. It only refuses to lie about solving it."

Fabron closed his eyes once. "Then we carry the debt."

"Yes," Mira said.

So they sedated it, froze it, and marked it as an ethical grave for later.

Not specimen.

Not asset.

Not child, because that would steal a word from people who had lived.

Ethical grave.

Lars stayed until the cryo light turned blue. He did not know what prayer would be appropriate, so he checked the clamps twice.

The archive unlocked under duress as the ship lost power spine authority. Its doctrine files were worse than the rooms because they had no smell, no trembling hands, no immediate ugliness to warn the reader away. Just clean diagrams and ambitions.

Aerit blood as key.

Creator portals as gates.

KBC Void as throne.

Nomad field as godhead to be consumed.

Black Hole did not understand the science. It understood appetite. It believed scale meant ownership. It believed enough harvested anomaly, enough forced continuity, enough stolen route maps would let it sit in the quiet basin and command whatever older field the Creators had misunderstood.

"They think scale means ownership," Grim said. His voice was precise again, but every category sounded strained. "Common predator category error. Large appetite is not jurisdiction."

"Predators usually do," Lars said. "Then they act surprised when victims learn the layout."

"This metaphor is less comforting inside a nursery."

"Good. Comfort would be stupid."

Fastulv pulled away under false salvage distress while Havets erased the routes that mattered and left the routes that would lead Black Hole's competitors into legal trouble. Fabron's soldiers sat with rescued donors in the blisters, helmets off when safe, hands visible when people woke confused. Jessa refused to stop holding Bay Nineteen's hand until med took over. Rauk's dampers burned out in a neat row, cheap parts dying expensive deaths.

Isaac slept in a protected compartment.

His consent record displayed one line.

Move me. Do not open me.

Lars stared at it longer than necessary.

Fabron came to stand beside him. The major had blood on one sleeve and foam residue on the other. He looked older by a decade and more responsible than anyone should have to be.

"We left records behind," Fabron said.

"Some."

"We left bodies."

"Yes."

"We froze a question in a box."

"Yes."

Fabron swallowed. "I will account for my part."

"Don't make it a speech."

"It is not a speech. It is a responsibility."

That landed where Lars could not joke it away.

Mira joined the channel without video. "The donors need independent advocates before debrief. No Aphex custody language. No anomaly labels in medical headers. Isaac's boundary line should be visible to anyone who enters his room."

"Done," Lars said.

"And the vat file?"

"Ethical grave. Locked review. No owner."

Her breath shook once over the channel. "Good. Not enough, but good."

Lars sent Isaac's line to every trusted channel as warning and vow.

Not because it solved the nursery.

Because if they forgot that one sentence, Black Hole had already taught them its language.
