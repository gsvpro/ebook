# Chapter 47: The Blood Market

Black Hole's blood market hid inside a refueling swarm that serviced ships too poor to ask questions.

Hundreds of tanks, tugs, machine shops, dorm pods, brothels, clinics, noodle stalls, prayer capsules, and illegal labs orbited a dim red dwarf under no single jurisdiction. It was the perfect place to buy fuel, organs, forged papers, reactor parts, debt forgiveness, missing persons, and bad ideas.

The swarm called itself Little Mercy.

Vigor said any place that named itself mercy should be searched for knives.

Lars went in with Fabron, Grim, and twelve people who did not look like a team because Vigor had dressed them. Lars wore a stained maintenance coat over armor. Fabron wore a trader's jacket he regarded as a personal attack by textiles. Grim carried a crate of counterfeit nutrient cultures and had painted one cheek with engine grease after studying smuggler feeds too literally.

"You look like smugglers with unresolved gastrointestinal jurisdiction," Fabron said.

"Exactly," Vigor replied over comm. "A sour stomach is better camouflage than a flag. No one bothers such people unless they owe money."

"I object to the odor component."

"Then breathe less fancy."

The market cell fed the pod fleet: biological processor gel, stolen blood, nanobot-contaminated tissue, false consent tags, sedatives, quiet-basin navigation rumors. Cut supply, reduce force at First Door. Capture records, identify buyers, save civilians if the swarm turned into a fireball, and do it before Black Hole noticed Fastulv moving visible toward the ancient door.

They could not simply raid it. Too many civilians. Too many hostages. Too many records that would vanish if frightened. Too many desperate people using the same docks because honest ports had priced survival like a luxury.

So they bought an appointment.

The broker wore a child's face by choice, which told Lars everything necessary about its ethics. The body was adult by bone scan, surgically narrowed, voice modulated soft and high because predators liked testing whether disgust made buyers careless.

"You were referred as serious procurement," the broker said.

"I am never serious," Lars said. "But I am expensive."

Fabron's jaw tightened. Grim ran twelve parallel disgust classifications and kept his face pleasantly blank.

The broker led them through a clinic that smelled of antiseptic, hot noodles, and old fear. Real patients sat beside trafficked inventory without knowing which category the person next to them belonged to. A mother argued over dialysis credit. A courier slept handcuffed to a fuel contract. Two Black Hole guards pretended to be bored mechanics near a door with too much cooling.

"Do not start early," Fabron murmured.

"You say that like I lack restraint."

"I say it because evidence suggests restraint has filed a complaint against you."

The broker offered samples in sealed vials, each labeled by anomaly class. Stolen tissue had become taxonomy. AERIT-POSSIBLE. NANOBOT-ACTIVE. QUIET BASIN RESPONSE. FIELD COMPATIBLE. CONSENT TAG ATTACHED, the last phrase printed in green beside codes Grim had already identified as forgeries.

One label made Grim's body go still.

AERIT-PROBABLE: SOVAN LINEAGE MATCH.

Mira, listening from L-Prime, said nothing.

The silence was not empty. Lars heard the small intake of breath she tried to suppress, the muted scrape of a chair, the way her channel noise narrowed as if she had become a single load-bearing beam.

Lars did not move for one breath. Then he smiled like a drunk idiot.

"Expensive?"

The broker relaxed, mistaking rage for greed. "Very. Source protected, but we have echoes."

"Echoes, because apparently evil needed a poetry budget?"

"Relatives, discarded tissue, medical waste, pattern-compatible donors. Enough for ritual, science, or appetite."

Fabron's hand twitched. He corrected it into a cuff adjustment with parade-ground violence.

"Consent records?" Grim asked, mild.

The broker smiled with stolen innocence. "Attached."

"Forgery quality: mediocre," Grim said privately. "Ink age mismatch. Signature reuse. One donor was dead before the alleged draw."

Lars bought the entire lot with Arsenault's emergency account.

Arsenault whispered, "That number has teeth, Monsieur Odin."

"Asset pain registered," Grim said. "Continue behaving like civilization."

"Civilization is currently buying grave dirt with my money."

"Yes," Fabron said. "Try to deserve the expense."

The purchase opened the trust path. Grim followed payment metadata into the archive and found not a vault but a distributed guilt machine: buyers masked as clinics, research shells, Syren devotional labs, Black Hole logistics fronts, and one Aphex subsidiary so distant Arsenault went silent for a full three seconds.

"I will need that name," he said.

"Later," Lars replied.

"Now."

"Later, unless you want your righteous corporate murder to spook the market before we get civilians out."

Arsenault made a sound like polished glass cracking. "Later."

Fabron's disguised teams blocked exits with fuel safety inspections. Vigor's people started three kitchen fires in exactly the wrong places, filling one corridor with smoke and another with angry vendors carrying soup pots. Rauk's dampers cut power to lab freezers but preserved civilian life support. Local clinic alarms began evacuating patients for a fake coolant leak that was, because Grim disliked lying wastefully, based on a real coolant leak two modules away.

Then Lars stopped pretending.

The Ragnarrök stayed holstered. Too many pressure walls, too many children, too many oxygen lines and innocent bodies that would not care how good his aim was. He used hands, walls, foam charges, and the market's cramped geometry. Black Hole soldiers liked fear and open lanes. The swarm offered neither. Civilians slammed shutters. Mechanics dropped tools into pursuers' paths. Noodle vendors overturned boiling stock with heroic profanity.

A guard raised a weapon toward a clinic bed.

Fabron shot the weapon, then the guard's knee, then looked personally offended by the need for both.

"Custody," he snapped at a local volunteer. "Alive if possible. Documented always."

"You giving orders here?"

"Yes."

The volunteer obeyed because Fabron sounded like paperwork with a sidearm.

Grim found the live processing lab behind a cold wall. The tanks were smaller than Lars expected. That made them worse. Tissue suspended in gel twitched under electrical rhythm, not conscious in any whole sense but not innocent of pain either. Biological computation did not need personhood to make cruelty real. Black Hole had built processors from stolen scraps and called the result efficiency.

"Can they feel?" Lars asked.

Mira answered before Grim could. "Some nociceptive activity. No stable identity pattern in these tanks. Pain without a person is still not a tool."

"Right."

He planted charges with hands that wanted to shake and did not get the privilege.

The child-faced broker triggered a thermal purge.

Mira stopped it through materials, not memory. She had studied the cryo seals from purchase scans and found every stress cheat, every false tolerance dressed as manufacturing variance. One command cycled temperature just wrong enough to jam purge valves without rupturing samples. It was a tiny act of violence against bad engineering and it saved all the records.

"Cracks do not lie," she said, voice cold. "People do."

They captured vials, ledgers, buyers, and one living technician who had joined Black Hole after Syren convinced him the Void wanted flesh returned to the field. He cried when Vigor's medic treated his broken arm. He looked younger when he cried, which made Lars angrier, not less.

"I thought we were helping them become more than bodies," the technician said.

Vigor's medic splinted the arm anyway. "Bodies first. Philosophy after swelling goes down."

"Do not confuse medical custody with absolution," Fabron warned.

"I don't," Lars said. "I just like civilization when it remembers arms break before sermons do."

The Sovan-labeled samples were not Mira's blood. Attempts, approximations, relatives too distant, stolen hospital metadata. Black Hole was guessing, but its guesses were improving. Worse, the ledger showed how they improved: every public advisory, every rumor, every heroic clip, every careless debate about hidden bloodlines taught the market where to look next.

Mira requested the biological material destroyed.

Grim hesitated. "Evidence category at risk."

"Copy records. Destroy tissue. The material is not yours because it is measurable."

Her voice did not rise. That made the sentence heavier.

Lars backed her before anyone argued. Consent did not end when tissue left the body. They copied ledgers, sterilized samples, destroyed active tissue, and marked the act as evidence destruction justified by personhood risk.

Fabron signed as witness. Arsenault signed as owner of the funds used to buy the material, jaw tight enough to fracture porcelain. Grim signed after adding a note that evidence without ethics became an acquisition pathway.

The raid reduced Black Hole supply by thirty percent.

Not enough.

It also exposed the people who had sold samples for rent, research grants, cult promises, debt relief, and fear. Some were monsters. Some were cornered. Some were both. Vigor's network would have to build clinics and food routes in Little Mercy before the next market grew in the same wound.

"Burning it down is faster," Lars said.

"So is burning soup," Vigor replied. "Still leaves people hungry."

As they fled, a buyer packet decoded.

Converge through blood.
Force the quiet to answer.
If answer resists, brighten until rupture.

Lars looked toward First Door.

"They think the universe owes them a scream. Entitled little meat cult."

Grim's voice was low. "Then we deny them acoustics and record the attempt as extremely poor manners."
