# Chapter 22: After the Choice

Aphex called it a containment drill by morning.

The lie was Fabron's idea, which irritated Lars because it was a good lie. A drill explained shutters, local controls, disabled remote authority, soldiers in corridors, and Director Odin yelling through legal channels while an antique freighter embarrassed hostile ships near a brown dwarf. It gave central command something bureaucratic to swallow. Meanwhile the local people cleaned blood out of ventilation grilles and pretended the tremor in their hands came from caffeine.

The lie did not explain the black residue in the med bay.

It did not explain why one archive server had tried to cook itself rather than hold a dead infiltrator's last message.

It did not explain Dr. Mira Sovan sitting alone at 0300 with seventy-three years of impossible external records open in front of her, every file stripped of identity prompts, old names, and anything Grim's boundary monitor marked as memory load.

Lars found her in the materials lab because she let him. The corridors were quieter than they should have been after victory. Aphex soldiers stood near pressure doors with their rifles lowered but not loose. A maintenance woman changed a valve wheel by hand though the actuator had already come back online. The station had learned something about remote authority and did not seem eager to forget.

Fabron waited outside the lab with Grim, two coffees, and the expression of a man whose loyalties had become a maintenance problem.

"She asked for you," Fabron said. "Not for me. Not for Aphex. Not for medical."

"Good."

"It may not be good. It may be precise."

"That is her version of good."

Grim held up a small recorder. "Boundary monitor ready. It will alarm on biometric distress, semantic load drift, and unauthorized memory-load patterns. It will also produce a tone I find unpleasant."

"Why unpleasant?"

"So you stop before I have to be rude."

Inside, the lab smelled of ionized insulation, cold tea, and the sharp mineral dust from shattered test coupons. Mira had tied her hair back badly. Her shoulder brace was open on the bench beside her, repaired with a lab clamp and impatience. Six displays floated around the main table. She had arranged them like a failure tree.

"You said stop meant stop," she said.

"Yes."

"I am stopping before memory work. No probes. No helpful little miracles. No one tidies my skull."

"Yes."

"I am not stopping evidence. Evidence can sit on the table and behave."

"Evidence is usually better trained than people."

"Not by much."

Lars sat across from her and kept his hands visible. He did not say the old greeting. He did not reach for her lock. He did not let his face do the lonely hopeful thing that would make her responsible for comforting him. He let the records sit between them where honest things belonged.

Mira had marked the coordinate burst in six colors. She had separated confirmed signal from archive damage, thermal noise, med-coffin telemetry, and the pieces that looked like intent only if you were already afraid.

"This is void-aligned," she said. "Low foreground noise, low survey density, low gravitational clutter. Not Arkology Zero exactly. Same absence family, different fracture line."

"From one dead enemy cough?"

"It coughed in coordinates. That is rude, but useful."

"You keep using useful like it excuses the cost."

"I am using it for the data. Not for myself."

"Noted."

She opened the old decompression footage from 2655.

The recording was grainy, taken from three damaged corridor cams and a suit recorder with a cracked time base. A younger Mira moved through a broken air bridge with two unconscious technicians clipped to her harness. Her suit vented atmosphere in bright crystals. Her right glove had split at the seal. She used the exposed hand anyway, locking a manual clamp while her own oxygen warning stuttered red.

Mira watched herself cross the gap.

She did not flinch this time. That cost her more than panic would have.

"I remember a gas leak," she said. "I remember coughing. I remember being ashamed because I cried."

"That may all be true," Lars said. "Still does not make the shape honest."

"I hate that answer."

"Still the least dishonest one."

The display froze on her younger face behind a scratched visor. The woman in the footage was terrified. She was also doing impossible load calculations with her hands while dying.

Mira touched the frame with one finger.

"Do not tell me what I was feeling."

"Wasn't going to."

"Good. Everyone else does. They see heroism and sand the structure smooth."

"Smooth surfaces hide cracks."

"Now you are learning."

Her question, when it came, was almost technical.

"What am I called?"

Lars looked at Grim through the glass wall. Grim's recorder light stayed green. Fabron stood farther back, giving the room the dignity of distance while still being close enough to shoot anyone who broke it.

"This may hurt," Lars said. "Not because I want it to. Because your lock is still active and the word can breach cover memory."

"Answer within the boundary."

"Aerit," Lars said.

No ceremony. Just the word, set down like a hot tool.

The word did not bring thunder for Lars. His old lock stayed quiet. The partial unlock had left scars, grief, and weight under the ribs, but not the old neurological trap.

Mira's lock was still alive.

Pain tightened behind her eyes, quick and surgical. She inhaled once through her teeth. The boundary monitor flashed amber. This was exactly why the Quiet Knock put naming after consent instead of before it. She had asked the question. The evidence feed was narrow. Grim had the load gate running. The spike rose, met the consent limit she had defined, and flattened into a hot line across the base of her skull instead of becoming the full fallback collapse the protocol was built to avoid.

Mira gripped the table until the acoustic rack behind her registered a tiny vibration, as if an old frequency had brushed it and failed to settle.

"Pause," she said.

Grim froze the display. Lars shut his mouth. Fabron did not enter.

They waited through twenty-eight seconds of recycled air, amber light, and Mira refusing to let anyone own the pain by panicking louder than she did.

At last she nodded.

"Aerit," she repeated. The monitor twitched, then held. "Category: people?"

"Yes. Annoying, breakable, dangerous, hungry. People."

"Are you in the category?"

"Unfortunately."

"And Grim?"

"Grim is a category error with opinions."

The door opened one centimeter.

"I heard that."

Mira almost smiled, then did not. "If I accept partial confirmation, not memory, what is the load path?"

"One question," Lars said. "Evidence answer. Boundary recorded somewhere neither I nor Aphex can fiddle with using money and bad judgment. You stop when you say stop."

"Who holds the record?"

"Three places. Your local vault. Grim's sealed log, readable by you. A legal dead drop Fabron can verify but not alter."

Fabron's voice came through the cracked door. "And my personal note that violating it will be treated as assault, not research."

Mira considered that. "Acceptable pending review."

Her question was simple: immediate danger because of what she was, or because of what she knew?

Grim displayed three branches: Black Hole sampling patterns, Aphex metadata leakage, and the void-aligned coordinate family. Each branch had probabilities. Mira hated the softer ones and made him relabel them assumptions.

"Both," Lars said. "Today mostly because some bastard thinks you can point at the quiet room."

"KBC?"

"A low-noise region. A hiding place. Maybe a machine room. Maybe an old mistake. We have evidence, not an answer."

"Good," she said.

"Good?"

"Early answers contaminate the test. People stop checking the model."

Mira studied the evidence until her neck warmed and tears came without permission. She looked furious at the tears. Lars looked away just enough to spare her the labor of being observed.

"Previous operating state is gone," she said. "That does not authorize a new owner. I stay here for now."

"Name your terms."

"I stay at L-Prime under Fabron's local protection. I keep my lab. I get clean data, no hidden medical access, and an alarm if evidence starts behaving like memory without permission. You harden this place. You do not own the material."

"Agreed."

"Acceptance came too fast."

"I occasionally recognize a correct answer before ruining it."

She breathed out, carefully. "One more term. No one calls me rescued."

That one hit harder than the rest.

"Agreed," Lars said. "Protected, if you accept it. Contacted, maybe. Rescued only if you use the word."

"I do not."

"Then we don't."

By sunrise, the unofficial story had spread through maintenance boards, chapel kitchens, reactor guilds, and soldier gossip. The words were different in every place. Some said Aphex central had been embarrassed by its own locks. Some said Dr. Sovan had caught enemy ships by listening to metal breathe. Some said old prayers about local hands had worked because people had performed them with wrenches instead of mouths.

Local authority held.

Repair before worship.

In a freezer under the chapel kitchen, someone found three quiet circles around an empty center scratched into frost from the inside.

Somewhere beyond Aphex, someone had answered.
