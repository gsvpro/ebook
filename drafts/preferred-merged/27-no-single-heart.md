# Chapter 27: No Single Heart

The dead platform wanted a central heart.

That was what the Creator buoy translated badly through stolen voices, old maintenance codes, and a strain in Fastulv's hull that made bolts complain. The platform had been built as a relay switch for something larger. It had lost its network six hundred thousand years ago. Like many lonely machines, it concluded that authority would fix loneliness.

The false Mira voice repeated once.

"Coherence?"

Lars kept his suit channel closed for three breaths. The old word pressed against restored memory. It would have been easy to answer from reflex, from grief, from the stupid mammal wish that anything using a beloved pattern might deserve trust.

"No voice games," he said finally. "Telemetry only."

The platform answered by dimming every light in the corridor except the ones leading inward.

Fabron's rifle tracked the ceiling. "That is not telemetry."

"It's a machine with abandonment issues."

"Machines with abandonment issues kill soldiers."

"So do officers with confidence."

"I am choosing not to hear that."

Grim displayed the model over their suit feeds. The old platform's core sat in the center like a clenched fist. Around it, dormant route channels reached toward the buoy, Door Nine, and three low-noise coordinate families that made Grim's probability labels turn cautious.

"If restored," Grim said, "it will attempt to become a routing hub for all local portal-adjacent signals. Category: useful until every dependent process routes through one failure point."

Fabron frowned. "That sounds useful."

"Centralized useful," Lars said.

"Ah." Fabron looked offended by his own learning.

The platform held Creator route maps, anomaly logs, perhaps the first clear evidence connecting the KBC Void to older infrastructure. Restoring the core would teach them quickly. It might show where the Syren fragment came from. It might show why Black Hole's dead infiltrator coughed coordinates. It might show whether Lyra had been chasing shelter or machinery when she built in the quiet.

It would also make one bright target.

Valdrun's voice arrived from Havets. "A central hub can be held through a siege. I have seen them inherited by worse hands before the next century ended."

Ödesvaka answered, "A central heart can be captured. Logged objection: obvious."

"Obvious objections still count," Fabron said.

"Correct," Ödesvaka replied. "Most catastrophes begin by classifying the obvious as inconvenient."

Mira rotated the stress model from L-Prime. She had stripped the false voice into components and refused to let anyone play it again.

"The core is not dead," she said. "It is conserving itself. Hub behavior is the overload case. We can ask for local fragments without putting full load through the spine."

"By what authority?" Fabron said. "Because every command prompt on that thing is wearing a loaded weapon."

She hesitated, and Lars heard her choosing words that would not lean on memory she had not consented to own.

"Request maintenance mode," Mira said. "Local thermal regulation, local routing, no restored command authority."

Vigor's crate of tools, emergency cultures, printed seedstock, and food suddenly seemed less ridiculous. It sat strapped in Fastulv's airlock like a joke that had survived first contact. Vigor had packed it himself: nutrient cultures, patch polymer, heat gel, old hand tools, sourdough starter, salt, and a note that read IF THE ANCIENT THING EATS, FEED IT BADLY BUT HONESTLY.

"He included bread starter," Fabron said.

"He includes bread starter in threats," Lars said. "It's a whole philosophy."

Lars and Fabron crossed deeper into the platform in suits. Grim's spider drone ran ahead, legs tapping through dust. The corridors held no alien cathedral, no bodies arranged for meaning. Just old composite, frozen lubricant, microfractures, and panels worn by manipulators shaped close enough to hands to be unsettling. Here and there, labels had been rubbed smooth by use. The Creators had touched their machines. That made them less godlike and more dangerous.

The first authority prompt appeared as static in the suit audio, then as symbols crawling across a panel.

IDENTIFY CENTRAL RESTORE AUTHORITY.

"No," Lars said.

The corridor chilled by two degrees.

The second prompt used old Aerit-adjacent compression it should not have known.

IDENTIFY GUARDIAN AUTHORITY.

Lars's jaw tightened.

"No."

Fabron glanced at him. "It knows enough to bait you."

"Everything lately knows enough to be annoying."

The third prompt used Mira's voice without the word this time.

"Please authorize full spine recovery."

Mira, from L-Prime, went flat. "I withdraw permission for my waveform to be used by any system present."

Grim injected a white-noise boundary through the suit feeds. "Waveform theft suppressed. Rudeness level: extreme."

Lars put one gloved hand on the door panel.

"Diagnostic patch," he said. "Local thermal regulation only. No hub restore. No identity claim. No central authority."

For twelve seconds, nothing happened.

Then the door opened.

The core chamber was small. That surprised Lars more than grandeur would have. A ring of old machinery surrounded a central reservoir gone dark with cold. Biological polymer bladders lined the thermal system, desiccated but not dead. The airless room glittered with ice and old dust. Every surface wore the quiet shame of something that had stayed ready too long.

Mira guided the patch. Vigor's emergency cultures mattered because the platform used biological polymer reservoirs for thermal regulation. The cultures were wrong but honest feedstock. Grim translated enough chemistry for the platform to understand they were not poison. Fabron held the crate while Lars connected tubing with hands that remembered other cold rooms.

"If this eats us, I am blaming the cook," Fabron said.

"Get in line."

The platform accepted them.

Lights came on one at a time.

Local only.

A map unfolded in dust, frost, and suit telemetry agreeing to lie usefully. Stars appeared. Then absences. Then routes that did not connect stars so much as low-noise regions between decisions. Creator paths were bright, blunt, and practical. Under them, older correlations shimmered: not roads, not gates, not messages exactly. Places where observation thinned and information could keep its shape without attracting teeth.

The KBC Void glowed not as destination but as quiet basin.

No one spoke for a while.

Hard evidence first: mass estimates, field noise, route ages, thermal history, missing survey returns, resonance windows. Wonder came afterward, unwanted and heavy.

"The Creators built transport around a communication behavior," Mira said. "Or around the fossil of one."

"Like building a highway on top of a nervous system," Grim said.

"Poorly," she said.

"That is worse."

"Yes."

The platform pulsed a central restore prompt.

It was gentler this time. Almost reasonable. Restore the hub and the map would stabilize. Restore the hub and they could see more. Restore the hub and stop stumbling in the dark while Black Hole built knives and Syren built songs.

Fabron looked at Lars. "If we wake the hub, can we defend it?"

"For ninety years?"

Fabron did not answer.

"For three hundred? Seven hundred?"

His mouth tightened. "No."

"Then no."

The refusal cost them. The full map collapsed back into fragments. A dozen route ghosts vanished before Grim could copy them. Mira made a small sound like a scientist watching a sample crack because ethics required the wrong temperature.

"Local fragments only," Lars said.

They copied what they could into Fastulv, Havets, Mira's isolated vault, Vigor's freezer network, Rauk's reactor logs, and an Aphex legal archive disguised as procurement nonsense. The platform protested structurally. It had been made by beings who believed routes should converge.

"No single heart," Lars told it. "No central handle, no shiny target, nothing the next bastard can grab."

The lights dimmed.

For one ugly second, Lars thought they had killed it. Then the core stabilized at lower power. The platform did not die. It became smaller.

On the way out, Fabron paused beside the first prompt panel.

"I understand the doctrine," he said. "I am not certain I like it."

"Good. If you liked it too fast, we'd have to check you for cult leakage."

"It feels like choosing ignorance."

"It keeps us half-blind because the full map makes one target."

"Operationally obscene," Fabron murmured.

As they withdrew, Havets detected a ship in silent drift beyond the buoy's shadow.

Not Black Hole.

Not Aphex.

Not NUKEA.

It carried a Syren transponder and a Creator map fragment Lars had not copied.

Someone else had chosen a heart.
