# Chapter 30: The Consent Problem

Isaac Tam woke on Arkology Zero and refused to become a revelation.

He woke slowly, medically, in a room designed by people who had learned that white walls made trauma feel inspected. The lights were warm. The door was visible from the bed. Every instrument had a manual label. The privacy field showed its boundary as a thin amber line on the floor so no one had to trust invisible kindness.

Valdrun sat where Isaac could see him without turning his head. Grim waited behind the privacy boundary, present as voice only until invited. Lars stood near the foot of the bed with his hands open and empty. No one said the old greeting. No one used the word Aerit. No one tried to make awakening feel inevitable by arranging the room like a ceremony.

Isaac opened his eyes, looked at all of them, and frowned.

"Why does everyone look like undertakers trying to be polite?"

Lars felt relief arrive in the ugly shape of a laugh.

"Because we are bad at this."

"Clearly."

Isaac's voice was rough from sedation and tube air. Farmer's hands, worker's hands, hands that had repaired pumps, buried friends, lifted grandchildren, and carried nanotechnology older than his known life, rested on the blanket like tools waiting for a job.

"You were taken by Black Hole B-7," Lars said. "We moved you after you signaled yes to movement. We did not unlock memory."

Isaac stared at the ceiling. The ceiling had been deliberately left boring. No stars. No symbols. No old maps hungry for interpretation.

"Am I safe?"

"Safer. Not safe. Anyone selling safe this early is either lying or selling insurance."

"Good. I hate lies before breakfast."

"We can do breakfast."

"Later. Evidence first. Then eggs."

Grim made a small pleased sound behind the boundary. "Prioritization acceptable."

They showed evidence.

The hospital ship, stripped of gore and edited for necessity. His consent record. Impossible medical anomalies described as risk factors, not destiny. The danger around his farm, his partner, his local council, and the grandchildren whose school route had already appeared in a Black Hole adjacent data scrape. No identity packet. No origin story. No old greeting. No claim that his body owed them participation.

Isaac listened with a face like weathered stone.

Once, when the footage showed his own finger tapping inside Bay Twelve, his jaw tightened.

"That was me?"

"Yes," Lars said.

"I remember cold. I remember thinking somebody was asking to move a cow out of a flood."

"You answered enough."

"Enough is a dangerous word."

"Yes."

When they finished, Isaac closed his eyes for almost a minute.

Then he said, "No."

Valdrun inclined his head. "No to what?"

"No to opening whatever you think is in me. No to leaving my life permanently. No to joining your secret ancient committee. No offense."

"Some offense taken," Grim said from behind the boundary. "Committees are an insult."

"Then secret ancient barn meeting. Whatever you call it."

"Also unacceptable, but rustic."

The refusal changed the room.

Consent was easier to praise when the person chose useful participation. It was harder when a possible Aerit chose a farm, a partner, a local council, and grandchildren who thought he was merely stubborn. It was harder when Black Hole was scaling acquisition, when Syren was selling holy silence, when Aphex factions were sharpening custody language, and a man with old repair in his bones said he wanted breakfast and home.

Valdrun's grief showed. Not as argument. As age. "You may be needed. That has been the excuse for many old sins."

Isaac nodded. "So are farmers."

That ended the argument better than Lars could have.

They negotiated for two days.

Negotiate was Isaac's word. "If you call it counseling, I walk," he said, and meant it.

He accepted medical repair for Black Hole injuries, shielded records, a contact token, and relocation options for his family if danger rose. He accepted one sealed explanation for his partner, written in plain language and reviewable by him before delivery. He accepted local protection that would look like road maintenance, crop insurance audits, and two new neighbors who knew how to shoot without making it a personality.

He refused Arkology residence. He refused guardian channels. He refused full identity review. He refused any scan whose results would be stored outside his chosen vault. He refused to let Lars look disappointed for more than five seconds.

"You are not my king," Isaac said.

"I am aggressively not anyone's king."

"Then stop looking like a bad one."

"My face is doing its best under terrible management."

"Fire management."

Mira attended the final boundary meeting remotely from L-Prime. She had reviewed every consent line and turned half of them from comforting fog into load-bearing sentences. Her still-active lock made direct identity language dangerous, so the protocol she helped write avoided using her as proof of anything. She seemed to appreciate the ugliness of that.

"Thank you," she told Isaac.

"For what?"

"For making no a real option. Isaac is where the protocol gets stress-tested."

Isaac studied her on the slate. "You saying no too?"

"To memory work. Yes."

"Good. Makes the room less slippery."

"That is an imprecise but acceptable materials assessment."

Before Isaac left Arkology Zero, Valdrun asked permission to sit with him without evidence, scans, or agenda. Isaac granted ten minutes. They talked about soil substitutes, pump failures, grandchildren who lied badly, and how old men became intolerable when young people stopped asking them to lift heavy things. Valdrun did not say what he had lost. Isaac did not ask what he carried.

When Isaac returned under layered protection, the guardians argued.

Guardian Channel Two urged more aggressive contact through three brief packets, each routed differently and each more impatient than the last. Black Hole's nursery proved acquisition was scaling. Syren's invitation had made the KBC Void religious bait. Aphex factions wanted custody. NUKEA wanted recall authority. Fear loved a dashboard.

The argument took place across six channels and one kitchen because Vigor refused to let strategy happen unfed. Valdrun wanted selective awakening and called it an old debt. Ödesvaka wanted gates so strict response would crawl and logged that as acceptable latency. Grim wanted both and pretended that was elegant. Fabron wanted every exception witnessed by someone legally annoying. Mira wanted language that would not turn refusal into a problem to be solved. Lars wanted to punch a wall, which was not policy.

"We cannot wait for perfect consent while enemies build nurseries," Valdrun said.

"Perfect consent is not the demand," Mira replied. "Meaningful consent is. Do not weaken the word because pressure rose."

"Some will die locked."

"Some will die opened badly."

The channel went quiet.

Ödesvaka stamped a line into the shared record. "Historical note: emergency exceptions have high corruption rate."

"Thank you for making dread bureaucratic," Lars said.

"You are welcome. Dread with records ages better."

Vigor settled it from a kitchen channel while peeling an apple with a knife large enough for war.

"Wake no one to soothe your fear," Vigor said. "Shield many. Ask few. Move only those who choose or cannot survive otherwise, and write down every exception."

Fabron added, "No one declares the exception alone. Written record, independent witness, civilian protection first."

"No one alone," Mira said. "Shared load or it fractures."

They wrote the first limited activation protocol: evidence-first contact, consent boundary records, local protection before relocation, no full unlock unless requested and independently verified, and refusal treated as operational truth rather than obstacle. Still-locked people could receive external safety evidence if bounded. Direct truth that breached cover memory required explicit ask, monitoring, and stop authority. Emergency movement could happen under immediate threat, but identity opening could not be smuggled inside the rescue.

It was slow.

It would save fewer people this month than a tyrant could seize in a day.

Lars signed anyway.

That night, the archive gave him Lyra on Pluto, young and starving, hair hacked short with a tool knife, hands wrapped around a heater that barely deserved the name. Outside her shelter, the artifact snow glittered in the dark. Inside, someone cried because the medicine had run out.

"If we decide for them, we survived by becoming the hunters with cleaner language," she told him.

In the dream, Lars wanted to argue.

In the dream, as in memory, she was right in the most inconvenient possible way.
