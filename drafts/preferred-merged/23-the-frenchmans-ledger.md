# Chapter 23: The Frenchman's Ledger

Gabriel Arsenault received Lars in a room designed to imply that civilization had been invented by French furniture.

The office sat high inside Aphex Defence's Paris orbital branch, where the windows showed Earth with such tasteful restraint that Lars suspected expensive software was removing the ugly weather. The carpet muffled footsteps. The air smelled of paper, polish, and money pretending not to sweat. A wall of old campaign maps implied continuity. A wall of legal screens implied violence with invoices.

The desk was real wood.

Lars set a greasy paper bag on it. Grim set a second beside the first.

Arsenault stared. "Monsieur Odin, is that food on a seventeenth-century desk?"

"Breakfast."

"It is fourteen hundred hours and the desk has survived wars."

"Calendars are advisory. Wood builds character."

Grim removed a pastry and examined its layers. "This object contains butter in a structurally ambitious configuration."

"It contains crumbs," Arsenault said.

"A predictable failure mode."

The Aphex director had sent three messages since L-Prime. One demanded a report. One offered support. One contained an old NUKEA procurement trail connected to the dead infiltrator's void-aligned burst. Profit, fear, leverage, survival. Lars almost admired the tidiness. Arsenault could make concern look like asset management and still mean part of it.

The official report lay unopened on the desk between pastry grease and inherited empire.

"You called it a containment drill," Arsenault said.

"Fabron did."

"Major Fabron also filed an addendum describing remote command latency as a hostile collaborator."

"Good addendum."

"It has caused an internal review."

"Even better."

Arsenault's smile showed no pleasure. "You have placed my corporation in the uncomfortable position of being correct for the wrong reasons."

"Try not to make a habit of it."

For a moment, the polished room went quiet enough for the ventilation to admit it existed. Lars saw the tiredness under Arsenault's eyes then. Not guilt. Not softness. A man calculating the distance between his appetites and a cliff.

Arsenault opened a wall vault and removed a paper ledger.

It was bound in cracked leather, reinforced along the spine, and ugly with repairs. Real ink. Real tabs. Real fingerprints at the corners. In a room full of encrypted systems, the ledger looked indecently alive.

"Before you begin vandalizing my dignity, note that this survived eight network collapses, three hostile audits, and one divorce."

"My money is on the divorce."

"A correct assessment. Rare from you."

The ledger held shell suppliers, minority investments, rescue grants, military redundancies, and reactor-hardened agricultural habitats sold at a loss under charitable fronts. It also held names: long-lived engineers, impossible survivors, smugglers moving medicine without invoices, soldiers who disobeyed remote orders during system failures and were later protected by someone who should not have known.

Each name had context. A coolant crisis on Abantu Station Twelve. A famine convoy that should have been intercepted and was not. A child immunization program funded through a mining insurance rebate. A maintenance guild that refused a profitable standardization scheme and then survived a cascade that killed three competitors.

Lars turned pages more carefully than he wanted Arsenault to notice.

"You were tracking hidden people," Lars said. "That is a hobby with teeth."

"I was tracking anomalies that correlated with institutional survival," Arsenault said. "Do not flatter yourself by assuming my ignorance lacked rigor."

"You didn't know what you had."

"I knew I had a pattern that made fragile systems less fragile. In my profession, that is a religion if one is poor and a portfolio if one is honest."

The word appeared in seventeen spellings. Återviens as discipline, superstition, guild law, rural etiquette, reactor prayer, mess-hall joke. Sometimes it was a mark beside a supply route. Sometimes a family saying. Sometimes only the repeated habit of refusing to let a single authority hold the only key.

Fabron's Clear Hand appeared twice, once as a charitable maintenance order and once as a minor security nuisance.

"Careful," Lars said. "You wrote down a church with guns."

"Major Fabron would object to the word church."

"He objects professionally. It keeps his blood moving."

Arsenault poured coffee from a silver pot and did not offer any to Grim, who looked offended on behalf of categories.

"I treated Återviens as a useful infection," Arsenault said. "Systems touched by it failed less stupidly. In my profession, that is nearly romance."

"You tried to buy the infection."

"Of course. It resisted purchase, which improved both my admiration and my temper."

"Did you try to own the people?"

The question cut through the furniture.

Arsenault set the cup down. "I have tried to own many things. These, not successfully. Not cleanly enough to pretend otherwise. A scholarship here. A contract there. Protection with invoices attached. Some of my subsidiaries behaved as predators. Some behaved as shields. I am not offering sainthood. I am offering a ledger before someone worse uses a cleaner one."

Lars wanted to hate that answer more. It had the problem of being useful.

Grim slid a page closer. "Low-noise accounts recur near acquisition attempts. Ownership: absent, masked, or deliberately non-person."

"Meaning?"

"Someone besides Aphex made a ledger without Arsenault's furniture."

The final section was worse. Aphex was not the only watcher. NUKEA compliance shells, Syren charities, Black Hole brokers, and low-noise accounts with no owner had circled the same patterns. Someone had mapped the habits without understanding the people inside them. They had noticed where local hands kept systems alive. They had not noticed that the hands mattered more than the pattern.

Arsenault slid a thin recall dossier across the desk.

A NUKEA recall had been moved forward across twelve marginal colonies. One, Havskant, sat near a misfiled portal anomaly and a cluster of ledger names. The recall was packaged as safety. Its funding came through three shells and a public pressure campaign about reactor harmonization. The timing matched the med-bay coordinate burst by seventeen hours.

"The recall is real," Arsenault said. "The timing is engineered. There is a difference, and people with budgets exploit it."

Grim skimmed the recall dossier and stopped chewing pastry.

"The firmware dependency can centralize emergency control across all twelve reactor governors. Pastry enjoyment suspended."

"NUKEA promised that was impossible."

"They lied, or they employed optimism as engineering. I object to both categories."

Arsenault leaned forward, theater dropping. "Aphex central contains factions that would put Dr. Sovan in a sealed lab and invoice it as protection. NUKEA contains factions that would license survival by the quarter. Black Hole would harvest it. Syren would compose hymns over the corpse. I am greedy, Monsieur Odin, not suicidal."

"I am moved. Mostly toward nausea."

"Practical," Arsenault said. "Help me make Aphex useful before Aphex becomes the sort of cancer that insists it is an immune system."

Lars looked out at Earth. The planet below had survived empires, plagues, Rakh'Na, and humans discovering new ways to call old mistakes strategy. Somewhere under the clouds, old Aerit decisions had become village habits, soldier discipline, reactor checklists, and soup before speeches. Not enough to save everyone. Enough to annoy predators.

"Mira stays out of your custody," Lars said.

"Agreed."

"Fabron keeps local authority over L-Prime security."

"He will become intolerable."

"He already is. Builds character."

"You ask much for a man eating on my desk."

"I'm about to ask more. You will leak the right amount of fear into NUKEA channels. Not Aerit. Not KBC wonder. Just boring engineering liability."

Arsenault's eyes sharpened. "Boring fear travels farther in committees."

"Exactly. Make them afraid of being sued by physics."

"Physics is a poor litigant but an excellent executioner."

Lars took the recall dossier. "Fine. But if I catch you harvesting anomalies, I turn this desk into a canoe."

Arsenault looked genuinely pained. "That threat is more effective than you deserve."

"Motivate yourself, Frenchly."

At the door, Lars paused. The oldest page lay open behind him. In the margin beside an old Pluto rumor entry, someone had copied a three-line translation from a much older source. It was not a message arriving now, and not handwriting Lars recognized. It was archival marginalia: a clerk, ancestor, or early Aphex watcher trying to translate a fragment of Återviens-adjacent language first into French, then into trade English. The corrections were messy because the writer had clearly understood the danger: translate too grandly and people would build a religion; translate too small and they would miss the warning.

Not gods. Not owners.

Old engineers passing through.

Lars left before the line could make him feel anything useful.
