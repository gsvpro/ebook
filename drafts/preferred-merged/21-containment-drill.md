# Chapter 21: Containment Drill

The three incoming ships arrived like bad decisions wearing stealth plating.

They came out of the brown dwarf's dirty light without a clean drive plume, sliding between particle gusts and sensor blind folds. L-Prime 41 was a mining dependency, not a fortress. Its outer rings carried materials labs, rotating crew berths, refinery blisters, and repair yards that had grown in layers around whatever Aphex had underfunded last decade. The whole station smelled, through the filters, of hot metal, old coolant, and too many people trying not to breathe loudly.

Mira saw the ships first as fatigue signatures.

Their hulls flexed against the wake in three incompatible rhythms: built, grown, and stolen, forced into one fleet by someone who believed maintenance was weakness. The stress display did not show enemies. It showed bad joins, dishonest composite, living tissue welded to factory frame, and a third hull that looked normal only if you trusted surfaces more than load.

"Black Hole," Lars said. "That hull is lying with both hands."

"Director, hull stress is not a legal identification method," Fabron snapped over the tactical channel.

"She reads bones. I insult criminals. Division of labor."

Mira did not look away from the console. The materials lab had become a local sensor station, not a command center, because her instruments were better at telling structural truth than Aphex's command software. If she was Aerit, she was still locked: no old Aerit channels, no Arkology feed, no hidden history except the few current facts she had consented to hold. Two soldiers had wedged a table against the door. Someone's forgotten tea floated in a sealed bulb beside a rack of impact coupons. The emergency lights painted the room red every four seconds.

Grim's boundary monitor stayed green: current hull behavior only, no blood, no identity, no memory language.

"Still inside scope," Mira said, before anyone could ask.

"Logged," Grim replied. "I am not enjoying restraint, but I am performing it."

Fabron's voice came tight through the channel. "All local detachments, containment posture. Manual confirmation only. If central sends contradictory instructions, you will ask me before obeying the distant idiot."

"Major," an Aphex lieutenant said, scandalized and relieved at once, "that phrasing is not regulation."

"Then remember the meaning and forget the phrasing."

Fastulv detached from Dock Two with every running light bright, every transponder loud, and its visible weapons arranged like an elderly freighter trying very hard to look suicidal. Lars wanted the hostile ships to see the wolf. He wanted them to believe all the old violence was outside, rusty and theatrical, away from the station's soft blood.

Havets Sista Sång stayed absent from Aphex sensors, a hidden pressure behind the station's skin.

The old ship did not announce itself. It slid its attention through maintenance relays, hand overrides, chapel kitchen circuits, station guild tags, and old local caches Fabron's Clear Hand people had kept as ritual without knowing why. On one deck, a cook saw a freezer light blink twice and closed the manual fire doors before the remote panel woke. In a refinery crawlspace, a pipefitter felt the vibration pattern change and spun a brass wheel his apprentice had been told never to touch unless the air tasted like pennies.

Doors stopped obeying remote orders.

Shutters accepted hand-cranked authority.

Emergency lights pulsed in a rhythm workers understood before anyone explained it: isolate, verify, repair.

"Your station is committing disobedience in an organized manner," Grim said.

Fabron exhaled. "Good."

The first hostile launched a sampling drone toward the materials ring. Not a missile. A needle with engines. It skipped around point-defense prediction, cold until the last instant, then opened a throat lined with soft black vanes.

Mira's fingers tightened on the edge of the console. "Sampling throat opening along the warmest pressure boundary. It wants atmosphere first, blood second. Point defense is still waiting for some remote idiot to bless local physics."

"Remote safety authority has achieved traditional failure," Grim said. "I am logging it under civilization, recurring defects."

"Fastulv," Lars said.

The freighter rolled, dumped two empty cargo pods, and heated them just enough to look like panicked evacuation mistakes. The drone chose the warmer target. Fastulv's belly turret cut it into vapor, and the shock flashed white across the lab windows before the shutters came down.

People cheered in three rooms. Fabron killed that with one word.

"Focus."

The second hostile learned faster. It kept its distance and extended a longer filament probe, thin enough to hide in the sensor noise, aiming for the materials ring's intake baffles. If it reached the air system, it could sample every crew member in the lab deck before anyone bled.

Mira leaned closer to her board.

"I can make the ring look injured," she said. "Cracked braces, phantom torsion, heat gradients wrong enough to invite a greedy system."

"Risk to station?" Fabron asked.

"If I do it badly, we lose a brace and three labs. If I do it well, it opens its mouth."

"Do it well," Lars said.

Mira gave him a look sharp enough to score alloy.

"By permission," he corrected. "Your call."

Her jaw worked once. "My call. Grim, give me the clean sensor lanes and stop decorating them."

"My labels are informative."

"Your labels are noise."

"Noise removed. Emotion noted and categorized as justified irritation."

Mira fed the second hostile a false song of cracked braces, phantom torsion, and impossible heat gradients. It was not ancient instinct. It was a dirty certification trick turned inside out by a woman who had spent decades catching lies in beams, cradles, and reactor casings. It took three long minutes. The station groaned for real in places that did not know they were lying. A child cried over an open channel until someone hushed the line. Lars stood in the docking throat to Fastulv, helmet sealed, Ragnarrök inert at his thigh, and waited for the enemy to believe in an injury.

It opened a sampling throat wider.

Havets touched the probe.

No beam appeared. No miracle filled the glass. The probe's control loops simply forgot how to agree with each other. The throat clenched, opened, clenched again, and spat its own steering filament into the brown dwarf wake. The hostile ship slewed sideways, exposed its flank, and Fastulv fired a shot that cut grown tissue away from stolen frame. The frame spun away trailing atmosphere, coolant, and red-black droplets that boiled into nothing.

The third hostile ran.

Fabron ordered pursuit. Lars countermanded it before Fastulv could burn.

"It will report our response posture," Fabron said. "That is an operational exposure."

"Yes. It will report that Fastulv is the loud problem, Aphex has teeth when adults ignore headquarters, and Dr. Sovan remains protected by consent instead of stolen open."

On the lab screen, the third ship dwindled into wake clutter.

Mira looked up from the sensor panel. She had a smear of blood under one nostril where stress and recycled air had punished her, and she seemed angry at the blood for being imprecise. "Useful?"

The word landed badly. Too many people had already used possible Aerits like tools.

"Bad word," Lars said. "By current evidence. By your permission. Nothing else."

That softened nothing. It did draw a boundary around the word.

Fabron's soldiers began the ugly part: counting injuries, opening stuck doors, convincing frightened technicians that a drill could bruise like war. One refinery apprentice had a broken wrist from sealing a manual valve. A med tech vomited in a corridor after seeing what the sampling drone had tried to become. Lars passed two Aphex officers arguing with central command and heard Fabron's voice through their speaker, cold enough to frost law.

"You may file disciplinary review after you explain why your remote authorization queue attempted to outvote a visible hostile sampler."

Then the med bay alarm screamed.

The captured infiltrator from the previous attack had been held in a sealed coffin under three kinds of quarantine. It died inside the box without anyone touching it. The body folded inward, flesh and black particulate collapsing into inert grit. For half a second the coffin glass showed the shape of a face that had not belonged to the body.

The med archive lit with unauthorized traffic.

"Stop it," Lars said.

"Attempting," Grim replied. His voice lost all humor. "It prepared this death as transmission event."

Havets closed three archive routes. Fabron's med guards physically cut two more. Mira, from the lab, spotted a stress spike in the archive server housing and ordered someone to kick the cooling manifold until the casing cracked.

"That is not tactical doctrine," Fabron said.

"It is thermal doctrine," Mira snapped. "Kick the manifold."

A corporal obeyed with professional disbelief.

The burst still got through.

Not all of it. Enough.

Black particulate collapsed into grit on the coffin floor. The med bay smelled of scorched antiseptic and old blood. No one spoke until Grim decoded the first layer.

Not a name.

Not a rendezvous.

A coordinate family aligned with the KBC Void.

Lars looked at the dead grit and felt the shape of the next problem.

"Containment drill," Fabron said, as if testing the lie he would have to sell.

"Yeah," Lars said. "We contained almost nothing. Great drill."
