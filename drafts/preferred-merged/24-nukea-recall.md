# Chapter 24: NUKEA Recall

Havskant Colony had been built by people who distrusted planets.

It hung inside the broken magnetosphere of a dead ocean world: twelve rotating habitats lashed to a modular reactor spine. Algae farms on one side. Salvage yard on the other. The ocean below had boiled away centuries ago and left salt flats bright enough to hurt cameras. Charged dust rose from the dead basins in slow curtains. It licked the colony's shields and made long-range telemetry stutter like a liar.

The colony looked improvised because it was. Docking collars had different manufacturers. Habitat paint came in six eras of fading. Every corridor had handholds polished by use and hand-lettered arrows added by people who did not trust emergency lighting to survive emergencies. It survived because improvisation had become culture before management could forbid it.

NUKEA's recall ship arrived wearing polished certainty.

Fastulv arrived wearing rust, dents, and a declared cargo of preserved lemons.

"Why lemons?" Grim asked. "They are structurally unsuited to diplomacy."

"Vigor said colonies under stress need acid, salt, and stories."

"That is not a recognized logistics category."

"It is if your logistics include actual people."

The cargo bay smelled of citrus brine, old socks, and weapon lubricant. Fabron stared at the stacked jars as if they were evidence in a trial.

"I am beginning to suspect Mr. Hallman runs a supply network disguised as hospitality."

"Beginning?" Lars said. "Major, catch up."

The recall team wanted to replace twelve local reactor governors with harmonized firmware. Sales called it resilience. Architecture called it obedience. One crisis key could put every reactor on the same emergency authority chain.

On a rich station, that would have been convenience. On Havskant, where a dust storm could make one habitat blind while the next saw clearly, it was a loaded gun passed around in the dark.

Chief Engineer Imani Rauk hated it before Lars arrived.

She met them in Habitat Four's transfer lock with a wrench in one hand and a baby strapped to her back. The baby slept through the pressure equalization with the offended confidence of someone born near alarms. Rauk's coveralls had scorch marks on both knees. Her hair was shaved on one side where a coolant flare had taken liberties.

"You are the Aphex nuisance," Rauk said.

"Board director, captain, cultural treasure."

"Nuisance fits on a work order."

"Use small font."

Rauk had already delayed the recall by losing forms, misplacing adapters, requiring heritage compatibility tests, and scheduling a mandatory feast for visiting technicians. Vigor's influence was obvious. Half the colony fed the NUKEA team until they became too polite and sleepy to centralize anything. A grandmother named Saela had cornered two firmware engineers with pickled kelp and a three-hour story about the last time central control saved money by nearly killing everyone.

Delay bought them one day.

It did not buy them safety.

One test reactor had accepted the firmware.

They reached Reactor Three through a maintenance crawl that ran warm along the spine. The corridor vibrated with the colony's pulse: pumps, flywheels, coolant, people living because boring machines kept promises. A painted sign above the access hatch read LOCAL HANDS, LOCAL LOADS. Someone had added underneath: AND NO CLEVER CLOUD KEYS.

The NUKEA supervisor, Vela Kint, stood beside the governor rack in a suit too clean for the room.

"The upgrade passed vendor validation," she said.

Rauk snorted. "So does powdered soup."

"Chief Engineer, with respect, this recall was mandated after seventeen incidents across comparable colonies."

"Comparable colonies have prettier brochures and worse mechanics."

Grim found the hidden dependency in six minutes. Mira, remote from L-Prime and still refusing memory work, confirmed the structural risk in four. Her face appeared on a rugged wall slate, lit by her lab's blue monitors and exhaustion she refused to name.

"The firmware treats contested telemetry as a reason to escalate authority," she said. "Havskant's magnetosphere makes contested telemetry normal. That is not resilience. That is a fatigue crack under decorative paint."

Fabron translated it into military terms that made Kint sweat.

"A hostile actor with emergency-key access could scram every reactor in sequence," Fabron said. "Alternatively, they could hold them online past local failure limits. Both outcomes are operationally obscene."

Kint objected. She had a binder full of approvals and the brittle courage of a competent person handed bad orders. Lars let her use three sentences, then put the dead infiltrator's coordinate burst on the wall.

"Someone is looking for quiet colonies with unusual survivability patterns," Lars said. "Your recall turns twelve independent failures into one emergency key. One key means one person can turn it."

"Told them," Rauk said. "Used smaller words, too."

The solution had to look bureaucratic. If Lars simply overrode NUKEA with Aphex pressure, the next supervisor would arrive with lawyers and a cleaner knife. The halt had to grow from Havskant's own records.

So they built a wall out of boredom.

Fabron found a colonial safety clause requiring manual governor compatibility during contested telemetry. Rauk produced three generations of maintenance logs proving contested telemetry was normal near the dead world's magnetosphere. Saela provided handwritten incident meals. These turned out to be dinner rosters tied to previous reactor anomalies because Havskant fed repair crews by crisis type. Grim generated a compliance simulation so boring it could kill ambition at twenty meters. Mira annotated the failure model in language sharp enough to cut marketing.

Kint read it twice.

"This will make my superiors furious," she said.

"Good," Rauk said. "Let them be furious on paper instead of freezing us in practice."

"I did not design the dependency."

"Then help us bury it before it breeds."

Kint looked at the sleeping baby on Rauk's back, then at the vibrating reactor wall. Her shoulders dropped by one centimeter.

"Temporary halt pending local anomaly review," she said.

"Write permanent in pencil," Lars advised. "It confuses cowards."

The recall halted.

For six hours, Havskant celebrated by pretending not to. Technicians removed one test firmware image. NUKEA staff ate lemon stew under protest and asked for seconds. Children ran messages through corridors because the grown-ups had decided official comms were emotionally contaminated. Rauk finally handed the baby to Fabron while she crawled under a governor rack.

Fabron held the child like unexploded ordinance.

"Support the head," Rauk said.

"I am supporting everything."

"Babies are not chain of command, Major. They wiggle."

Then Black Hole tried to buy the halted hardware.

The broker arrived on a salvage shuttle with clean nails, a dead smile, and a contract offering three times market rate for removed governor modules. He cared nothing for reactor details. He cared about serial numbers, test exposure, and whether any "long-cycle local operators" had handled the units directly.

Rauk served fish stew.

Lars sat in the corner pretending to be drunk. Fabron stood where he could see the broker's hands. Grim occupied the room's old service drone and complained privately about grease on its wheels.

"Your private client is generous," Rauk said.

"My client values obsolete safety equipment," the broker replied.

"No one values obsolete safety equipment except museums and murderers."

"Chief Engineer, there is no need for hostility."

Lars lifted his head from the corner. "Does your private client bleed black glitter when annoyed?"

The broker's wrist opened.

The filament sampler inside launched toward the baby, not because the child was special, but because Black Hole had learned that families made people stupid and fast.

Rauk hit the table with her wrench hard enough to make every plate jump. The baby woke and screamed. Fabron drew first because his body respected procedure faster than thought. Grim sealed the room and announced, "Dining environment reclassified as hostile." Lars crossed the space and drove the Ragnarrök's inactive hilt through the sampler's base before the filament crossed the soup steam.

The broker tried to bite through his own tongue. Havets, listening through a freezer compressor two decks away, shut down the implant in his jaw with a quiet click.

They kept him alive long enough to learn Black Hole was not hunting Aerits by name. It was hunting distributed resilience patterns: colonies that refused central failure, people who lived too long, networks that repaired before worship. The broker had no theology. He had target categories and price tiers.

That was worse in some ways.

Afterward, Rauk sat on the floor with the baby against her chest and her wrench across her knees. Kint, pale and shaking, signed the halt order with both hands.

"Your recall nearly gave them a handle," Rauk said.

"I know."

"Knowing is the cheap part."

"Then tell me the expensive part."

Rauk looked toward the reactor spine. "Help us make the halt stick when your bosses remember profit."

By station evening, children painted new labels on reactor panels.

LOCAL HANDS FIRST.

No one explained the phrase as doctrine. No one needed to. A habit practiced in paint and grease would outlive a speech, if enough people kept touching it.
