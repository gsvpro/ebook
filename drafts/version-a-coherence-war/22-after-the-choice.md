# Chapter 22: After the Choice

Mira did not choose memory.

She chose a room with one door, two exits she could verify herself, a chair bolted to the floor, and access to raw sensor logs from the attack. She chose coffee that tasted like boiled insulation because she did not trust anything luxurious after a boarding action. She chose to keep her name, her job title, her files, and the right to tell Lars Odin to shut up.

Lars accepted all of it.

Fabron recorded the consent boundary with the solemnity of a treaty. Grim built the ledger in three places: Mira's own encrypted key, an Arkology Zero archive shard, and an Aphex legal shell so boring that anyone trying to read it would need two assistants and a spiritual crisis.

"No memory work," Mira said.

"No memory work," Lars agreed.

"No words in old languages unless I ask."

"Agreed."

"No relocating me by force."

Fabron glanced at the cracked wall, the emergency crews, the med bay where a Black Hole probe had killed itself. "Doctor, there are circumstances under which evacuation becomes—"

"No relocating me by force," she repeated.

Fabron shut his mouth.

Lars said, "If the hull is open and you are unconscious, we carry you. Otherwise no."

Mira considered that with the fairness of an engineer inspecting a weld. "Acceptable."

Only then did she ask to see the hostile's remains.

The med bay smelled of sterilizer, burnt metal, and fear. The thing had left little biology. Black Hole craft preferred denial when capture threatened. Grim's sampling drones found protein fragments, trace Aphex armor polymers, growth scaffold residue, and something worse: nanotech designed not to repair tissue but to keep it legally alive through abuse.

Mira watched the models build in the air. Her face did not soften. "They are not trying to understand the bloodwork. They are trying to industrialize it."

"Yes," Lars said.

"Badly."

"Also yes."

"That makes them more dangerous, not less."

Lars looked at her then, because that was exactly the sentence an Aerit would say and exactly the sentence a good human engineer would say. The distinction mattered less than the practice.

Fabron's comm lit with Arsenault's delayed reply. The Aphex chairman appeared as a tight-beamed image, immaculate except for the eyes. He had been awake too long.

"Director Odin," Arsenault said, "I am told my dependency has suffered a terrorist penetration, a classified board asset has used undeclared force, and Major Fabron has locked me out of three command channels."

Fabron stood straighter. "Temporarily."

"Major, I selected you because you were reliable."

"I remain reliable. You may dislike the object of reliability."

Lars smiled. Mira did not, but the corner of her mouth remembered the idea.

Arsenault looked from face to face, then stopped pretending this was only about property. "What did they want?"

"Blood," Grim said.

The chairman went still.

Lars stepped into the silence. "And response data. They know Aphex has unusual people in its databases. They know some are hidden in ordinary jobs. They are mapping who protects whom."

"How many?"

"We do not know."

"That is not reassuring."

"I charge extra for reassuring."

Arsenault's image flickered as the secure channel rerouted around damaged relays. "You will share evidence."

"We will share enough to prevent stupidity."

"That is not a corporate category."

"It should be."

Mira interrupted before the old men could convert disaster into posture. "The attack used an Aphex thrust cage. Your salvage market is leaking restricted frames. Your medical metadata is compromised. Your emergency response net accepts remote authority too easily. If you centralize all countermeasures under board secrecy, Black Hole will only need one successful penetration next time."

Arsenault stared at her.

Fabron looked almost proud.

Lars felt the old doctrine move through the room without anyone naming it. No single heart. No rescue that became ownership. Repair before worship.

Mira chose partial protection.

She would remain Mira Sovan, materials fatigue specialist, officially assigned to post-attack structural investigation. Unofficially, she would have access to a hardened contact protocol, a local Aphex guard detail under Fabron, and a hidden channel to Grim that she could sever at any time. She refused relocation to Arkology Zero. She refused memory work. She accepted one blood scan, performed by herself, with Lars and Aphex receiving only derived safety models.

The scan showed active nanotech.

Mira sat very still when the image resolved: not magic, not divinity, not destiny, but machines smaller than cells maintaining her, hiding her, and waiting under rules she had never agreed to because another version of her might have agreed centuries before.

"I do not know how to feel about this," she said.

"Good," Valdrun said through Lars's private channel, forgetting only Lars could hear him.

Lars winced.

Mira noticed. "Someone else is listening."

Lars closed his eyes. "Yes."

"One of yours?"

"Yes."

"Tell them they are rude."

Valdrun said, "She is correct."

Lars said aloud, "They agree."

Mira looked at the scan again. "I am not joining your ancient whatever."

"Good," Lars said. "We are mostly bad at meetings."

Outside, repair crews sealed the sensor boom. Fastulv remained docked, loud and visible. Havets Sista Sång withdrew into silence with three captured wake fragments and a model of the enemy's timing. Fabron sent messages on Aphex channels and one old private channel he pretended not to have.

By station dawn, the attack had become an audit incident, a salvage leak, a reactor safety concern, and three incompatible classified reports.

That was enough confusion to survive.

For now.
