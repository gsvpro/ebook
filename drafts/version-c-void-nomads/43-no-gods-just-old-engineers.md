# Chapter 43: No Gods, Just Old Engineers

The first person to kneel to Lars did it in a docking corridor beside a broken vending machine.

He was a mechanic from a pilgrim transport diverted away from First Door by Vigor's food-and-safety blockade. Young, exhausted, wearing a Syren thread around one wrist and grease under his nails. When he recognized Lars, his face changed with unbearable relief.

Then he dropped to one knee.

Lars reacted badly.

"Get up."

The mechanic flinched.

Fabron, behind Lars, made a warning sound. Too late.

"I said get up," Lars snapped. "I am not your altar, your door, your proof, or your excuse to stop thinking. Stand up and check that vending machine. It is making a noise like it wants to kill children."

The mechanic stared.

The vending machine clicked ominously.

Grim tilted his head. "It is indeed unsafe."

That saved the moment. The mechanic stood, confused and crying, then opened the service panel. Inside, a power regulator had scorched half through its casing. Five minutes later he was repairing it while Lars held a flashlight and felt like the galaxy's least qualified counselor.

"I only wanted to thank you," the mechanic said.

"Then thank me by not kneeling."

"What should I do instead?"

Lars looked at the exposed regulator. "Learn why it burned. Teach someone else. Refuse anyone who says the Void wants your blood. Eat before making cosmic decisions."

The mechanic laughed through tears.

Fabron watched with a strange expression.

"What?" Lars asked afterward.

"That was pastoral care by assault."

"Worked, didn't it?"

"Theologically alarming. Operationally sound."

The incident spread because everything spread. The Refuser nickname grew teeth. Syren splintered over it. Some called Lars proof that true messengers denied themselves. Others called him enemy of transcendence. A smaller, more useful group began calling for repair vigils: public maintenance work instead of pilgrimage.

Vigor amplified that mercilessly.

"Bring tools, not candles," his kitchens posted.

Madame Fabron added, "Candles acceptable if power fails."

Mira, who had become unwilling patron saint of people refusing sainthood, sent Lars a message: Your anti-cult has terrible branding.

He replied: Good.

The hard conversations continued.

In a private council, Valdrun admitted he had wanted Lars to become central because he was tired. Six centuries of watchfulness made command look merciful. Ödesvaka logged the admission without mockery. Grim admitted he sometimes preferred central models because distributed emotion was difficult to parse. Vigor admitted he liked being useful enough that he occasionally mistook usefulness for permission. Fabron admitted his order could become exactly what it opposed if fear made ritual rigid.

Mira listened, then said, "I am afraid that if I ask for more memory, I will lose the life I built without it."

No one answered quickly.

Finally Lars said, "You might lose parts. You might gain parts. You might decide not to ask. All of those are allowed."

"Even if you need me?"

"Especially then."

Her eyes shone. She looked away, angry at water.

The Nomad hypothesis expanded beyond them. Not publicly, but through trusted fragments. Scientists who could handle uncertainty received narrow datasets. Maintenance guilds received practical safety guidance. Military cells received anti-centralization protocols. Farmers received timing patterns disguised as weather work. Everyone got only what they needed and no one got the whole throne.

Black Hole responded by accelerating toward First Door with a biological processor fleet.

Syren's largest faction announced a synchronized silence ceremony timed to the same window.

Aphex central prepared an emergency fleet despite Arsenault's stalling.

NUKEA quietly moved power ships "for safety."

Every pressure line converged.

Lars stood aboard Havets and looked at the KBC basin map. The scale was obscene. Civilizations hiding by dimming themselves. Older engineers spread across field states. Creator ruins like abandoned transit mistakes. Humanity and its cousins shouting in the dark with weapons and sermons.

He accepted the scale.

He refused the interpretation.

"No gods," he said.

Grim waited.

"Just old engineers," Lars continued. "And living people who still have to fix the vending machines."
