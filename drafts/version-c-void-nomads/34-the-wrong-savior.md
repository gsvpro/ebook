# Chapter 34: The Wrong Savior

By morning, Lars Odin had saved the galaxy, doomed the galaxy, revealed the gods, murdered transcendence, exposed Aphex, concealed Aphex, and become the Herald of the Quiet Void.

According to the feeds.

None of that was true, which did not slow anyone down.

The Syren station had leaked partial recordings before Grim killed the broadcast. Viewers saw Lars under the cupola, Door Nine burning behind him, Mother Pell accusing him of denying the opening, and the lights failing as an invisible power intervened. They did not see the phase math, the broken microphones, the civilians moving in local cells, or Fabron threatening a panicking convert with court-martial despite the convert not being in any army.

The wrong savior was born in six hours.

Arsenault called. "Congratulations. You are now a market category."

"I will become a crime scene."

"Also likely."

Aphex wanted to capitalize. NUKEA wanted distance. Syren wanted martyr language. Black Hole wanted revenge and samples. Independent colonies wanted to know whether the KBC Void was safe, holy, profitable, or about to eat their children.

Lars wanted coffee and a small asteroid to hide under.

Mira refused three interview requests, two medical reviews, and one polite invitation from Aphex central to visit a secure wellness facility that everyone involved knew was a prison with better towels.

Fabron made the refusal official by citing post-attack stress, audit privilege, and a clause about expert witness contamination so obscure that Arsenault sent him a single message: Beautiful. Never do this to me.

Vigor responded by feeding reporters.

Not bribing. Feeding. Hungry people asked better questions for about twenty minutes after soup. In those twenty minutes, Vigor's network seeded the stabilizing lie: Door Nine had been an illegally activated ancient transport hazard; Director Odin and Aphex personnel had prevented a mass-casualty event; rumors of divine communication were Syren manipulation; local emergency doctrine saved lives.

True enough to hold.

False enough to hurt.

Lars watched Pail, the young Syren sympathizer from Vigor's table, appear in a public clip. He had been at the station. He had helped carry injured converts after the song broke.

"I thought silence meant surrender," Pail told the interviewer, shaking. "But the people who saved us kept telling us to check seals and carry water. Maybe that is what listening means. I don't know. I don't know anything."

That clip spread farther than any official statement.

Mother Pell survived and vanished into custody under a legal arrangement so tangled that three governments could claim jurisdiction and none could safely interrogate her alone. Good. Single custody would become single corruption.

The Creator fragment Syren had carried did not vanish. Grim and Mira extracted it from the station wreckage, wrapped it in isolation, and learned that it contained partial route data from beyond Door Nine.

Not to a planet.

To a network of dead portals aligned around low-observation basins, including but not limited to the KBC Void.

"Subway map," Grim said. "Drawn by someone who misunderstood walking."

"Creators," Mira said.

She looked exhausted. Her consent boundary had held, but the field analysis had brushed old memory hard enough to leave residue: dreams of corridors, unfamiliar hands, a child laughing in a language she woke up knowing how to mourn but not speak.

She did not ask for more.

Lars did not offer.

Fabron visited him in Fastulv's cargo bay after the final debrief. He carried his paper notebook.

"My order is mobilizing," he said.

"That sounds central."

"It is not. Local cells. Chapels, maintenance guilds, military fraternities, disaster kitchens. Many thought they were alone. They are discovering siblings."

"Dangerous."

"Yes. Also necessary."

Lars nodded.

Fabron hesitated. "Some of them have begun calling you the Refuser."

"Absolutely not."

"I told them you would hate it. That helped."

"I will shoot the nickname."

"Nicknames are famously bulletproof."

For once, Lars had no answer.

Later, alone except for Grim pretending not to monitor his emotional state, Lars opened the Creator map fragment. The data did not explain. It invited experiment. Dead portals. Failed models. Field correlations. Lyra's impossible residue. The KBC Void not as throne, mouth, or heaven, but as quiet engineering on a cosmic scale.

The book of the galaxy had not opened to a final page.

It had opened to a maintenance manual written by beings who had made terrible assumptions about older machinery.

Lars smiled despite everything.

"No gods," he said.

Grim looked up. "Just old engineers?"

"And younger idiots with tools."

"I feel represented."
