# Chapter 24: NUKEA Recall

Havskant Colony had been built by people who distrusted planets.

It hung inside the broken magnetosphere of a dead ocean world, twelve rotating habitats lashed to a modular reactor spine, with algae farms on one side and a salvage yard on the other. It looked improvised because it was. It survived because improvisation had become culture before management could forbid it.

NUKEA's recall ship arrived wearing polished certainty.

Fastulv arrived wearing rust, dents, and a declared cargo of preserved lemons.

"Why lemons?" Grim asked.

"Because Vigor said colonies under stress need acid, salt, and stories."

"That is not a logistics category."

"It is if you are good at logistics."

The recall team wanted to replace twelve local reactor governors with a harmonized firmware package. The sales language called it resilience. The architecture called it obedience. One crisis key could put every reactor on the same emergency authority chain.

Lars hated it immediately.

So did the colony's chief engineer, a square woman named Imani Rauk who greeted him with a wrench in one hand and a baby strapped to her back.

"You are the Aphex nuisance," she said.

"Board director, captain, cultural treasure."

"Nuisance is shorter."

"Fair."

Rauk had already delayed the recall by losing forms, misplacing adapters, and scheduling a mandatory feast for visiting technicians. Vigor's influence was obvious. Half the colony appeared to be feeding the NUKEA team until they became too polite and sleepy to centralize anything.

But delay was not enough. The firmware had already entered one test reactor.

Grim found the hidden dependency in six minutes. Mira, remote from L-Prime and still refusing memory work, confirmed the structural risk in four. Fabron, assigned by Arsenault as liaison and pretending not to enjoy fieldwork, translated the risk into military terms that made the NUKEA supervisor sweat.

"A hostile actor with emergency-key access could scram every reactor in sequence," Fabron said. "Or keep them online past local failure limits."

The supervisor tried to object. Lars let her for three sentences, then put the dead infiltrator's coordinate burst on the wall.

"Someone is looking for quiet colonies with unusual survivability patterns," he said. "Your recall turns twelve independent failures into one handle."

Rauk spat into a drain. "Told them."

"You did," Grim said. "Your memo was excellent and ignored in a traditional manner."

The solution had to look bureaucratic, because a dramatic victory would attract the wrong eyes. Lars did not seize the recall ship. He did not reveal Aerits, Arkology Zero, or Havets. He did what every empire feared more than rebellion.

He made paperwork locally correct.

Fabron found a colonial safety clause requiring manual governor compatibility during contested telemetry. Rauk produced three generations of maintenance logs proving contested telemetry was normal near the dead ocean world's magnetosphere. Grim generated a compliance simulation so boring it could kill ambition at twenty meters. Lars signed as Aphex board director that Aphex weapons warranties would be void if NUKEA centralized local reactor authority without third-party review.

Arsenault, contacted in the middle of his dinner, backed the threat with visible reluctance and invisible delight.

The recall halted.

Then Black Hole tried to buy the halted hardware.

Not openly. A salvage broker offered three times market value for the recalled governors. Rauk accepted the meeting, served fish stew, and let Lars sit in the corner pretending to be drunk. The broker was too smooth, too clean, and too unconcerned with reactor details.

"Why do you want obsolete governors?" Rauk asked.

"Research resale."

"To whom?"

"Private client."

Lars lifted his head. "Does the private client bleed black glitter when annoyed?"

The broker's left pupil contracted wrong.

Fabron had his pistol out before the broker reached for anything. Rauk hit the table with her wrench. Grim locked the room doors. The broker's skin opened at the wrist, releasing a filament sampler toward the baby on Rauk's back.

Lars moved without the old headache, without mystical certainty, without any clean answer from the universe. He simply crossed the room and drove the Ragnarrök's inactive hilt through the sampler's base.

The broker screamed in a human voice and something underneath it screamed in math.

They took it alive for eighteen minutes.

Long enough to learn that Black Hole was not hunting individual Aerits by name. It was hunting distributed resilience patterns. Colonies that refused central failure. People who lived too long. Networks that repaired before worship.

Long enough to learn that Havskant's portal anomaly was older than the colony and misfiled as gravitational debris.

Afterward, Rauk stood beside Lars on the observation deck, the dead ocean world turning below them.

"You brought trouble," she said.

"Trouble was already en route. I brought lemons."

She snorted.

Behind them, colony children painted new labels on reactor panels: LOCAL HANDS FIRST.

Not doctrine. Not revelation.

A habit that might outlive everyone in the room.
