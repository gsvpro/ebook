# Chapter 29: Black Hole Nursery

The nursery was hidden inside a hospital ship that had never treated a patient.

Its registry claimed disaster relief. Its hull carried faded charity markings. Its cryo bays held no refugees, only growth racks, marrow vats, neural scaffolds, and refrigerated blood sorted by anomaly probability.

Black Hole B-7 had learned to farm what it could not understand.

Lars stood in the observation duct above the main chamber and felt old anger arrive without the old neurological lock. This was not migraine. This was memory with teeth.

Fabron lay beside him in infiltration armor, silent for once. Grim occupied the ship's internal maintenance system, whispering through their implants. Havets watched from outside the hospital ship's shadow, holding Fastulv in reserve like a visible mistake waiting to happen.

"Twenty-six living donors," Grim said. "Most ordinary by Aerit standards. Some long-lived. Some simply resilient in ways Black Hole overvalues."

"Any hidden Aerits?" Lars asked.

A pause.

"One possible. Locked. Unconscious. Consent unavailable."

There it was. The rule under pressure.

Fabron's jaw tightened. "If we do not wake them, they may die."

"If we force it," Lars said, "we may kill who they are trying to remain."

"That is a hard line to hold in a slaughterhouse."

"Yes."

They moved.

Fabron's soldiers breached the lower dock under cover of a sanitation alert Grim had upgraded into a full biohazard panic. Vigor's smugglers cut external clamps. Mira, remote, fed structural weak points to Aphex assault teams without leaving L-Prime. Rauk from Havskant had sent jury-rigged reactor dampers that made the hospital ship's power spine stutter like an old drunk.

Lars entered the nursery chamber through the ceiling.

The first Black Hole surgeon wore human skin and insectile patience. It turned with a bone saw in one hand and a filament injector in the other. Lars did not banter. He broke its arm, pinned it to a rack, and let Fabron foam it alive for interrogation.

The second released a swarm of black particulate toward the donor bays.

Havets moved.

Again, no visible miracle. The swarm lost coordination. Particles fell like soot into containment fields that had not existed a second earlier and would not appear in the hospital ship's logs. Old ship, old methods, local intervention.

The possible Aerit lay in Bay Twelve, a broad-faced man with farmer's hands and a century-old labor tattoo. His file called him Isaac Tam, age forty-nine. His bone density said two hundred. His neural lock patterns sang quiet refusal even under sedation.

Grim created a consent boundary record and projected a simple message into the bay's medical audio.

You are in danger. We can move your body without unlocking memory. We will not open locked identity without consent. Signal if you understand.

For seven seconds nothing happened.

Then Isaac Tam's finger tapped once against the cryo pad.

Not yes to awakening.

Yes to movement.

Good enough.

They took him, and twenty-five others, and enough records to make Black Hole's buyers nervous for decades. They did not save everyone. A vat cluster self-sterilized when Grim touched its archive. Four cloned tissue lines died before release. One Black Hole child-thing in a tank opened eyes too old for its body and asked, in no language Lars knew, whether it had been made wrong.

Lars wanted to destroy the chamber.

Instead he asked Mira to analyze the tank.

Her answer came shaking. "It is not a person yet. It is a failed interface grown around stolen nanobot fragments. Killing it may be mercy, but do not call it cleanup."

So they did not. They sedated it, froze it, and marked it as a grave ethical problem for later. Coherence did not make choices clean.

As they withdrew, the nursery's central archive unlocked under duress. Black Hole's model appeared on Grim's feed: Aerit blood as key, Creator portals as gates, KBC Void as throne, Nomad field as godhead to be consumed.

"They think scale means ownership," Grim said softly.

Lars looked at the racks. "Predators usually do."

Fabron set charges only on systems that could not be repurposed. Medical life support stayed. Navigation stayed. Records copied to distributed vaults. The hospital ship would be found by three jurisdictions and one very angry charitable oversight board.

The public story would be trafficking.

True, but incomplete.

When Fastulv pulled away, Isaac Tam remained asleep in a protected compartment. His consent record displayed one line.

Move me. Do not open me.

Lars read it twice.

Then he sent it to every guardian channel they trusted.

A warning and a vow.
