# Chapter 45: The Cancer Model

Grim modeled galactic collapse as cancer because he had recently become fond of biological metaphors and wanted everyone to suffer with him.

"Healthy systems differentiate," he said. "Cells specialize, communicate locally, obey growth limits, self-destruct when damaged, and maintain boundaries. Cancer centralizes growth around itself, ignores consent signals, recruits blood supply, corrupts immune response, and consumes the body that sustains it."

He displayed Black Hole, Syren, Aphex central, NUKEA emergency licensing, and several historical empires as overlapping tumors.

Arsenault objected to Aphex's color.

"You made us purple."

"Purple tested as morally anxious," Grim said.

"I am not accepting chromatic slander."

"Your objection is recorded in purple."

The model was funny until it was not.

Black Hole was the obvious malignancy: harvesting, forced coupling, biological processors, acquisition without understanding. Syren was ideological metastasis: synchronized surrender, meaning replacing maintenance, martyrdom as recruitment. Aphex and NUKEA were not enemies by nature, but their emergency instincts created central blood vessels any tumor could hijack.

The KBC quiet basin was not a mystical immune system. It was a low-noise tissue where complex coordination could persist. Bright, forced, centralized action could scar it. Enough scarring might expose Arkology Zero, damage hidden sleepers, and make future Nomad-layer contact impossible or hostile.

"So," Fabron said, "our treatment is distributed immune response."

"With minimal chemotherapy," Mira added. "No killing the patient to prove decisiveness."

They built the operation in layers.

Vigor would redirect pilgrims into repair vigils, food stations, and medical checks far from First Door. His network would not argue theology. It would ask people to carry water, fix seals, feed strangers, and delay departure until they were less ecstatic and more tired.

Fabron's Clear Hand cells would slow military centralization by insisting on local command verification and legal liability for ancient-system engagement.

Arsenault would fracture Aphex central from inside. He looked deeply offended by how useful betrayal could be when performed in the name of institutional survival.

Rauk and NUKEA dissidents would isolate emergency keys under reactor safety pretexts.

Mira would publish escalating technical advisories making insurers and civilian captains afraid of First Door's instability.

Grim and Ödesvaka would shield known sleepers and suspected Aerits with consent-aware alerts, relocation offers, and medical privacy traps.

Havets and Fastulv would go to First Door.

"As bait?" Fabron asked.

"As irritant," Lars said.

"That is worse."

"More accurate."

Valdrun reviewed the plan in silence. He had argued for stronger action two chapters of their lives ago, though no one said it that way. Now he traced the distributed layers with one mechanical finger.

"It may fail because no part is strong enough."

"Yes," Lars said.

"It may succeed because no part can be captured alone."

"Also yes."

Valdrun looked up. "Coherence uncertain."

"Coherence honest."

The first failure came quickly.

Black Hole attacked a repair vigil near Juno Gate, not for samples but for footage. They wanted to show Återviens-adjacent civilians dying while carrying tools instead of weapons. Vigor's people were not soldiers. Three died before Fabron's local cell and Aphex volunteers stopped the attackers.

The footage did spread.

But not as Black Hole intended.

A woman with blood on her face kept pressure on a stranger's wound while shouting at the camera, "Do not chase them. Fix the airlock. Children are in Section C. Fix the airlock."

That became the clip.

Not martyrdom. Maintenance under fire.

Repair before worship.

The phrase moved through the networks faster than Syren's holy silence.

Lars watched it aboard Havets and felt the terrible humility of not being the center of the thing he had helped start.

Good.

Then Grim's model flashed black.

Black Hole's main fleet had split into hundreds of small biological processor pods, each too minor to justify fleet engagement, together forming a distributed tumor of their own.

They had learned.

"Cancer can adapt," Grim said.

Lars stood.

"Then so can immune systems."
