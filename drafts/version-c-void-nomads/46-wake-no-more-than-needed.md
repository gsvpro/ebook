# Chapter 46: Wake No More Than Needed

The limited activation protocol broke its own heart before the enemy did.

There were seventeen suspected sleepers near Black Hole's projected pod routes. Six consented to evidence contact. Three refused all involvement. Four could not be reached without exposing them. Two were children by current life context despite impossible biology. One was in coma. One, an old station pilot named Ren Koss, answered the first evidence packet with the Aerit greeting before anyone prompted him.

Coherence?

Lars stared at the response until the room blurred.

Ren chose partial unlock.

Not full. He wanted enough memory to fly a route he did not understand but had apparently been born, remade, or trained to know centuries before. His current family sat with him during the boundary recording: husband, daughter, three grandchildren, all terrified and trying to be brave.

"Will he still be himself?" his daughter asked.

Mira answered because Lars could not. "If we do this right, yes. But himself may become larger and less simple. That is still a cost."

Ren laughed softly. "I was never simple. Ask your father."

The partial unlock did not look like enlightenment. It looked like an old man vomiting into a bucket, swearing in three languages, then weeping because he remembered a harbor on Heliostat where his hands had been young and Lyra had told him his talent for bad landings might someday save lives.

No migraine. The lock did not punish. It opened a small door and charged rent in grief.

Ren's route memory let them identify a service corridor through the pod swarm. One pilot, one route, one local use. Not a general awakening.

The coma patient was harder.

A suspected Aerit named Sella Nyr lay in a medical facility near First Door, injured in an unrelated industrial accident weeks before the crisis. Black Hole pods would pass within sampling range. She could not consent. Moving her risked death. Leaving her risked harvest.

The protocol required three independent approvals for emergency movement without awakening: local physician, guardian review, and trusted non-Aerit witness.

The physician approved.

Valdrun approved with visible pain.

Fabron served as witness and did not sleep afterward.

They moved Sella under medical necessity and recorded the exception as debt. Not victory. Debt.

Two children remained untouched except for shielded records and family relocation offers phrased as mundane safety advisories. Lars wanted to do more. Mira stopped him.

"No," she said. "You want to soothe yourself. Their guardians can choose with evidence. We do not make their bodies into strategy."

He hated her for eight seconds.

Then he thanked her.

The three who refused became the real test. One sent back a single sentence: I believe you and choose my life.

Lars printed it and pinned it inside Havets' planning chamber.

Black Hole pods continued moving.

Syren's synchronized silence ceremony gathered millions across relays despite Vigor's delays. Most were not near First Door physically. That was worse in one way: distributed minds trying to synchronize through public signal could feed the very field instability they misunderstood.

Grim proposed cutting the relays.

Fabron objected. "Censorship confirms their story."

Vigor proposed drowning the ceremony in noise.

Mira objected. "Noise can also destabilize the basin."

Lars looked at Ren Koss, pale but steady after partial unlock, and asked, "What did Lyra teach you about bad landings?"

Ren smiled. "Never fight the spin as one thing. Give each axis a smaller problem."

There it was again.

They would not silence Syren. They would desynchronize participation by offering local acts at the same time: repair vigils, meals, safety checks, mourning names, private silence unlinked from central broadcast. Let people keep meaning without becoming one waveform.

Fabron's order spread the instruction. Vigor's kitchens made it attractive. Mira's hazard paper made central synchronization sound medically irresponsible, which helped. Arsenault paid influencers to mock mass synchronized breathing as bad for insurance, then denied doing so with artistic sincerity.

On the eve of First Door, Lars visited Ren's family.

The old pilot's granddaughter asked, "Are you taking him away?"

"No," Lars said. "He is helping fly one route. Then he comes back if physics and his stubbornness cooperate."

Ren grinned. "My stubbornness has seniority."

The girl looked at Lars. "Are you a god?"

"No."

"Good. Gods in stories are bad at promises."

Lars bowed slightly.

"Then I will avoid promotion."
