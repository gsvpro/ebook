# Chapter 23: The Frenchman’s Ledger

Gabriel Arsenault received Lars in a room designed to imply that civilization had been invented by French furniture.

The desk was real wood, grown under gravity on a protected estate moon and insured for more than Fastulv's declared hull value. Lars set a greasy paper bag on it. Grim set a second bag beside the first. Arsenault stared at both as if they were tactical devices.

"Is that food?" he asked.

"Breakfast," Lars said.

"It is fourteen hundred hours."

"I have suffered worse calendar crimes."

Arsenault did not smile. That was how Lars knew the man was frightened.

The Aphex director had sent three secure messages since L-Prime 41. The first demanded a full report. The second offered private support. The third contained an old NUKEA procurement trail connected to the dead infiltrator's coordinate burst.

Layered agenda. Profit, fear, leverage, survival. Lars almost respected the efficiency.

Arsenault opened a wall vault and removed a physical ledger. Actual paper. Actual ink. The kind of affectation rich people used when they wanted illegality to feel historical.

"Before you insult this," Arsenault said, "understand that it has survived eight network collapses, three hostile audits, and one divorce."

"The divorce sounds worst."

"It was."

The ledger contained Aphex minority investments, shell suppliers, quiet rescue grants, military redundancies, and reactor-hardened agricultural habitats sold at a loss under charitable fronts. It also contained names. Not Aerits. People with patterns: long-lived engineers, impossible survivors, maintenance saints, reactor nuns, smugglers who moved medicine without invoices, soldiers who disobeyed remote orders during system failures and were later promoted by someone who should not have known.

"You were tracking hidden people," Lars said.

"I was tracking anomalies that correlated with institutional survival," Arsenault replied. "Do not flatter yourself by assuming I knew what you were."

Grim leaned closer. "This ledger is morally untidy."

"So is history."

Arsenault turned a page. There was a line from Pluto-era artifact rumors to early GRIN supply chains, then a scatter across Heliostat archives, Kalari exchange stations, Nexus Outpost maintenance guilds, and eventually Aphex procurement.

Lars felt old memory brush the edge of attention. Not pain. No lock migraine. Only resonance, like hearing a song through a wall and knowing he had once hated the singer and loved them anyway.

"Återviens," he said.

Arsenault tapped the ledger. "The word appears in seventeen spellings. Most sources treat it as a discipline, superstition, guild law, or rural etiquette. I treated it as a useful infection. Systems influenced by it fail less stupidly."

"You tried to buy it."

"Of course. I buy useful things. It resisted purchase, which increased my admiration and irritation."

That, Lars thought, was Arsenault in one confession.

The ledger's final section held the danger. Aphex had not been the only watcher. NUKEA compliance shells, Syren charities, Black Hole brokers, and unknown low-noise accounts had all circled the same anomalies. Someone had mapped the quiet habits without understanding the people inside them.

"Why show me this?" Lars asked.

"Because if I hide it, you will find it and damage expensive systems while doing so. Because Aphex central contains factions that would put Dr. Sovan in a sealed lab and call it protection. Because if the galaxy is actually rotting, I prefer to own shares in the repair industry rather than the coffin industry."

"Touching."

"Practical."

Arsenault slid one last packet across the desk. It showed a NUKEA recall scheduled for small modular reactors across twelve marginal colonies. One colony, Havskant, sat near a minor portal anomaly and a cluster of ledger names.

"The recall is real," Arsenault said. "The timing is not. Someone moved it forward after L-Prime."

"Bait?"

"Or net."

Grim consumed a pastry with solemn concentration. "The recall standard contains a firmware dependency that can centralize emergency control across all twelve colonies."

Arsenault's mouth flattened. "NUKEA promised that was impossible."

"They lied or were optimistic. I dislike both."

Lars looked at the old paper ledger, then at the living map of frightened institutions pretending to be strategy.

"You want me to stop NUKEA without damaging your investment."

"I want you to prevent a monopoly failure mode that will kill people and bankrupt me. The order is negotiable."

"There it is."

Arsenault leaned forward. For once, the theater dropped. "Monsieur Odin, I am greedy, not suicidal. If your hidden civilization teaches one lesson, it is that central control feels safest right before it becomes a weapon. Help me make Aphex useful before Aphex becomes that weapon."

Lars hated how close that came to honesty.

He picked up the NUKEA packet.

"Fine. But if I catch you harvesting anomalies, I will turn this desk into a canoe."

Arsenault looked at the wood, pained. "That would be barbaric."

"Motivate yourself."

As Lars left, the ledger's oldest page remained open behind him. In faded ink beside a Pluto rumor, someone had written a phrase in bad early translation.

Not gods. Not owners.

Old engineers passing through.
