# Chapter 36: Dead Subway

The dead subway had stations no one should enter and timetables written in gravitational scars.

Havets moved along the first route at a cautious fraction of its real capability. Fastulv followed in ordinary space where it could, vanishing from the story whenever topology stopped respecting normal travel. Fabron hated that. He wrote three official reports, deleted all of them, and finally titled the fourth: Unusual Salvage Transit Conditions.

"Cowardice in four words," Lars said.

"Admissible cowardice," Fabron replied.

The first station was a Creator portal folded around a cometary graveyard. Dead rings hung among frozen bodies, each ring cracked at the same angle. Not battle damage. Systemic fatigue. The Creators had used the route until the older layer pushed back or stopped cooperating.

Mira guided the survey. "Do not energize. Do not synchronize. Passive only."

Grim sent passive pings so delicate they were almost apologies. The station answered with old logs.

Creator crews had called the network a triumph. They moved ships through shortcuts, then cities, then fleets. They mapped quiet basins as low-loss transit corridors. They saw the older anchors as stabilizers, natural or abandoned. They built over them, through them, around them.

Then came the failures.

Ships arrived with crews remembering conversations they had never had. Cargo holds gained or lost grams of unstable nanomaterial. Some travelers reported temporary bodies seen in sealed compartments, dissolving before measurement. Portal operators called them ghosts. Engineers called them cross-layer contamination. Priests, because every civilization produced them eventually, called them summons.

"They had the same argument," Fabron said.

"Everyone does," Lars replied. "The universe keeps handing people weird plumbing and they keep founding churches near the leak."

The second station had been deliberately killed.

Not destroyed. Quieted. Its control systems were intact but locally severed, each subsystem holding a tiny shard of function without allowing any one unit to become a hub. The kill pattern looked familiar.

Återviens before Återviens.

Or something older that had taught the same lesson.

Valdrun's voice shook. "Lyra saw this."

"Maybe," Lars said. "Or Pluto showed her an echo."

They found evidence in the third station: a maintenance cache left by a Creator faction that had realized the mistake. Its record translated in fragments.

We mistook reply for road.
We increased brightness.
The field withdrew.
The quiet regions are not empty. They are agreements.
The older engineers do not command.
They cohere.

Grim replayed the final line until Mira told him to stop making everyone feel haunted.

"It is evidence," he said.

"It is poetry wearing evidence's coat," she said. "Useful, but slippery."

The station offered one active test: a micro-transit through a dead ring, unmanned, low mass. Lars agreed only after three veto paths and a local abort authority held by Mira, Fabron, and Ödesvaka separately.

They sent a probe the size of a breadbox.

It vanished.

It returned eleven seconds later with frost on one side, dust from no known local source, and a recording of Lars's own voice saying, "Do not make me the answer," captured three minutes before he said it.

Fabron turned slowly toward him.

"I dislike time anomalies."

"Everyone sensible does."

Mira analyzed the probe for six hours. Her conclusion was cautious enough to be brutal.

"Not time travel in the dramatic sense. Field correlation across probable local states. The communication layer is seeing nearby possibilities and feeding them through matter systems that were never designed for it. Consciousness projection could ride that if coupled to a prepared body. A bad model would call it prophecy or ghosts."

"And a good model?" Lars asked.

"Would say we are underqualified."

Grim nodded. "Finally, consensus."

The dead subway's route map ended at a sealed station named by Creators as First Door.

Under that, older absence marked it with a warning that translated only when they reduced observation, dimmed lights, and stopped active scanning.

Not first.

Lars looked into the dark beyond the ring and felt wonder arrive like vertigo.

Not mystical. Worse.

Measurable.
