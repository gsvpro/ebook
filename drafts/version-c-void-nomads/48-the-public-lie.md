# Chapter 48: The Public Lie

The lie had to be ugly enough to work.

Beautiful lies invited believers. Noble lies invited martyrs. Clean lies invited investigators. Lars needed a bureaucratic lie with bad lighting and insurance implications.

Mira wrote the technical core: First Door and similar ancient transit objects presented severe instability under synchronized observation, high-energy activation, biological computation, massed fleet telemetry, and unlicensed devotional acoustics. She used the phrase unlicensed devotional acoustics because Grim dared her and because regulators feared anything they could not categorize.

Fabron wrote the security advisory: no central fleet engagement, no pilgrim convergence, no remote emergency override, local evacuation cells only.

Arsenault wrote the corporate liability memo. It was a masterpiece of cowardice weaponized for survival. Any company, government, cult, militia, charity, research institute, or private individual approaching First Door without certified distributed safety protocols could face unlimited liability across seventeen jurisdictions and one Aphex weapons warranty clause that no one understood but everyone feared.

Vigor wrote the human version.

Do not go to the old door hungry, afraid, or in crowds. Fix something near you. Feed someone. Wait three days. If the universe needs you, it can respect a proper meal.

That version spread fastest.

The public lie reduced pilgrim traffic by half in six hours. Not by convincing everyone. By giving doubt something practical to do. People who might have joined Syren's synchronized silence instead attended local repair vigils, medical checks, and unsynchronized mourning circles. Meaning remained. Waveform broke.

Lars hated the lie anyway.

He sat alone in Fastulv's cockpit while the advisory moved through the networks. Grim entered with two sandwiches and an expression of mechanical concern.

"You are brooding with inefficient posture."

"I am preserving brand consistency."

"You dislike hiding evidence."

"Yes."

"You also dislike mass death, cult capture, corporate monopoly, and Black Hole eating the communications layer."

"Your emotional support style needs sanding."

Grim set a sandwich beside him. "Eat. Vigor threatened me."

The lie produced consequences immediately. Syren called Lars a censor. Black Hole called the advisory proof that hidden blood elites feared human ascension. Aphex central called Arsenault to ask why liability had become more powerful than fleet doctrine. NUKEA delayed its power ships pending risk review, which meant Rauk's dissidents had bought them time.

Mira called next.

"I signed the paper," she said. "My name is on a public lie."

"Your name is on a public hazard advisory."

"Do not varnish it."

"Fine. Yes."

She exhaled. "I understand why. I still want it recorded privately that the omitted truth matters."

"Recorded."

"And that after the crisis, we need a way to widen truth without creating a throne."

"Recorded with dread."

She almost smiled. "Good."

The hardest part came when Pail, the former Syren sympathizer, asked for a private channel. He had become a minor voice for repair vigils, which meant both Syren and anti-Syren feeds hated him in useful balance.

"Is the advisory true?" he asked.

Lars looked at the young man's tired face and gave him the only answer that did not make him cargo.

"It is true enough to keep people alive. It is not all the truth. I am not giving you the rest today because you are still deciding whether enormity means surrender."

Pail flinched, then nodded.

"Will you tell us someday?"

"Not us. Not from a balcony. But yes, more people will need more truth. Slowly. With tools in hand."

Pail looked relieved and disappointed. That was probably healthy.

By the time Fastulv and Havets departed for First Door, the public lie had done its work. The crisis space was less crowded, less synchronized, less bright.

Not safe.

The Black Hole pod swarm still converged. Syren's core faithful still prepared central silence. Aphex still had ships moving under fragmented orders. First Door still waited with Creator hardware wrapped around older absence.

But across the galaxy, millions of people who wanted revelation were fixing airlocks, stirring soup, checking reactor governors, and sitting in private silence that belonged to no central broadcast.

Lars looked at the distributed map.

"Maybe civilization has a chance."

Grim bit into his sandwich paper and all.

"This is inedible."

"Remove the paper."

"Civilization remains uneven."
