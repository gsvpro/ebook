# Chapter 30: The Consent Problem

Isaac Tam woke on Arkology Zero and refused to become a revelation.

He woke slowly, medically, with Valdrun in the room, Grim observing from behind a privacy boundary, Lars standing where he could be seen, and no one saying Coherence until Isaac asked why everyone looked like undertakers trying to be polite.

"You were taken by Black Hole B-7," Lars said. "We moved you after you signaled yes to movement. We did not unlock memory."

Isaac stared at the ceiling. His hands flexed. Farmer's hands, worker's hands, hands that had repaired pressure pumps, held children, buried friends, and apparently carried nanotechnology older than his known life.

"Am I safe?"

"Safer. Not safe."

"Good. I hate lies before breakfast."

Lars liked him immediately, which was inconvenient.

They showed evidence. Not identity. Not origin. The hospital ship. The consent record. His impossible medical anomalies. A map of danger around his old colony. Isaac listened with a face like weathered stone.

When they finished, he said, "No."

Valdrun inclined his head. "No to what?"

"No to opening whatever you think is in me. No to leaving my life permanently. No to becoming part of your secret ancient committee. No offense."

"Some offense taken," Grim said. "Committees are an insult."

Isaac looked at him. "Is the robot always like that?"

"Worse when hungry," Lars said.

"Robots get hungry?"

"This one has aspirations."

Isaac laughed, then stopped because laughing hurt where Black Hole had placed needles.

The refusal changed the room.

It was one thing to say consent mattered when the person chose useful participation. It was another when a possible Aerit, a hunted survivor, looked at centuries of hidden danger and chose a farm, a partner, a local council, and grandchildren who thought he was merely stubborn.

Valdrun's old eyes held grief.

"You may be needed," he said.

Isaac nodded. "So are farmers."

That ended the argument more cleanly than Lars could have managed.

They negotiated for two days.

Isaac accepted medical repair for injuries caused by Black Hole, but no lock probing. He accepted a contact token, shielded records, and relocation options for his family if danger rose. He refused Arkology residence. He refused to meet any guardian channel. He refused to let Lars look disappointed for more than five seconds.

"You are not my king," Isaac said.

"I am aggressively not anyone's king."

"Then stop looking like a bad one."

Valdrun made a sound suspiciously close to pleasure.

Mira attended the final boundary meeting remotely. She watched Isaac with an expression Lars could not read until she spoke.

"Thank you," she said.

Isaac frowned. "For what?"

"For making no a real option."

He considered that, then nodded once. "You are welcome, stranger."

After he returned under a layered protective identity, the guardians argued.

Guardian Channel Two, still not fully verified, urged more aggressive contact. Black Hole's nursery proved the enemy was scaling acquisition. Syren's public invitation had turned the KBC Void into religious bait. Aphex factions were already asking why Director Odin had diverted assets. NUKEA wanted its recall authority restored. Every system leaned toward central action because fear loved a dashboard.

Valdrun wanted a selective awakening protocol.

Ödesvaka wanted consent gates so strict they would slow response to a crawl.

Grim wanted both and pretended this was logically elegant.

Lars wanted to punch a wall, which was not policy.

Vigor, on a grainy kitchen channel, settled it by peeling an apple with a knife large enough for war.

"Wake no one to soothe your fear," he said. "Shield many. Ask few. Move only those who choose or cannot survive otherwise, and count every exception as a debt."

Fabron added, "And document who has authority to declare the exception."

"No one alone," Mira said.

There. The hinge.

They built the first limited activation protocol: evidence-first contact, consent boundary records, local protection before relocation, no full unlock unless requested and verified by at least three independent guardians or trusted local equivalents, with refusal treated as operational truth rather than obstacle.

It was slow.

It was fragile.

It would save fewer people this month than a tyrant could seize in a day.

Lars signed anyway.

That night, he dreamed of Lyra on Pluto, young and starving, telling him that survival without consent became just another predator with better manners.

When he woke, Grim was beside the bed holding a diagnostic slate.

"You made distress noises."

"That is called sleeping."

"It sounded inefficient."

Lars sat up. "Any new disasters?"

"Several. The Syren invitation now includes a countdown. Also, the Creator map fragment from the dead platform has opened a route labeled, in approximate translation, dead subway."

Lars rubbed his face.

"Of course it has."
