# Chapter 40: Fabron’s Lineage

Fabron took Lars to church and warned him not to be irritating.

The chapel was not grand. It occupied the lower deck of an old Aphex veterans' habitat outside Marseille Orbital, between a prosthetics clinic and a reactor parts exchange. Its altar was a workbench. Its candles were inspection lamps. Its saints, if saints they were, appeared as silhouettes repairing doors, sharing water, carrying batteries, refusing crowns.

The congregation called itself Clear Hand.

They did not know Aerits. Not fully. They knew practices, stories, jokes, rituals. They knew that authority must be local first, that rescue without consent became conquest, that a broken pump deserved attention before a beautiful speech, that no person should be allowed to become the only hinge.

They sang badly.

Lars liked that.

Fabron's grandmother met him after the service. Madame Ysabeau Fabron was ninety-six, narrow as a blade, and looked at Lars with the unimpressed focus of someone who had survived officers, priests, executives, and grandchildren.

"So," she said, "you are the old trouble."

"I prefer experienced nuisance."

"You would."

She led him to an archive room where paper, metal tokens, memory glass, and children's drawings shared shelves without hierarchy. The oldest records did not name Återviens. They spoke of the Clear Ones, the maintenance vow, the refusal of thrones. Some dated to post-Rakh'Na reconstruction. Some were copied from Heliostat exchange manuals. Some carried Kalari margin marks. One fragment, older than the order admitted publicly, described a woman with star-wolf hair teaching colonists to make their safety rituals impossible to purchase.

Lyra, probably.

Or someone Lyra had nudged.

Madame Fabron watched Lars read. "We thought the Clear Ones were a moral fiction. A way to teach children that power must wash dishes."

"Good fiction," Lars said.

"Was it planted?"

He answered carefully. "Some seeds, maybe. Much grew because people needed it. That makes it yours."

Her face softened by one degree. "My grandson fears divided loyalty."

"He should. It means he has more than one thing worth betraying badly."

Fabron, behind them, muttered in French.

The archive proved what the plan had only suggested. Återviens had spread far beyond Aerits: maintenance cultures, farm vows, reactor ethics, disaster kitchens, station guilds, military habits, mutual-aid networks, religious orders that preserved resilience by misunderstanding it usefully. No single church. No secret army. A galaxy full of people who had learned, in fragments, not to worship central power.

That was Lyra's recovery apparatus at human scale.

Not controlled. Not pure. Not safe.

Alive.

During supper, Madame Fabron placed Lars at a long table and made him peel potatoes. Grim received one potato for examination and became attached to it. Vigor, on remote, approved of everything. Mira watched quietly from L-Prime until Madame Fabron told the screen she looked underfed.

"I am not physically present," Mira said.

"Excuses travel faster than bodies," Madame replied.

Fabron looked delighted for three seconds before remembering dignity.

The peace broke when an Aphex central warrant arrived demanding all Clear Hand anomaly archives be preserved for strategic review. Arsenault had not sent it. A rival faction had.

Fabron read the warrant once, then set it on the workbench altar.

"No," he said.

His grandmother smiled.

Lars did not intervene. That mattered. If he rescued the archive, it became his. If Fabron refused with local authority, the order remained itself.

Fabron called three legal clerks, two veterans' unions, a food safety inspector, and Arsenault in that order. Within an hour, the archive was temporarily classified as an active religious, medical, historical, and culinary preservation site. No single authority could seize it without angering five others and one elderly woman with knives.

"You weaponized bureaucracy," Lars said.

"You are a terrible influence."

"I am moved."

Before Lars left, Madame Fabron handed him a steel token: three circles, deliberate crack.

"We do not worship the Clear Ones," she said. "We repair what they could not. Remember that when people start kneeling."

Lars closed his hand around the token.

"I will be rude," he said.

"Good. But peel better next time."
