# Chapter 21: Containment Drill

The three incoming ships arrived like bad decisions wearing stealth plating.

Mira saw them first as fatigue signatures, not silhouettes. Their hulls flexed against the brown dwarf's particle wake in three different rhythms, which meant they had been assembled from stolen systems and grown components forced to agree by someone with more appetite than patience.

"Built, grown, and stolen," she said from the conference room display. "In that order, and badly. Their compensation curves are fighting each other. Whoever made them does not maintain things. They disciplines them until they stop complaining."

"Black Hole culture in one sentence," Lars said.

Fabron's voice cut through the tactical net. "Director Odin, you are not authorized to categorize enemy civilization while we are under attack."

"I am on the board. I authorize myself."

"Then I object on behalf of civilization."

Fastulv detached from Dock Two with all running lights bright, all transponders loud, and every visible weapon showing the posture of a freighter too old and too proud to retreat. Lars sat in the pilot cradle with Grim beside him, while Havets Sista Sång remained absent from every Aphex sensor. The ancient ship did not hide by being invisible. It hid by making the question of where it was slightly impolite to ask.

The first hostile fired a sampling drone at the facility instead of a missile. Mira's analysis flashed red.

"That is not explosive," she said. "It wants atmosphere, blood, and live diagnostics."

"Shoot it," Fabron ordered.

Aphex point defense woke, aimed, then hesitated. Remote authorization conflict. The kind of conflict created by people who believed centralized safety was safer because they had never met betrayal with admin privileges.

Lars swore. "Grim."

"Already being criminal."

Fastulv rolled hard, vented two empty cargo pods, and used their tumbling mass to mask a micro-burn. The sampling drone corrected toward the warmer pod and died when Fastulv's belly turret cut it into vapor.

"Visible role fulfilled," Grim said. "We look brave and moderately lucky."

"Moderately?"

"Your ego is not mission-critical."

The second and third ships split. One moved toward the med bay section where the captured infiltrator lay in containment. The other slid under the facility's materials ring, looking for a seam in the shielding.

Mira stood in the conference room despite Fabron's medical officer ordering her not to. "They are not attacking the ring. They are listening through it. The stress sensors are bidirectional if you abuse them."

"Can you stop them?" Fabron asked.

"I can make the ring sing false."

Lars felt Havets stir in the hidden layer, an old pressure behind thought. Not command. Readiness. He did not tell it what to do as if it were a gun. He shaped an intention and left room for the ship's older judgment.

Havets answered by moving nowhere and everywhere useful.

Across L-Prime 41, maintenance panels snapped open for no logged reason. Local relays dropped remote commands and reverted to manual. Emergency lights pulsed not in panic code but in a maintenance rhythm older than Aphex. Workers who had never heard the word Återviens recognized the instruction anyway: isolate, verify, repair.

Fabron heard his soldiers begin repeating it. "Local authority only. Confirm by voice. No remote orders."

He looked once toward Mira. She was pale, sweating, furious, and alive with attention.

"Doctor," he said, "you are adjacent to my command again."

"I am beginning to dislike your command less."

"I will accept that as morale."

The hostile ship under the ring latched a needle probe into an external sensor spine. Mira sent her false song.

The ring reported stresses that did not exist: impossible heat gradients, phantom torsion, hull-fatigue ghosts. The hostile probe believed it was seeing a weakness and opened its sampling throat wider.

That was when Havets touched it.

Nothing visible happened. No beam, no heroic flash. The probe simply forgot which direction was useful. Its internal control loops scattered, each trying to become local authority without coherence. The hostile ship twitched, overcorrected against the brown dwarf wake, and exposed its flank to Fastulv.

"Now?" Grim asked.

"Now."

Fastulv fired. The shot did not destroy the ship. It cut the grown component loose from the stolen frame. The frame spun away trailing atmosphere. The grown thing contracted like a wounded organ and went dark.

The third ship, the one hunting the med bay, did the smartest thing any enemy had done all day.

It ran.

Fabron ordered pursuit. Lars countermanded it on the board channel, then muted the resulting legal explosion.

"Let it go," he said.

"It will report," Fabron snapped.

"Yes. With the wrong story."

Mira looked up from her station. "What story?"

"That Aphex has hidden local-defense protocols, that Fastulv is more dangerous than it looks, and that you are useful but not awakened."

"I am useful?"

"Unfortunately."

She stared at him until he added, "By consent and current evidence only."

Her shoulders lowered a millimeter.

Then the med bay alarm screamed.

The captured infiltrator had died without anyone touching it. Its body collapsed into black particulate, and in the residue Grim found a tight burst of data sent through the facility's own medical archive.

Not to Black Hole.

To somewhere quieter.

Mira read the first line of the decoded fragment and went still.

It was not a name. It was a coordinate family.

Aligned with the KBC Void.
