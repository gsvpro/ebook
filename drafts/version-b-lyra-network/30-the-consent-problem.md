# Chapter 30: The Consent Problem

The oxygen farmer on Mirrorglass Station wanted nothing to do with them.

Her name was Sella Korr. She maintained algae towers in a habitat whose windows faced a gas giant's storm bands. She looked fifty, had records going back one hundred and forty years, and once survived chlorine exposure that killed three emergency drones. Her tower crews loved her because she fixed pumps before they failed and remembered everyone's children's allergies.

She listened to Lars for twelve minutes.

Then she said, "No."

Not angrily. Not fearfully. Like closing a valve.

Lars nodded. "All right."

Fabron blinked. "That is all?"

"That is all," Sella said before Lars could.

They stood between algae columns humming with green light. Grim had already withdrawn the evidence display to a sealed buffer. Vigor had brought bread and left it untouched on a workbench, which was how Lars knew the conversation had become serious.

Sella's hands were scarred from nutrient-line burns. "I have a life. I have crews. I have three marriages behind me and two grandchildren who think I am ordinary in ways that comfort them. If you tell me I am ancient, hunted, or secretly full of miracle machines, that helps whom?"

"Maybe you, if danger comes," Lars said.

"Danger already comes. Pumps fail. Companies lie. Children get fever in low-pressure weeks. I know what to do with those dangers. I do not know what to do with yours."

He wanted to argue that the danger was hers whether she accepted it or not. The sentence was true. It was also a crowbar.

Mira, listening through delay, sent one line to his wrist: Do not make fear your consent form.

Lars breathed out.

"We can leave protection without identity disclosure. Watch queries. Harden medical privacy. Place evacuation routes under agricultural grants. You never have to hear from me again unless you ask."

Sella studied him. "Can you do that without making me special?"

Vigor answered. "Yes. We do it for many people who are not ancient mysteries."

"Good. Then do that."

Fabron looked uncomfortable. "If Black Hole comes directly?"

"Then I will decide with current facts, not with a stranger's mythology poured into my skull beforehand."

No one spoke.

Sella softened a fraction. "I am not ungrateful. I am refusing the part that would make me belong to your emergency."

Lars felt the old self in him, the war-trained idiot, the man who wanted to carry everyone because carrying looked noble from the outside. No lock-migraine punished him for remembering. He had to punish himself with discipline instead.

"Coherence?" he asked, because she had permitted the word in the evidence brief.

Sella's eyes filled with tears she clearly hated.

"I don't know," she said.

"That is an answer."

She nodded once. "Then coherence enough."

They left Mirrorglass with no awakened Aerit, no dramatic reunion, no new guardian for the war.

They left behind privacy shields, spare pumps, three evacuation drills disguised as storm-season practice, and Vigor's bread, which Sella accepted at the airlock after pretending not to want it.

On Fastulv, Fabron said, "That felt like losing."

"Yes," Lars said.

"Was it?"

He watched Mirrorglass rotate over the gas giant, fragile and stubborn.

"No. But I hate that too."
