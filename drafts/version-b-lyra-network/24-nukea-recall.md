# Chapter 24: NUKEA Recall

NUKEA recalled thirty-seven thousand modular reactor regulators for a flaw that did not exist.

The public notice blamed ceramic creep in field governors under rare brown-dwarf radiation profiles. The language was precise, dull, and terrifying enough that procurement managers signed replacement waivers before finishing breakfast.

The flaw was not in the governors.

It was in the colonies around them.

Lars learned this inside a cargo hangar on Vigor, where crates of regulator kits sat beside seed vaults, pressure curtain rolls, printed medical membranes, and enough illegal spare parts to make three licensing attorneys faint.

Vigor presided over the inventory with a stew spoon in one hand and a customs database in the other.

"A recall is a beautiful thing," she said. "Everyone expects confusion."

"You are smuggling under a safety bulletin," Fabron said.

"I am correcting supply-chain theology."

"That is not a legal category."

"It should be. Eat."

Fabron accepted a bowl because refusing Vigor's food required either courage or poor survival instincts. Grim accepted three bowls and began a sensory analysis that made the kitchen staff proud and unsettled.

The target colony was Kestrel Mercy, a ring habitat built around an old comet mine. Its reactor had six months of margin, three weeks of honest maintenance capacity, and one priest-engineer who sent NUKEA warranty complaints as if composing psalms.

The priest-engineer was not a priest in any central church. His order taught that a reactor was a social contract written in plasma. Every family learned shutdown drills. Every meal rotation included one maintenance question. Children could recite coolant-loop ethics before they could lie convincingly.

NUKEA's official recall ships carried replacement regulators. Vigor's unofficial containers carried manual pump kits, food catalysts, printstock, shield mesh, and a medical privacy server Grim had built after Mira threatened to solder his fingers together if he ever again called stolen metadata "emotionally adequate."

Lars flew Fastulv as the visible contractor. Havets rode beyond local causality, nudging customs scans away from the extra mass with the gentleness of a giant moving dust.

At Kestrel Mercy, the docking crew greeted them with grease on their faces and three interlocking circles burned into the handle of a wrench.

"Coherence?" the oldest technician asked, not to Lars but to the crate manifest.

Vigor answered, "Coherence."

Lars looked at her.

She shrugged. "Farmers listen. Maintenance people talk. You thought your old word stayed in the walls?"

"I did not think. That was the problem."

The reactor repair took nineteen hours. There was no battle, unless fatigue counted, and Lars was old enough to know fatigue always counted. He crawled through a coolant access with Fabron, argued with a pump that had more personality than many executives, and watched a twelve-year-old girl correct an Aphex-trained engineer's lockout sequence.

"Who taught you that?" Fabron asked.

"Grandmother," the girl said. "She says central panels are for people who trust weather."

Fabron bowed very slightly, which made the girl grin.

When the reactor came back to full redundancy, the habitat did not cheer. It logged the repair, fed the crew, updated three paper manuals, and copied the method to six stations that officially did not know each other.

That night, Lars stood by the comet ice window with Vigor.

"Lyra?" he asked.

"Maybe. Maybe not. Culture does not need one mother forever. It grows teeth."

Outside, recall ships departed in different directions, carrying ordinary crates with extraordinary routing codes.
