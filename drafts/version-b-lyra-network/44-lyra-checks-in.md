# Chapter 44: Lyra Checks In

Lyra appeared in a locked freezer full of vaccine cultures.

The freezer belonged to a mixed-species clinic on Station Pelican, six decks below the corridor where Jae-Min Orra had trapped a Black Hole courier with a spill alarm. Its access logs showed no entry. Its thermal record showed a seven-second heat debt. Its mass balance gained fifty-three grams and lost them again. Three cameras recorded static shaped like irritation.

Jae-Min found the residue while retrieving booster stock and called Vigor because she trusted soap gifts more than police.

By the time Lars arrived, the manifestation was gone.

It had left a handprint in frost, a smear of unstable nanomaterial, and one ceramic data bead sitting between vaccine racks. The bead opened to Mira's consent key, not Lars's.

Mira stared at it through the remote feed. "Of course she gives it to me."

"You sound annoyed," Lars said.

"I respect the tactic and resent the manipulation. Humans are complex."

"You may be more than human."

"I did not consent to that sentence before coffee."

She opened the bead.

Lyra's image appeared badly. The temporary body had been older than the bad-body journal version, face sharper, left hand incomplete, skin flickering with projection artifacts. Hardly a goddess. Barely a stable person. Her eyes, though, were Lyra's: amused, exhausted, measuring the cost of every word.

"Coherence?" the recording asked.

No one answered at first.

Then Valdrun did, voice breaking through Havets. "Coherence."

Lyra smiled as if she had expected Valdrun and missed her anyway.

"This is a delayed local manifestation record, not live command. If you are watching, the recovery apparatus judged open contact less dangerous than continued silence. That judgment may be wrong. Good systems allow that sentence."

Lars swallowed.

"Black Hole B-7 is a predator pattern, not the root. Reduce appetite, expose supply, protect persons. Do not spend hidden Aerits like ammunition. The apparatus has watched thousands and contacted few. Many are not ready. Some will never want us. They are not failures."

Mira's face went very still.

Lyra continued. "Återviens has outgrown us. Good. Culture that requires its founders to remain pure has already become a trap. Human orders, station guilds, farms, alien kin-circles, reactor crews: treat them as peers in practice, not children carrying our fragments."

The image glitched. For one frame, Lyra looked directly at Lars.

"And Lars. If you are trying to become central command because guilt feels like purpose, stop. Join the network. Do not become its excuse to stop thinking."

Grim whispered, "Accurate."

"Shut up."

The recording ended with coordinates, not answers: shelter nodes, false trails, preserved-body vaults, and a triage index showing hidden Aerits categorized by risk, consent status, local support, and century-scale impact models.

Century-scale.

Lyra had been planning in time horizons long enough to make Lars's urgency look like weather.

Mira closed the bead.

"She checked in," Fabron said.

"No," Lars said, looking at the frost handprint as it sublimated. "She handed us work."
