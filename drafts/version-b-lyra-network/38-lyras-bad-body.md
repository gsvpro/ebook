# Chapter 38: Lyra's Bad Body

The holojournal began with Lyra Vargstjärna falling out of a maintenance cabinet.

She hit the floor face-first, lay still for three seconds, then lifted one hand and said, "Success criteria require revision."

Lars laughed so hard he had to sit down.

The recording was old but not Pluto-old. The timestamp placed it nearly two centuries after the flight from Earth, long after Återviens had grown from survival habit into doctrine and long before Lars had been born. Lyra looked older than in Pluto fragments, younger than myth, and furious at the temporary body she had just grown in a sealed lab somewhere beyond declared Aerit space.

The body was bad.

Not monstrous. Worse: inconvenient. Its left eye focused four seconds late. Its balance system believed walls were suggestions. Its artificial marrow produced nanobot carrier proteins in enthusiastic clumps. Every time Lyra tried to stand, the body overcorrected and punched a cabinet.

A younger Valdrun, still mostly biological, filmed without helping.

"For the record," Valdrun said, "I advised incremental remote manipulators."

"For the record," Lyra replied from the floor, "you are smug because gravity likes you."

The room watching the journal softened. Even Fabron smiled before remembering he had a reputation.

Then the recording became less funny.

Lyra ran diagnostics. The temporary body could maintain consciousness projection for twenty-two minutes before decoherence. It left residue: unstable nanomaterial, heat debt, corrupted local recordings, mass-balance errors. It could appear inside a shielded room if an anchor had been seeded earlier. It could not fight well, could not survive panic, and could not be mistaken for a ghost by anyone with a decent thermal camera.

"Good," recorded Lyra said. "If people call this mystical, revoke their laboratory privileges."

Mira pointed at the residue spectra. "This matches the L-Prime corridor."

"And Havets' handprint," Grim said.

The journal continued through failures: bodies that dissolved too fast, agents that developed local autonomy and anxiety, preserved biological substrates that could host projection but not identity without consent safeguards. Lyra's language changed over decades. Early notes spoke of retrieval. Later notes spoke of triage.

Watch. Shield. Contact. Ask. Partial unlock. Relocate. Leave locked.

Consent moved from moral note to system requirement.

Lyra looked into the recorder near the end, hair tied back, eyes exhausted.

"If you are watching this because the recovery apparatus has touched your life, remember this: a sleeping person is not missing equipment. A hidden Aerit who refuses us is not a failed rescue. A culture that carries Återviens without knowing us is not lesser than us. If Lars ever forgets that, make him eat something unpleasant and explain why."

Grim paused the recording.

Everyone looked at him.

"I accept this duty," he said solemnly.

Lars wiped at his face and pretended it was because of laughter.

The bad body on the screen tried to stand again, succeeded, and immediately walked into a door.

Lyra swore in three languages, two of them dead.

The apparatus was not divine.

It had bruises.
