# Chapter 25: Vigor's Table

Vigor believed diplomacy began when everyone had too much bread to posture efficiently.

Her table occupied the central greenhouse of her ship-habitat, under citrus trees grafted to tolerate low gravity and terrible songs. Aphex soldiers sat beside smugglers. NUKEA technicians sat beside station monks who denied being monks. Two Abantu traders with throat fans like folded silk argued pricing with a Mars-born quartermaster while Grim ranked soups by mineral honesty.

Lars stood at the edge and felt useless.

That was new enough to irritate him.

"Sit," Vigor ordered.

"I am observing."

"You are hovering. Hovering is what men do when they want command but fear being called out. Sit."

He sat.

At the table's far end, Mira attended through a secure projection, still at L-Prime 41, surrounded by sensor overlays and stubbornness. She had declined relocation. She had accepted a hidden courier line, a hardened lab, and the right to call Lars an idiot in writing.

"Your station guild contacts found two more candidates," she said.

The table quieted.

"Do not call them candidates," Vigor said. "Call them people whose lives we may ruin by noticing."

Mira's mouth tightened. "Correct. Two people whose lives we may ruin by noticing. One oxygen farmer on Mirrorglass Station, one arthropod mathematician in a mixed-species archive near Tau Ceti. Both show age drift, emergency survivals, and protective accidents around records."

The Abantu trader clicked her throat fan. "The archive people have their own consent law. External identity claims require kin-circle hearing."

Fabron frowned. "That could take months."

"Good," Vigor said. "Months are sometimes how ethics says yes slowly."

Lars thought of Aerit time: decades, centuries, not the frantic weeks he kept trying to impose because Black Hole had made urgency addictive.

The meeting did not produce orders. It produced offers.

A farm convoy could carry shielding under grain protein. A station guild could watch medical queries. Aphex dissenters could misfile Black Hole procurement traces. NUKEA crews could hide rescue beacons inside recall kits. Fabron's order could provide disciplined humans who understood refusal without needing the whole truth.

"This is chaos," Fabron said.

Vigor smiled. "No. Chaos forgets to feed people. This is a table."

Grim displayed a model above the soup. Nodes bloomed across the local arm: farms, reactor crews, maintenance guilds, food cooperatives, old families, little orders, alien mutual-aid circles, Aphex malcontents, smugglers with standards. The network did not have a center. Every attempt to draw one made the model weaker.

Lars watched his own name appear as a high-connectivity node.

He deleted it.

Grim restored it.

Lars glared.

"Evidence," Grim said. "Not flattery."

"Reduce dependency," Lars said.

"Working on it."

Vigor passed him bread. "You can be important without being the answer. Try it. It is restful."

"I dislike growth experiences."

"Everyone noticed."

A station monk-not-monk raised a cup. "Repair before worship."

Voices answered across species, accents, and incompatible theologies. Some said the words as ritual. Some as joke. Some as maintenance oath. None knew the full Aerit truth, and that ignorance did not make the practice false.

Lars lifted his cup last.

"Coherence?" Grim asked softly, testing the old greeting like a new utensil.

Around the table, several people answered without knowing why.

"Coherence."

The sound was messy.

It was alive.
