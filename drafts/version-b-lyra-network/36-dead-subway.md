# Chapter 36: Dead Subway

The Creator portal looked like a subway station built by a civilization that hated passengers.

It hung inside a hollowed asteroid: a ring of black composite, twelve silent pylons, and a central aperture filled with nothing dramatic. No glowing holy threshold. No music. No whispering void. Just dead engineering, dust, and radiation scars where previous explorers had made expensive mistakes.

"Disappointing," Lars said.

"Good," Mira replied. "Beautiful doors are how tourists die."

Havets held position outside the asteroid while Fastulv docked visibly with a licensed survey team. Fabron brought six soldiers and three portable barricades. Vigor sent food because she distrusted any mission plan that assumed people thought better hungry. Grim wore his android body and carried sensor packs like a proud metal mule.

The portal had been inactive for at least eighty thousand years. Its power interface was crude compared with Aerit fold control, but the underlying field couplings were not crude at all. That contradiction bothered Grim enough to make him stop joking for eight minutes.

"Creators built the ring," he said. "They did not build the anchor."

Mira placed ceramic dust across the floor. "Then let's see what the anchor thinks of weight."

They tested everything: thermal response, vacuum harmonics, neutrino scatter, local gravity shear, nanomaterial affinity, acoustic resonance through pylons. No prayers. No guesses without instruments. Wonder had to wait its turn behind calibration.

When Lars opened a narrow link to Havets, the dead portal woke badly.

The aperture did not bloom. It stuttered. Space inside it folded into a shallow corridor filled with afterimages: not destinations, but failed attempts at destinations. A red desert under three moons. An ocean full of black towers. A station ring shattered and repaired with vines. Then the corridor collapsed into a static field that tasted, on every sensor, like old lightning.

Fabron said, "That was transport?"

"That was transport trying to remember communication," Grim said.

Something moved in the dust.

Not a body. A pattern. Ceramic particles arranged themselves into three interlocking circles and a line that repaired itself, then cracked again, then repaired again.

Mira crouched, scanner ready. "Local field manipulation. Nanoparticle alignment. No mass addition."

"Message?" Lars asked.

The dust shifted.

DO NOT USE DOORS AS GODS.

Fabron looked at Lars.

Lars raised both hands. "I feel unfairly targeted."

The second line appeared more slowly.

LYRA LEFT BAD LOCKS ON PURPOSE.

Valdrun, remote through Havets, inhaled like old machinery under strain. "Bad locks?"

The dust lost coherence before it could answer.

They sealed the site and copied every sensor frame to three independent archives, including Kesh's kin-circle because the archive had found the map and trust required return flow.

On the way back to Fastulv, Lars felt the portal behind him like a dead station on a line still carrying current.

The Creators had built subways on top of a language.

No wonder so many doors led nowhere safe.
