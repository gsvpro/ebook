# Chapter 43: No Gods, Just Old Engineers

The first person to call the Nomads divine was an Aphex physicist with tenure, fear, and insufficient sleep.

He did it during a closed briefing, which saved his career and nearly cost him his dignity. Lars stood in front of a wall of data: thermal deficits, portal residuals, Lyra's projections, nanomaterial residue, KBC quieting models. Around the table sat Aphex scientists, NUKEA engineers, archive advocates, Fabron's Mother Aveline, Homa Sen, Vigor, Mira, Grim, and three species whose translators disliked metaphor.

"If consciousness can exist in a field layer beyond ordinary spacetime," the physicist said, "then we are discussing gods."

"No," Lars said.

Too fast. Too sharp.

The room waited.

He tried again.

"We are discussing evidence that intelligence may operate through substrates we do not yet understand. Old does not mean divine. Powerful does not mean worthy. Strange does not mean exempt from error. The Creators were older than us and apparently built a subway through someone else's nervous system."

Homa Sen raised a hand. "Excellent phrasing. Horrible image. Continue."

Lars looked at the physicist. "If you call them gods, you stop asking engineering questions. Worse, you start asking obedience questions. Predators love obedience questions."

Mother Aveline nodded. "Faith without maintenance becomes theater."

Mira added, "And theater under pressure becomes crowd crush."

The briefing shifted after that. Not away from awe. Away from surrender.

They listed what they knew: artifacts hosted nanotech capable of local biological integration. Creator portals exploited anchor fields without understanding the deeper communication layer. Lyra's agents could manifest temporarily through seeded substrates, leaving measurable residue. Hidden Aerits existed as persons with locked memories and current lives. Återviens-like practices had spread through human and multi-species cultures because they worked.

They listed what they did not know, which took longer and improved everyone's mood except Arsenault's.

Black Hole B-7 remained an immediate threat because it wanted blood and shortcuts. Syren remained a memetic threat because it wanted meaning with bodies attached. Central governments remained a structural threat because fear made them efficient in the worst direction. Aphex remained Aphex.

"I heard that pause," Arsenault said from projection.

"Good," Lars said.

The meeting's final product was not a doctrine. It was a protocol for language. No divine terms in technical channels. No public claims about hidden persons. No central registry of suspected Aerits. No forced testing. No artifact activation without local consent, environmental model, and independent refusal authority.

Fabron smiled faintly at refusal authority.

"Your order is leaking into policy," Lars said.

"Repair before worship, Monsieur. Even policy needs tools."

Afterward, Lars found the Aphex physicist alone by the window.

"You were not wrong to feel awe," Lars said.

"I was wrong to make it lazy."

"That is a better sentence."

Outside, ships moved like sparks across the dark.

No gods had answered.

The old engineers, if they were listening, kept their boundaries.
