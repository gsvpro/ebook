# Chapter 23: The Frenchman's Ledger

Gabriel Arsenault did not summon Lars to a boardroom. He summoned him to an accounting vault.

The vault orbited Paris in a shell company stack so dense that even Grim admired the administrative violence of it. Its walls contained no art, no flags, and no heroic Aphex slogans. Only ledgers. Supply ledgers, casualty ledgers, fuel ledgers, actuarial collapse models, and one private wall where Arsenault tracked conflicts by the tonnage of replacement parts they consumed.

"You expected champagne," Arsenault said.

"I expected lies with better lighting."

"This is better. Numbers are slower liars."

Lars walked past a projection of L-Prime 41's repair schedule. Mira's hardened lab appeared as a line item with three aliases and one black-budget source named AGRICULTURAL REDUNDANCY PILOT.

"Cute," Lars said.

"You protected an employee. I protected the invoice."

Arsenault looked smaller without public performance. Not kinder. Smaller in the way a man became when he stood next to his own fear and stopped pretending it was strategy.

He opened the central ledger. It was not profit.

It was survivability.

Colonies below reactor redundancy. Stations with single-vendor food catalysts. Habitats whose defense grids depended on one corporate firmware tree. Mining families priced out of pressure suit replacement. Aphex contracts that made all of it worse and, sometimes, prevented it from becoming fatal.

"You want monopoly," Lars said.

"I want leverage. Monopoly is one crude form."

"Crude forms become tumors."

Arsenault smiled without pleasure. "Yes. That is why you are useful. You hate what I am tempted to become."

Grim stood near a ledger pillar, pretending not to ingest six centuries of procurement pathology. "Your failure model assumes central authority remains responsive under stress. It will not."

"I know, machine."

"Then why build it?"

Arsenault touched a line of red colonies. "Because distributed virtue does not deliver reactor shielding by Thursday."

Lars hated that because it was partially true. Återviens did not mean everyone held hands while logistics solved itself. Redundancy required inventory. Autonomy required spares. Consent required time, shelter, and people willing to carry crates without applause.

"Black Hole knows about Mira," Lars said. "Someone sold or leaked the pattern."

"Yes."

No denial. That was almost rude.

Arsenault opened another file: internal Aphex dissenters, sealed complaints, procurement anomalies, employees who had diverted parts to colonies that could not pay. Some had been disciplined. Some had been quietly protected. Several used symbols Lars now recognized: circles, cracks, hands, table marks, reactor prayers.

"What is this?"

"My problem," Arsenault said. "And perhaps yours. There is a culture inside my company that refuses certain profitable efficiencies. I thought it was union romanticism, religious residue, or engineers being engineers. After L-Prime, I am less certain."

Fabron, standing stiffly behind Lars, said nothing.

Arsenault noticed him not saying it.

"Major, your grandmother's people are everywhere. Did you know?"

Fabron's face closed. "No."

"Neither did they. That is what makes them interesting."

Lars looked at the ledger again and saw not a conspiracy but capillaries. Farms that donated seed stock. Maintenance crews that preserved manual overrides. Station guilds that refused single-point command. Aphex officers who followed orders until orders broke repair.

"You want me to centralize them," Lars said.

Arsenault laughed once. "I am not suicidal. I want you to keep them from deciding I am the enemy before Black Hole finishes becoming one."

"And afterward?"

"Afterward, you keep me honest, or you kill me, or you become worse than me. I have not priced all scenarios."

Lars should have enjoyed that.

Instead he saw Lyra's long shadow: not an army, not a church, not seven guardians waiting for a king. A distributed apparatus using culture as infrastructure because culture crossed borders where ships could not.

The Frenchman's ledger was ugly.

It was also evidence.
