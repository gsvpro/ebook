# Chapter 28: Fabron Sees Too Much

Fabron confronted Lars in a laundry module because secure rooms had become too obvious.

The module served Aphex transit personnel on Vigor's outer dock. It smelled of detergent, overheated bearings, and soldiers pretending uniforms cleaned themselves. Fabron locked the door with a mechanical wedge rather than the panel.

Lars approved despite himself.

"You are older than your file," Fabron said.

"Rude. Moisturizer is expensive."

"Dr. Sovan is older than her file. Valdrun is impossible. Your robot is not a robot. Your hidden ship produces gravitational bookkeeping errors. My order knows phrases it should not know. And someone sent me instructions about the black wolf before Fastulv docked."

"That is a lot for laundry."

Fabron stepped closer. "Do not insult me with jokes now."

Lars almost did anyway. Then he saw the fatigue under the major's precision. Fabron's world had not broken loudly. It had misaligned one verified fact at a time until duty no longer fit inside the old drawers.

"I am not going to tell you everything," Lars said.

"Because I lack clearance?"

"Because knowledge changes blast radius. Because some truths make people targets. Because your order survived by not knowing enough to betray."

Fabron's jaw tightened. "You know my order?"

"I know its shape. Repair before worship. Refuse the throne. Keep the hinge small. Those are not random prayers. They are resilience practices, translated into culture after people forgot the engineering notes."

"Engineering notes from whom?"

The dryers hummed. Somewhere in the wall, an old bearing clicked every seventh rotation. Lars found comfort in the flaw.

"From people like me, maybe. From people like Lyra. From humans who learned beside us. It developed after Pluto, over decades, not all at once. Survival practice became doctrine because doctrine outlives witnesses."

Fabron looked as if he had been struck and promoted in the same second.

"Am I one of you?"

"No."

The answer hurt him. Lars saw that too.

"Good," Fabron said stiffly.

"It is good. You are proof the idea moved without blood, nanobots, or secret kings. You are human. Annoyingly, usefully human."

"Your praise requires fumigation."

"Agreed."

Fabron turned away, hands clasped behind him. "My grandmother taught that the Clear Hand must never become the whole body. I thought it meant humility before God."

"Maybe it still does for you."

"Do not patronize faith you do not share."

"I am not. I am saying a working practice can carry meanings its designers did not predict. No gods required. No contempt either."

Fabron studied him for a long moment.

"What do you need from me?"

"Not obedience."

"Obviously. I have standards."

"Connections. People who can shelter strangers without asking the wrong questions. Officers who can refuse illegal biological transfers. Priests who can keep a frightened family from turning a hidden person into a saint. Maintenance crews who will believe manual overrides matter."

Fabron nodded slowly. "And if my order asks what authority you have?"

Lars thought of saying none. It would be dramatic and false. Authority existed. The danger was letting it congeal.

"Tell them I have evidence, resources, and bad habits. Tell them not to trust me more than necessary."

For the first time that day, Fabron smiled.

"That, Monsieur Odin, may be the most credible thing you have ever said."
