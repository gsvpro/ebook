# Chapter 32: The Syren Invitation

The Syren Collective sent flowers.

They arrived at L-Prime 41 in vacuum glass, genetically tuned to bloom under brown-dwarf infrared. Their petals were black with silver veins. Their pollen contained no toxin, no nanotech, no tracker Grim could find after insulting the bouquet for twenty minutes.

The message was worse.

We honor the sleepers. We honor the blood that remembers. Come speak before those who can worship correctly.

Mira forwarded it to Lars with one note: Absolutely not.

The invitation named a neutral symposium on Conscious Survival After Catastrophe, hosted in a rotating cathedral-habitat called The Throat. Syren cells, fringe academics, grief cults, veterans, artists, and three governments too embarrassed to admit interest would attend. The Collective wanted Lars there. Or Mira. Or any hidden Aerit they could turn into proof that their extinction theology had found a body.

"Trap," Fabron said.

"Obviously," Lars said.

"You sound tempted."

"I dislike obvious traps. They imply I need help making bad decisions."

They did not send Lars.

They sent evidence, doubt, and a retired reactor inspector named Homa Sen who had spent forty years making charismatic men look foolish in public hearings.

Homa belonged to one of Fabron's sibling traditions, though she called hers the Guild of Last Checks and denied all religious content while wearing a pendant shaped like a cracked circle. She knew nothing about Aerits beyond the briefing she consented to receive. She knew everything about pressure vessels, martyrdom dynamics, and how crowds behaved when someone promised meaning in exchange for obedience.

The Throat's main chamber looked like a church built by acousticians with unresolved grief. Syren speakers sang about blood as continuity, hidden ancients as saints, Black Hole violence as profanation rather than crime. They offered the galaxy a simple story: the sleepers existed to be revealed, worshiped, and followed into transformation.

Homa took the stage with a reactor casing under one arm.

"This," she said, "is a cracked pressure liner from a school habitat. It nearly killed eight hundred children because a committee loved symbolic maintenance more than maintenance."

The audience did not know what to do.

Homa continued for forty-seven minutes. She did not deny wonder. She denied ownership. She showed how cults turned persons into functions, how centralized revelation created stampedes, how consent failed when crowds made refusal feel like betrayal. She quoted no Aerit secrets. She used public disasters, engineering reports, and one poem about a pump.

By the end, half the symposium was bored, a quarter was furious, and the useful quarter had learned vocabulary for refusing spectacle.

The Syren moderator tried to recover. "Inspector Sen, do you deny that hidden survivors may carry sacred continuity?"

Homa looked at him over the cracked casing.

"I deny your right to call a person sacred when you mean available."

That line spread faster than the invitation had.

Black Hole cells hated it. Syren channels argued with it for days and amplified it by accident. Station guilds printed it on maintenance stickers. Someone sent Mira a version stamped on a coffee mug.

She kept the mug.

Lars watched the fallout from Fastulv and felt the shape of Lyra's apparatus again: not a secret speech from a hidden queen, but a human inspector with a cracked liner making worship less useful to predators.

"We did not win," Grim said.

"No," Lars said. "But the trap caught less."
