# Chapter 34: The Wrong Savior

Minister Pell became famous by stealing the outline of the truth and filling it with himself.

He was handsome in the manufactured way of people whose lighting teams understood politics. He led a movement called Humanity Ascendant, which had previously worried about tariffs, alien labor quotas, and the moral dangers of imported cheese. After the Syren symposium, Pell discovered hidden ancients, blood continuity, and the urgent need for a central human authority to manage contact with whatever miracle was clearly waiting to endorse him.

He never said Aerit.

He said progenitors. He said sleepers. He said sacred engineers. He said humanity must not let corporations, aliens, or unaccountable drunks control the inheritance of the stars.

The last part came with Lars's picture.

"I look good," Lars said.

Mira, on the wall, said, "You look like a cautionary substance."

Pell's campaign used real leaks: Black Hole attacks, Syren rhetoric, Aphex anomalies, old images stripped of context. If Lars corrected him directly, he would confirm the premise. If he stayed silent, the lie would grow.

"We need another Homa," Vigor said.

"We need a thousand," Fabron said.

That became the answer.

Station guilds challenged Pell on maintenance law. Alien trade circles asked why his sacred inheritance excluded the species already keeping human habitats alive. Reactor orders demanded he explain his central authority's emergency coolant doctrine. Farmers flooded his channels with questions about seed sovereignty. Aphex dissenters leaked contracts showing Pell's donors profited from centralized licensing. NUKEA retirees published collapse models with titles like THRONE FAILURE UNDER NORMAL LOAD.

No one argued the secret.

They argued the shape of the lie.

Pell wanted a savior because saviors simplified command. The network answered with repair tickets, food ledgers, kin law, and jokes. Especially jokes. Grim wrote none of them, he claimed, despite one viral cartoon depicting Pell as a central control panel on fire while grandmothers calmly installed manual valves.

Black Hole tried to use the noise. A false whistleblower offered Pell "proof" of Aerit blood harvesting by Aphex. The file contained enough truth to ruin Mira's privacy and enough poison to start riots at three mixed-species stations.

Lars nearly intervened personally.

Fabron stopped him with one sentence. "You would make his story taller."

So the network handled it smaller.

A clerk in Arsenault's ledger office verified the file chain. An Abantu data advocate explained the forged consent markers. Homa Sen went on a children's science program and demonstrated metadata poisoning with jam jars. Mira anonymously contributed the fatigue signature proving the evidence package had been grown in a Black Hole nursery shell.

The story broke sideways. Pell accused enemies of conspiracy. His base loved it. Everyone else grew tired.

That was not victory. It was inoculation.

The wrong savior remained on screens, but fewer people mistook volume for coherence.

Late that night, Lars watched Pell give another speech muted.

"I could destroy him," he said.

Vigor sat beside him, peeling an orange. "Yes."

"You are supposed to tell me not to."

"No. You know not to. I am here in case knowing makes you lonely."

He looked at the network model. Thousands of local refusals pulsed against Pell's central narrative.

Lars turned off the screen.

For once, the galaxy did not need him to be louder.
