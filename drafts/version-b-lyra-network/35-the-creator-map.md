# Chapter 35: The Creator Map

The map was carved into a dead probe that should not have known where it was.

It drifted beyond Tau Ceti's archive perimeter, colder than background models allowed and old enough to make every dating method nervous. The archive's arthropod mathematician, whose chosen human transcription was Kesh, had found it by noticing a statistical bruise in dust impacts. Kesh had declined all identity discussion and consented to share the object because, in their words, maps are social animals.

Havets retrieved the probe without crossing the archive's kin-law boundary. That mattered to Kesh. It mattered to Lars because it mattered to Kesh.

Inside the probe was a lattice of dense ceramic memory. Grim read it slowly, complaining with reverence. The geometry resolved into portal locations: hundreds of ancient transport nodes, many dead, some sleeping, all aligned with outer-system artifacts in a pattern too precise to be coincidence and too awkward to be design origin.

"Creators," Valdrun said.

The word made the room feel smaller.

The Creators had built the portal network, or so Aerit fragments suggested. Six hundred thousand years gone. Builders of doors humanity had barely begun to understand. Old enough that arrogance should have decayed out of any sentence about them.

Mira, visiting Havets in person for the first time and pretending not to be impressed, pointed at the alignment residuals. "They copied something."

Grim brightened. "Yes. The portals are not primary. They are transport approximations built on top of older anchor behavior."

"Like using a communication tower as a bridge support," Fabron said.

Everyone looked at him.

"What? I understand analogies."

The map did not point to conquest. It pointed to misunderstanding. Creator nodes clustered near artifacts that resembled Pluto's infection source, but the portal geometries lagged the artifact network by centuries or millennia. They had found a field system, interpreted it as infrastructure, and built doors where they heard noise.

Lars felt Lyra in the absence. Young Lyra on Pluto had touched one artifact and survived by improvising. Later Lyra had spent centuries asking what else the artifact was doing while everyone else asked how to use it.

A hidden layer in the map opened only when Grim fed it Mira's fatigue-derived correction model. The display shifted from points to flows.

Not routes.

Correlations.

Kesh sent a delayed comment: Transport species draw lines. Communication species draw tolerances.

"That is irritatingly good," Lars said.

Mira smiled faintly. "Mathematicians are often useful when not being worshiped."

The oldest flow line ran toward the KBC Void.

Not into it exactly. Along its quiet edge, where observation noise dropped in ways standard cosmology dismissed as survey poverty.

Fabron crossed his arms. "So the empty place is not empty."

"Evidence first," Mira said.

"Evidence says," Grim replied, "the emptiness has architecture."

Lars looked at the map and felt wonder arrive with dread. Black Hole B-7 was a predator. Syren was a fever. Aphex was a temptation. All dangerous.

But the galaxy had older questions under its floor.
